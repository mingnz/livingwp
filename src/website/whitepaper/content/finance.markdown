# AI Adoption in Finance in Aotearoa New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper

**Introduction:** The New Zealand financial sector – including banks, insurers, advisors and fintech – is increasingly exploring artificial intelligence (AI) to improve services and efficiency. In 2024–25, regulators and industry reports highlight that NZ firms are experimenting with AI while aiming to manage new risks. For example, a Financial Markets Authority (FMA) study in Sept 2024 surveyed banks, fund managers, insurers and advisors on AI use ([www.fma.govt.nz](https://www.fma.govt.nz/news/all-releases/media-releases/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services/#:~:text=The%20Financial%20Markets%20Authority%20,New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20financial%20services%20sector)). Separately, the Reserve Bank of New Zealand (RBNZ) in its 2025 Financial Stability Report noted that AI is “starting to have a significant impact right across the financial system,” offering productivity and innovation gains ([www.rbnz.govt.nz](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2025/may/ai-pre-release/rise-of-the-machines#:~:text=We%E2%80%99ve%20been%20seeing%20some%20real,financial%20institutions%20better%20manage%20threats)). This living whitepaper compiles recent news, research findings, and case examples to give a snapshot of AI adoption in NZ finance.

## Current News  
- **FMA publishes AI research (Sept 2024):** New Zealand’s Financial Markets Authority released an “Understanding AI in Financial Services” report ([www.fma.govt.nz](https://www.fma.govt.nz/news/all-releases/media-releases/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services/#:~:text=The%20Financial%20Markets%20Authority%20,New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20financial%20services%20sector)). It drew on responses from 13 financial firms and found that AI is already used to enhance customer outcomes, operational efficiency and fraud detection ([anziif.com](https://anziif.com/professional-development/whitepapers/2024/09/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services#:~:text=Drawing%20on%20responses%20from%2013,within%20the%20next%2012%20months)). Firms see benefits but are “taking a cautious approach,” with emerging concerns around data privacy, algorithmic bias and cybersecurity ([anziif.com](https://anziif.com/professional-development/whitepapers/2024/09/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services#:~:text=Drawing%20on%20responses%20from%2013,within%20the%20next%2012%20months)) ([anziif.com](https://anziif.com/professional-development/whitepapers/2024/09/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services#:~:text=The%20report%20also%20highlights%20the,of%20robust%20governance%20and%20risk)). The FMA emphasised that robust governance and risk management are needed as AI adoption grows ([anziif.com](https://anziif.com/professional-development/whitepapers/2024/09/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services#:~:text=The%20report%20also%20highlights%20the,of%20robust%20governance%20and%20risk)). 
- **RBNZ flags AI in Financial Stability Report (May 2025):** The Reserve Bank’s latest report (“Rise of the machines”) explicitly discusses AI. It notes real benefits from AI – “enhanced productivity, increased innovation, [and] improved cyber resilience” – which can help financial firms manage threats ([www.rbnz.govt.nz](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2025/may/ai-pre-release/rise-of-the-machines#:~:text=We%E2%80%99ve%20been%20seeing%20some%20real,financial%20institutions%20better%20manage%20threats)). However, RBNZ warns that rapid AI adoption also brings risks: “AI-driven errors, data privacy concerns, market distortions, and cyber-attacks could amplify existing systemic risks” ([www.rbnz.govt.nz](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2025/may/ai-pre-release/rise-of-the-machines#:~:text=AI%20adoption%20is%20accelerating%20at,could%20amplify%20existing%20systemic%20risks)). As NZ’s bank regulator, RBNZ states that institutions “must be aware of and manage potential risks associated with AI” within existing oversight frameworks ([www.rbnz.govt.nz](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2025/may/ai-pre-release/rise-of-the-machines#:~:text=As%20the%20regulator%20of%20Banks,can%20be%20regulated%20through%20existing)).  
- **Open Banking push (2026 target):** A Commerce Commission report (Aug 2024) urged implementation of open banking by 2026 ([www.reuters.com](https://www.reuters.com/business/finance/nz-watchdog-finds-personal-bank-sector-lacks-competition-2024-08-19/#:~:text=competition,Financial%20Policy%20Remit%20to%20the)). While not AI per se, open banking will share customer data securely with third parties and is expected to spur fintech innovation (including AI-driven services) by enabling more competition and new financial apps.  
- **Public/Business sentiment:** NZ business leaders are increasingly focused on AI governance. A national survey in 2023 found **82%** of business respondents believed government should legislate AI use ([www.newshub.co.nz](https://www.newshub.co.nz/home/technology/2023/08/eighty-two-percent-of-nz-businesses-feel-government-should-legislate-over-ai-study.html#:~:text=As%20more%20and%20more%20businesses,for%20the%20use%20of%20AI)), reflecting concerns about unchecked AI impacts. This suggests strong stakeholder interest in ensuring AI is used responsibly in NZ finance.

## Research Overview  
- **FMA/ANZIIF findings:** The FMA’s 2024 report (summarised by ANZIIF) found AI is already deployed in NZ finance for customer support, fraud detection, and back-office automation ([anziif.com](https://anziif.com/professional-development/whitepapers/2024/09/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services#:~:text=Drawing%20on%20responses%20from%2013,within%20the%20next%2012%20months)). Most firms expect growing AI use in the next year. At the same time, the sector remains cautious: it is conscious of data-quality requirements and risks of biases or privacy breaches ([anziif.com](https://anziif.com/professional-development/whitepapers/2024/09/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services#:~:text=The%20report%20also%20highlights%20the,of%20robust%20governance%20and%20risk)). The FMA highlights the need for firms to build “robust governance structures” around AI ([anziif.com](https://anziif.com/professional-development/whitepapers/2024/09/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services#:~:text=Drawing%20on%20responses%20from%2013,within%20the%20next%2012%20months)) ([anziif.com](https://anziif.com/professional-development/whitepapers/2024/09/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services#:~:text=The%20report%20also%20highlights%20the,of%20robust%20governance%20and%20risk)).  
- **RBNZ analysis:** The Reserve Bank points out that AI tools (including machine-learning and generative models) are becoming widely used in banking and insurance. RBNZ research notes advances in data modeling, fraud detection, and cybersecurity driven by AI add value to NZ finance ([completeaitraining.com](https://completeaitraining.com/news/ai-growth-in-new-zealands-financial-sector-raises-stability/#:~:text=The%20report%20points%20to%20advances,providers%20might%20increase%20systemic%20risks)). Yet it also warns that complex AI systems and reliance on external AI providers could introduce new systemic vulnerabilities ([completeaitraining.com](https://completeaitraining.com/news/ai-growth-in-new-zealands-financial-sector-raises-stability/#:~:text=The%20report%20points%20to%20advances,providers%20might%20increase%20systemic%20risks)). Overall, RBNZ concludes that while AI is a productivity opportunity, its full implications for stability are uncertain and warrant proactive risk management ([www.rbnz.govt.nz](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2025/may/ai-pre-release/rise-of-the-machines#:~:text=AI%20adoption%20is%20accelerating%20at,could%20amplify%20existing%20systemic%20risks)) ([www.rbnz.govt.nz](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2025/may/ai-pre-release/rise-of-the-machines#:~:text=As%20the%20regulator%20of%20Banks,can%20be%20regulated%20through%20existing)).  
- **International perspective:** Globally, financial regulators are studying AI’s impacts. The ECB has said AI in finance can improve customer service and cyber-defense yet may produce “herding behavior” or over-reliance on a few tech providers ([www.reuters.com](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ais-use-finance-may-need-new-rules-ecb-says-2024-05-15/#:~:text=The%20European%20Central%20Bank%20,a%20few%20providers%2C%20and%20advanced)). In London, the financial watchdog warns AI could inadvertently discriminate (e.g. making some consumers “uninsurable” if bias is unchecked) ([www.ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/9f9d3a54-d08b-4d9c-a000-d50460f818dc#:~:text=Nikhil%20Rathi%2C%20the%20head%20of,enabled)). These international concerns underscore why NZ authorities stress a balanced approach. 

## Case Studies  
- **ANZ Bank’s “Jamie” Virtual Assistant:** ANZ New Zealand launched “Jamie” in 2018 – an AI-powered digital assistant built with Auckland-based Soul Machines. Jamie uses artificial conversational intelligence to answer common customer queries on ANZ’s website ([www.anz.com.au](https://www.anz.com.au/bluenotes/2018/07/her/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CKia%20ora%2C%20I%E2%80%99m%20Jamie,question%20about%20banking%20for%20me%3F%E2%80%9D)). This early use of AI (digital humans) demonstrated how Kiwi banks can adopt chatbots for customer service.  
- **Xero’s GenAI for Small Business:** Wellington-based fintech Xero announced in Feb 2024 a generative-AI initiative (“Just Ask Xero”). This tool lets small businesses and accountants ask business-related questions in natural language. It automates tasks like invoicing or quoting and provides insights to help run the business ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/BU202402/S00303.htm#:~:text=small%20businesses,to%20run%20their%20business%20better)). Xero’s AI platform (JAX) integrates across web, mobile, and even messaging apps, showcasing how NZ financial software providers use AI to boost productivity ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/BU202402/S00303.htm#:~:text=small%20businesses,to%20run%20their%20business%20better)).  
- *(Additional pilots & deployments: insurers and finance firms in NZ are quietly experimenting with AI in underwriting, compliance and KYC. For example, NZ’s largest insurer IAG (which owns AMI and State) has invested in advanced analytics; local fintech startups are exploring AI analytics, though public case details are limited.)*

## Trends and Predictions  
- **High and growing adoption:** The AI Forum’s latest survey shows AI use in NZ firms is rapidly expanding. As of early 2025, **82%** of surveyed organizations reported using AI (a 15% increase in six months) ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/reports/ai-adoption-surges-across-new-zealand-six-months-of-growth/#:~:text=%2A%20Adoption%20up%2015,support%20AI%20training%20for%20staff)). Nearly all (93%) said AI made workers more efficient, and 71% reported cost savings ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/reports/ai-adoption-surges-across-new-zealand-six-months-of-growth/#:~:text=%2A%20Adoption%20up%2015,support%20AI%20training%20for%20staff)). Only 7% saw AI replacing staff, indicating displacement is still low ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/reports/ai-adoption-surges-across-new-zealand-six-months-of-growth/#:~:text=%2A%20Adoption%20up%2015,support%20AI%20training%20for%20staff)). This suggests NZ finance companies (like others) are optimistic about productivity and savings from AI.  
- **Main applications:** Both globally and in NZ, finance organizations focus AI on customer support (chatbots, robo-advisors), data analytics (underwriting, pricing models), and cyber/fraud defense ([www.ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/9ffe888e-2f78-4f59-9536-2e31f5bde8b7#:~:text=The%20financial%20services%20sector%20is,from%20Harvey%20Nash%20and%20Michael)). For instance, international banks are testing LLMs for internal research summaries, and insurers use ML for claims screening. NZ banks and KiwiSaver providers are likely following suit, applying AI for recommending products or detecting anomalies. Financial cyber-security is another hot spot (RBNZ noted improved “cyber resilience” from AI ([www.rbnz.govt.nz](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2025/may/ai-pre-release/rise-of-the-machines#:~:text=We%E2%80%99ve%20been%20seeing%20some%20real,financial%20institutions%20better%20manage%20threats))).  
- **Workforce shifts:** Experts predict AI will create new specialist roles (AI trainers, ethicists, data scientists) even as it automates routine tasks ([www.ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/9ffe888e-2f78-4f59-9536-2e31f5bde8b7#:~:text=Conway%20from%20IBM%20Consulting%2C%20note,roles%20by%20creating%20new%20jobs)). NZ businesses are planning accordingly: 81% of NZ firms say they will invest in AI training for staff ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/reports/ai-adoption-surges-across-new-zealand-six-months-of-growth/#:~:text=,support%20AI%20training%20for%20staff)). The high training rate suggests NZ finance professionals will need upskilling (e.g. in data literacy and AI governance).  
- **Regulatory outlook:** NZ regulators emphasize fostering innovation while managing risks. The FMA states it is “technology-neutral and pro-innovation,” wanting NZers to access global AI advances, and encouraging firms to use AI to improve customer experiences ([www.fma.govt.nz](https://www.fma.govt.nz/news/all-releases/media-releases/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20FMA%20is%20technology,We%20are%20pleased%20that%20our)). At the same time, both FMA and RBNZ insist on “responsible” AI. They highlight that existing laws and standards must address AI risks. For example, RBNZ says regulated banks must have strong risk and governance frameworks for AI models ([www.rbnz.govt.nz](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2025/may/ai-pre-release/rise-of-the-machines#:~:text=As%20the%20regulator%20of%20Banks,can%20be%20regulated%20through%20existing)), and FMA underscores data quality and documentation as critical to ethical AI use. This guidance mirrors global moves (e.g. ECB and FCA urging monitoring and fairness) and suggests NZ will continue updating guidance as AI evolves.  
- **Risks and ethical concerns:** NZ financial firms are wary of common AI pitfalls. Data privacy, algorithmic bias and cyber-security top their concern lists (as highlighted by the FMA study ([anziif.com](https://anziif.com/professional-development/whitepapers/2024/09/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services#:~:text=The%20report%20also%20highlights%20the,of%20robust%20governance%20and%20risk))). Internationally, regulators echo these fears: the UK’s FCA warns unchecked AI could unfairly exclude individuals ([www.ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/9f9d3a54-d08b-4d9c-a000-d50460f818dc#:~:text=Nikhil%20Rathi%2C%20the%20head%20of,enabled)). NZ banks and insurers will likely increase due diligence on AI vendors and outputs to avoid such outcomes.  
- **Future outlook:** With open banking coming and digital tools growing, NZ finance is poised for more AI integration by the mid-2020s. Businesses show appetite for oversight – one survey found 82% back government AI legislation ([www.newshub.co.nz](https://www.newshub.co.nz/home/technology/2023/08/eighty-two-percent-of-nz-businesses-feel-government-should-legislate-over-ai-study.html#:~:text=As%20more%20and%20more%20businesses,for%20the%20use%20of%20AI)) – so we may see new NZ guidelines in coming years. AI is also expected to leverage improving data infrastructure (e.g. real-time payments data, potential digital ID) in NZ. Overall, the trend is toward deeper AI use in risk analysis, personalized services, and back-office automation, balanced by steady enhancement of rules and skills.

**Conclusion:** AI adoption in Aotearoa New Zealand’s finance sector is accelerating. Recent reports confirm firms are already using AI for customer service, fraud prevention and data analytics ([anziif.com](https://anziif.com/professional-development/whitepapers/2024/09/understanding-artificial-intelligence-in-financial-services#:~:text=Drawing%20on%20responses%20from%2013,within%20the%20next%2012%20months)) ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/reports/ai-adoption-surges-across-new-zealand-six-months-of-growth/#:~:text=%2A%20Adoption%20up%2015,support%20AI%20training%20for%20staff)). Regulators, for their part, are supportive of this innovation but stress cautious rollout. The RBNZ sums up the dual perspective: AI offers “enhanced productivity” and resilience ([www.rbnz.govt.nz](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2025/may/ai-pre-release/rise-of-the-machines#:~:text=We%E2%80%99ve%20been%20seeing%20some%20real,financial%20institutions%20better%20manage%20threats)), but institutions “must be aware of and manage potential risks” ([www.rbnz.govt.nz](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2025/may/ai-pre-release/rise-of-the-machines#:~:text=As%20the%20regulator%20of%20Banks,can%20be%20regulated%20through%20existing)). The coming years will bring a dynamic balance: NZ finance stakeholders can gain efficiency and insight from AI while building the governance and talent needed to ensure stability and trust. This living whitepaper will be updated as new developments emerge, helping decision-makers stay informed on AI’s evolving role in New Zealand finance.
