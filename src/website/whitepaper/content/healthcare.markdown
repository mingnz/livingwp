# AI Adoption in Healthcare in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper

## Introduction  
- New Zealand’s health system is under strain (staff shortages, long waitlists) – for example, over 65,000 patients waited 4+ months for a first specialist appointment ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=been%20swift%2C%20with%20doctors%20reporting,Gynaecology%2C%20Neurology%2C%20Orthopaedics%20and%20Urology)).  AI is seen as a way to augment scarce clinicians (e.g. for faster screening or admin tasks).  
- The government’s **precision health** strategy explicitly calls for data-driven, individualised care using genomics and AI, but insists all tools be “accessible, safe, effective, equitable, [and] accountable” ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/699742/Precision-health-AI-and-genomics---Ministry-of-Health-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,for%20diagnosis%2C%20treatment%20planning%2C%20drug)).  Early NZ pilots (like retinal screening AI) reflect this vision.  
- Adoption so far is **cautious**: Te Whatu Ora and experts warn that unvalidated generative AI (e.g. ChatGPT) must not be used on patient data, emphasizing that any clinical AI must have robust “guardrails” and human oversight ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=he%20explains,that%20could%20have%20clinical%20impacts)) ([brightstar.co.nz](https://brightstar.co.nz/ai-powered-diagnosis-a-game-changer-for-nz-health-but-who-bears-the-liability/#:~:text=New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20ACC%20Approach%20and,scenarios%20discussed%20in%20the%20article)).  NZ’s regulators are working on guidance (e.g. Medical Council guidelines due end-2025) to ensure trustworthy AI use ([www.nzdoctor.co.nz](https://www.nzdoctor.co.nz/article/news/medical-council-working-new-guidelines-use-ai#:~:text=The%20Medical%20Council%20is%20expected,Image%3A%20Suriphon%20Singha%20on%20iStock)) ([brightstar.co.nz](https://brightstar.co.nz/ai-powered-diagnosis-a-game-changer-for-nz-health-but-who-bears-the-liability/#:~:text=New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20ACC%20Approach%20and,scenarios%20discussed%20in%20the%20article)).  

## Recent Developments  
- **Health NZ Radiology AI Lab (May 2025):** Te Whatu Ora’s new AI Lab partnered with radiologists on pilot projects for high-volume imaging (chest X-rays, fracture and head CT scans).  These AI tools are used as a “co-pilot” – flagging urgent findings and reducing error – not replacing clinicians ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=to%20improve%20patient%20safety%2C%20streamline,explored%20to%20improve%20patient%20safety)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=MacDonald%20says%20studies%20show%20AI,enhance%20patient%20flow%2C%E2%80%9D%20she%20says)).  For example, Canterbury radiologists are testing AI to adjust worklist flags so suspicious images (e.g. potential cancer) are reported first ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=Sharyn%20MacDonald%2C%20chief%20of%20radiology,They%20can)).  NZ already uses some CAD tools in CT to detect abnormalities and even AI-driven MRI reconstruction to shorten scan time ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=Barnard%20says%20computer%20aided%20detection,the%20waiting%20time%2C%E2%80%9D%20he%20says)).  

- **National Radiology AI Orchestrator (Oct 2025):** Health NZ issued an RFI to build a **Radiology AI Orchestrator and App Marketplace** ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/712343/Health-NZ-seeks-integrated-AI-platform-to-transform-radiology-services.htm#:~:text=Key%20functions%20include%20rules,through%20intelligent%20triage%20and%20prioritisation)).  This seeks an integrated platform to route images through the right AI model, triage cases intelligently, and return results into PACS/worklists uniformly.  The aim is to scale AI radiology tools (stroke detection, X-ray triage, etc.) fairly across regions and speed up reporting by automatically prioritizing urgent studies ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/712343/Health-NZ-seeks-integrated-AI-platform-to-transform-radiology-services.htm#:~:text=Key%20functions%20include%20rules,through%20intelligent%20triage%20and%20prioritisation)). Responses are due Oct 2025, signaling a move toward system-wide AI infrastructure.  

- **Government AI Strategy (Jul 2025):** The national **AI Strategy – “Investing with confidence”** was launched, explicitly highlighting healthcare (e.g. diagnostics) as a key sector for AI innovation ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/705831/#:~:text=adoption%20and%20remove%20legislative%20barriers,foreword%20that%20it%20complements%20work)).  Science Minister Shane Reti pointed out examples like AI-powered diagnostics, and urged NZ businesses to become “smart adopters” of AI ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/705831/#:~:text=adoption%20and%20remove%20legislative%20barriers,foreword%20that%20it%20complements%20work)).  Officials stressed New Zealand can lead in “responsible AI” – Scott Arrol (HiNZ) noted our health sector is “well positioned to reap the benefits” of AI adoption ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/705831/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CTogether%2C%20these%20initiatives%20aim%20to,our%20health%20system%20and%20digital)).  

- **AI in Health Research Funding (Feb 2025):** The Health Research Council (HRC) launched a dedicated **AI in Healthcare research RFP** ([www.hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/news-and-events/new-ai-healthcare-rfp#:~:text=A%20total%20funding%20pool%20of,all%20these%20amounts%20exclude%20GST)).  It makes $5 million available for investigator-led projects (6–24 months, $100k–$700k each) that align with national health priorities (2024–27) and focus on *safe, ethical* AI innovations to improve care ([www.hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/news-and-events/new-ai-healthcare-rfp#:~:text=A%20total%20funding%20pool%20of,all%20these%20amounts%20exclude%20GST)).  Grants start in Aug 2025; topics range from diagnostics to efficiency.  This builds on earlier initiatives (e.g. CSA’s AI in Health report 2023) and seeks to accelerate NZ-specific AI solutions.  

- **Productivity Report & Surveys (2025):** An AI Forum report (Apr 2025) found AI use prevalent in NZ industries, with ~80% of organizations using AI. About 65% of health and social welfare entities reported some AI use ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=Intelligence%20on%20New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20productivity%2C,care%2C%20it%20covers%20a%20whole)), almost all in **non-clinical** roles (admin, finance) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=identifies%20the%20top%20five%20use,the%20data%20is%20sensitive%20and)).  The Forum’s health lead noted ambient AI scribes (voice-to-text in consults) are one active area, but clinical diagnostics remain cautious due to data sensitivity ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=private%2C%20so%20we%20need%20to,piloted%20or%20explored%20for%20the)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=that%20trust%2C%E2%80%9D%20he%20says,how%20far%20we%20want%20to)).  A BNZ healthcare survey (Oct 2025) echoed this: two-thirds of private providers have used or considered AI, with AI scribes and admin automation leading adoption, while imaging/diagnostic AI is mostly still in pilot stages ([www.healthcareitnews.com](https://www.healthcareitnews.com/news/anz/nz-private-healthcare-increasingly-adopts-ai-scribes-and-more-briefs#:~:text=The%20survey%20found%20that%20two,imaging%20remain%20in%20pilot%20phases)).  The survey explicitly links AI uptake to workforce shortages (clinicians seeking efficiency) ([www.healthcareitnews.com](https://www.healthcareitnews.com/news/anz/nz-private-healthcare-increasingly-adopts-ai-scribes-and-more-briefs#:~:text=The%20survey%20found%20that%20two,imaging%20remain%20in%20pilot%20phases)).  Notably, the NZ government has formally endorsed two ambient scribe systems (Heidi Health, iMedX) for trial in the public health sector ([www.healthcareitnews.com](https://www.healthcareitnews.com/news/anz/nz-private-healthcare-increasingly-adopts-ai-scribes-and-more-briefs#:~:text=Two%20ambient%20AI%20scribe%20tools,trial%20across%20public%20health%20services)).  

- **AI Chatbot for Veterans (Apr 2025):** The No Duff Trust launched a mental-health chatbot for NZ Defence Force veterans ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=No%20Duff%20Charitable%20Trust%20created,support%2C%20mindfulness%20and%20breathing%20modules)) ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=The%20new%20chatbot%20was%20trained,years%20of%20conversations%20with%20veterans)).  Trained on nine years of peer-support transcripts, the AI provides 24/7 crisis support, mindfulness and coping modules, with clinicians monitoring for safety. ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=No%20Duff%20Charitable%20Trust%20created,support%2C%20mindfulness%20and%20breathing%20modules))  Still in alpha testing, this initiative illustrates AI augmenting scarce mental-health resources.  Users have welcomed additional access, reflecting research that well-designed chatbots (with human oversight) can be as effective as therapists in some cases ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=Meanwhile%2C%20a%20recent%20clinical%20trial,in%20treating%20mental%20health%20issues)).  

- **Other Innovations:** Hospitals continue quietly adopting narrow AI.  For instance, Te Whatu Ora Whanganui trialled Nuance Dragon Medical One (logic-driven speech recognition) in its ED – reporting much “faster and more accurate” note-taking and more physician time for patients ([www.nuance.com](https://www.nuance.com/en-nz/healthcare/success/te-whatu-ora-whanganui.html#:~:text=,greater%20accuracy%20and%20improved%20efficiency)).  In the ambulance sector, Hato Hone St John piloted a local LLM trained on its protocols to audit paramedic records, showing promise at batch-reviewing large volumes and giving quality-feedback rapidly ([www.healthcareitnews.com](https://www.healthcareitnews.com/news/anz/nz-private-healthcare-increasingly-adopts-ai-scribes-and-more-briefs#:~:text=Hato%20Hone%20St%20John%20tests,AI%20for%20clinical%20audits)).  These examples show global AI tools (ambient speech, NLP) being applied in NZ care under clinical control.  

## Research and Initiatives  
- **HRC AI Projects:** The HRC’s 2025–26 AI RFP led to funded projects (e.g. ~24m study on AI-enhanced digital pathology for GI cancer, ~$700k budget) ([www.hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/resources/research-repository/improving-gastrointestinal-cancer-outcomes-ai-enhanced-digital#:~:text=Year%3A)).  Such grants (often co-funded with industry or MBIE) aim to translate AI from lab prototypes to practice, in areas like diagnostics, risk prediction, and care optimization.  Multidisciplinary, Tiriti-aligned research is explicitly encouraged.  

- **AI in Health Research Network:** Following the 2023 Chief Science Advisor report, New Zealand created a national **AI in Health Research Network** ([www.ai-health-research.org.nz](https://www.ai-health-research.org.nz/#:~:text=The%20Network%20fosters%20relationship,ethics%2C%20equity%2C%20and%20data%20sovereignty)).  This coalition of universities, clinicians, iwi and industry shares knowledge and best practices (with an emphasis on ethics, equity and Māori data sovereignty) to fast-track AI tools into healthcare.  The Network hosts seminars (Defining trust in AI), newsletters, and is developing an online registry of NZ health-AI projects to coordinate efforts ([www.ai-health-research.org.nz](https://www.ai-health-research.org.nz/#:~:text=The%20Network%20fosters%20relationship,ethics%2C%20equity%2C%20and%20data%20sovereignty)) ([www.ai-health-research.org.nz](https://www.ai-health-research.org.nz/post/issue-one-july-2025#:~:text=This%20two,address%20over%20the%20coming%20months)).  Such infrastructure aims to break silos so NZ’s fragmented pilots can scale.  

- **Policy and Governance:** The Ministry of Health’s precision-health strategy treats AI and genomics as future cornerstones (diagnosis, personalized prevention, workforce support) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/699742/Precision-health-AI-and-genomics---Ministry-of-Health-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,for%20diagnosis%2C%20treatment%20planning%2C%20drug)).  It emphasizes transparent, evidence-based adoption.  Manatū Hauora (MoH) and Te Whatu Ora are working with international partners on frameworks/standards for AI safety and privacy before deploying new tools ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/699742/Precision-health-AI-and-genomics---Ministry-of-Health-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20Ministry%20of%20Health%E2%80%99s%20leadership,own%20unique%20health%20care%20context)).  Technology governance (e.g. the Public Sector Algorithm Charter) is being applied – for example, proposed national algorithms presumably require impact assessments.  New guidelines are being drafted: the Medical Council and specialist colleges plan AI ethics guidance for clinicians by 2025–26 ([www.nzdoctor.co.nz](https://www.nzdoctor.co.nz/article/news/medical-council-working-new-guidelines-use-ai#:~:text=The%20Medical%20Council%20is%20expected,Image%3A%20Suriphon%20Singha%20on%20iStock)).  Overall, regulators are coupling AI encouragement with oversight on bias, liability and data rights.  

## Case Studies  
- **Medow Health AI Co-Pilot:** Christchurch startup Medow Health has deployed an AI “co-pilot” for hospital specialists ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20uptake%20from%20specialists%20utilising,technology%20should%20supercharge%20a%20reduction)).  The system listens to in-person consults and drafts patient notes automatically. Early adopters report reclaiming ~2–3 hours of work per day (enough for 1–2 extra patients) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20uptake%20from%20specialists%20utilising,technology%20should%20supercharge%20a%20reduction)).  With NZ specialist waitlists in the tens of thousands, this breakthrough dramatically accelerates reporting and reduces burnout.  Medow’s tool is in use in NZ and Australia (60,000 specialist consults assisted last quarter) and exemplifies how AI can augment doctors safely (founders emphasize it “helps, not replaces” clinicians) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20uptake%20from%20specialists%20utilising,technology%20should%20supercharge%20a%20reduction)).  

- **AI Diabetic Retinal Screening Pilot:** In Feb 2025 the government launched NZ’s first AI-enabled disease-screening program ([www.beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/new-ai-diabetes-screening-pilot-programme-gamechanger-wait-times#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20pilot%20programme%20will%20enable,the%20pilot%20by%20an%20ophthalmologist)) ([www.beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/new-ai-diabetes-screening-pilot-programme-gamechanger-wait-times#:~:text=%E2%80%9CReal,attend%20to%20core%20clinical%20practices)).  Technicians at seven Mangere GP clinics capture retinal images from local diabetes patients; an AI model immediately grades them for retinopathy risk.  High-risk images are automatically sent to ophthalmologists, while normal cases are cleared.  This “real-time” triage has sharply increased screening volume and cut wait-times: thousands of at-risk patients now move quickly through the system, freeing specialists to focus on true cases ([www.beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/new-ai-diabetes-screening-pilot-programme-gamechanger-wait-times#:~:text=The%20pilot%20uses%20specially%20trained,attend%20to%20core%20clinical%20practices)).  It is a concrete example of population-health AI improving access in under-served communities.  

- **Veterans’ AI Chatbot (No Duff Trust):** The No Duff veterans’ charity developed an AI chatbot to support ex-service personnel (April 2025) ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=No%20Duff%20Charitable%20Trust%20created,support%2C%20mindfulness%20and%20breathing%20modules)).  Trained on 9 years of cabin conversations, the bot provides tailored text-based mental health support (mindfulness, breathing, crisis coping), alerting human clinicians if needed.  The chatbot is “grassroots” but shows AI extending care into nights/weekends when staff are scarce.  Initial feedback indicates veterans welcome it as a stopgap when formal help is delayed. This mirrors studies showing tech like GPT-chat (when properly trained) can approximate human counselling ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=Meanwhile%2C%20a%20recent%20clinical%20trial,in%20treating%20mental%20health%20issues)).  

- **Ambient AI Transcription (Dragon, Heidi, iMedX):** NZ hospitals have quietly adopted speech-driven documentation tools. For example, Te Whatu Ora Whanganui ED trialled Nuance Dragon Medical One (cloud speech-to-text) and found it captured “faster and more accurate” notes in real-time ([www.nuance.com](https://www.nuance.com/en-nz/healthcare/success/te-whatu-ora-whanganui.html#:~:text=,greater%20accuracy%20and%20improved%20efficiency)). More recently, the Ministry formally endorsed two new AI “scribe” platforms (Heidi Health and iMedX) for trial in public clinics ([www.healthcareitnews.com](https://www.healthcareitnews.com/news/anz/nz-private-healthcare-increasingly-adopts-ai-scribes-and-more-briefs#:~:text=Two%20ambient%20AI%20scribe%20tools,trial%20across%20public%20health%20services)).  These services passively record consults and populate records, vastly reducing admin. Similar global tools (already used overseas) now have a path to scale in NZ under trial protocols.  

- **NZ-Designed Ambulance AI (Hato Hone St John):** In late 2025 the national ambulance service prototyped an AI quality-audit tool ([www.healthcareitnews.com](https://www.healthcareitnews.com/news/anz/nz-private-healthcare-increasingly-adopts-ai-scribes-and-more-briefs#:~:text=Hato%20Hone%20St%20John%20tests,AI%20for%20clinical%20audits)). Hato Hone St John trained a local LLM on its clinical guidelines so it could rapidly review thousands of patient care reports.  In proof-of-concept, the AI flagged documentation gaps and suggested feedback, showing that even paramedicine can leverage generative AI to improve safety and compliance ([www.healthcareitnews.com](https://www.healthcareitnews.com/news/anz/nz-private-healthcare-increasingly-adopts-ai-scribes-and-more-briefs#:~:text=Hato%20Hone%20St%20John%20tests,AI%20for%20clinical%20audits)). 

## Trends and Outlook  
- **Cautious Expansion:** AI adoption is growing but uneven. A 2025 AI Forum survey found ~65% of health/social orgs have experimented with AI (mostly non-clinical) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=Intelligence%20on%20New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20productivity%2C,care%2C%20it%20covers%20a%20whole)). Nearly all clinical executives agree AI should *augment* rather than replace staff: only ~7% fear losing jobs to AI ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=that%20trust%2C%E2%80%9D%20he%20says,how%20far%20we%20want%20to)). As one health lead put it, “Health is being cautious when it comes to applications in clinical care” ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=administration%20and%20finance,that%20could%20have%20clinical%20impacts)).  Thus far NZ has favored well-validated niche tools (e.g. imaging analysis) and is enforcing human-in-the-loop checks (radiologists co-approve AI flags) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=to%20improve%20patient%20safety%2C%20streamline,explored%20to%20improve%20patient%20safety)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=MacDonald%20says%20studies%20show%20AI,enhance%20patient%20flow%2C%E2%80%9D%20she%20says)). Regulatory “gatekeeping” remains strict for any high-risk AI.  

- **Efficiency and Workflows:** Intensifying workforce shortages (∼8,000 vacancies) are driving NZ to apply AI to tedious tasks. Transcription, coding, scheduling and triage are hot spots. AI note-taking (Medow, Dragon, Heidi) can effectively add 10–25% clinical capacity ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20uptake%20from%20specialists%20utilising,technology%20should%20supercharge%20a%20reduction)) ([www.healthcareitnews.com](https://www.healthcareitnews.com/news/anz/nz-private-healthcare-increasingly-adopts-ai-scribes-and-more-briefs#:~:text=The%20survey%20found%20that%20two,imaging%20remain%20in%20pilot%20phases)). On the horizon are more tools: smart triage bots for ERs, automated image pre-screeners, and digital scribes in telehealth. Industry reports highlight “the biggest opportunity of our time”: by offloading admin work, AI frees clinicians to see more patients ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20uptake%20from%20specialists%20utilising,technology%20should%20supercharge%20a%20reduction)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=that%20trust%2C%E2%80%9D%20he%20says,how%20far%20we%20want%20to)).  NZ companies (Medow, local startups) and international vendors see a ready market. Pilots like the retina screening confirm that AI tackling bottlenecks (waitlists, referrals, coding) has immediate impact.  

- **Regulation and Ethics:** New Zealand’s approach to health AI is firmly ethical and Tiriti-informed. The Health Ministry and Te Whatu Ora are crafting frameworks (often with international consent standards) before widespread roll-out ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/699742/Precision-health-AI-and-genomics---Ministry-of-Health-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20Ministry%20of%20Health%E2%80%99s%20leadership,own%20unique%20health%20care%20context)). The Medical Council expects clinicians to maintain professional judgment when using AI ([www.nzdoctor.co.nz](https://www.nzdoctor.co.nz/article/news/medical-council-working-new-guidelines-use-ai#:~:text=The%20Medical%20Council%20is%20expected,Image%3A%20Suriphon%20Singha%20on%20iStock)), and professional colleges (e.g. RANZCR) have co-authored best-practice statements for their specialties.  Public trust is paramount: leaders consistently emphasize transparency about an AI’s capabilities and limits ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=future,they%20are%20going%20to%20lose)), and substantial oversight of patient data privacy. On liability, NZ’s no-fault ACC system would cover any harm costs, but experts note legal accountability (provider vs. developer) is unresolved ([brightstar.co.nz](https://brightstar.co.nz/ai-powered-diagnosis-a-game-changer-for-nz-health-but-who-bears-the-liability/#:~:text=New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20ACC%20Approach%20and,scenarios%20discussed%20in%20the%20article)).  Calls are growing for clear standards, continuous monitoring, and mandatory disclosures to patients when AI is used in their care ([brightstar.co.nz](https://brightstar.co.nz/ai-powered-diagnosis-a-game-changer-for-nz-health-but-who-bears-the-liability/#:~:text=Addressing%20the%20liability%20gap)) ([www.pmcsa.ac.nz](https://www.pmcsa.ac.nz/artificial-intelligence-2/ai-in-healthcare/#:~:text=Deploying%20the%20right%20AI%20technology,technology%20to%20support%20human%20care)).  

- **Equity and Inclusion:** The NZ health strategy explicitly ties AI to equity goals. Stakeholders stress *Te Tiriti o Waitangi*–aligned deployment (indigenous governance) and addressing the digital divide. An AI network highlights ethics, equity and Māori data sovereignty as core ([www.ai-health-research.org.nz](https://www.ai-health-research.org.nz/#:~:text=The%20Network%20fosters%20relationship,ethics%2C%20equity%2C%20and%20data%20sovereignty)). Māori researchers are already embedding iwi values in AI design; e.g. Dr. Daniel Wilson’s *Tikanga Technology* project creates Maori-guided AI principles so that algorithms benefit (not disadvantage) Māori communities ([www.ai-health-research.org.nz](https://www.ai-health-research.org.nz/post/issue-one-july-2025#:~:text=Artificial%20intelligence%20is%20rapidly%20shaping,these%20systems%20safer%20and%20more)). The CSA 2023 report similarly warns that AI carries both promise (reducing disparities) and risk (worsening bias) ([www.pmcsa.ac.nz](https://www.pmcsa.ac.nz/artificial-intelligence-2/ai-in-healthcare/#:~:text=Deploying%20the%20right%20AI%20technology,technology%20to%20support%20human%20care)) ([www.pmcsa.ac.nz](https://www.pmcsa.ac.nz/artificial-intelligence-2/ai-in-healthcare/#:~:text=,than%20the%20healthcare%20system%20itself)). Future AI rollouts (like genomic screening) will need to ensure all populations can reap benefits.  

- **Future Directions:** Momentum is building for **precision and preventive health**. NZ’s genomic initiatives (newborn sequencing, cancer genomics) provide data that AI could use to predict disease risk and tailor interventions.  Experts envision AI-driven screening programs (e.g. personalizing wellness cues via wearable data) as major future shifts. Meanwhile, virtual care — already enabled by mediated chats and remote monitoring — is likely to incorporate more AI (for triage/chatbots in general practice and mental health).  However, analysts caution on sustainability: AI’s energy use and the need for public AI literacy are concerns to address ([www.pmcsa.ac.nz](https://www.pmcsa.ac.nz/artificial-intelligence-2/ai-in-healthcare/#:~:text=Deploying%20the%20right%20AI%20technology,technology%20to%20support%20human%20care)). Overall, consensus is that NZ cannot maintain the status quo; AI will be one pillar of transformation, provided it is rolled out incrementally with safeguards.  

## Conclusion  
- AI is **increasingly entering** New Zealand’s healthcare system through targeted pilots and digital tools.  Recent initiatives – from automated imaging triage to clinical note-generators – have shown real productivity gains (e.g. more patients screened, hours saved for doctors) ([www.beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/new-ai-diabetes-screening-pilot-programme-gamechanger-wait-times#:~:text=%E2%80%9CReal,attend%20to%20core%20clinical%20practices)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20uptake%20from%20specialists%20utilising,technology%20should%20supercharge%20a%20reduction)).  
- The prevailing strategy is to **augment, not replace**, clinicians: government and industry leaders emphasize that AI must operate under strict ethical oversight and complement human expertise ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=to%20improve%20patient%20safety%2C%20streamline,explored%20to%20improve%20patient%20safety)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=straight%20pattern%20recognition%20and%20anomaly,they%20are%20going%20to%20lose)). Public trust and equity guide all deployments.  
- With continued funding (RFPs, international grants) and collaboration (health agencies, academia, Māori stakeholders), NZ is steadily building the ecosystem to scale AI.  As these pilots mature into broader programs, this *Living Whitepaper* will be updated to inform stakeholders on leveraging AI safely and effectively for better health outcomes in Aotearoa.
