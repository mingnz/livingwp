# AI Adoption in Healthcare in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper

## Introduction  
- New Zealand’s healthcare system is under mounting strain from an aging population and chronic workforce shortages (e.g. an estimated shortfall of ~4,800 nurses and 1,700 doctors today, with a further 8,000 nurses and 3,400 doctors needed by 2033 ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/493139/te-whatu-ora-launches-strategy-for-health-recruitment-and-retention#:~:text=New%20Zealand%20is%20estimated%20to,with%20demand%20out%20to%202033))). These pressures have prompted policymakers and clinicians to look toward technology – especially AI – to enhance efficiency and extend clinical capacity.  
- The government’s **“precision health”** vision aims to tailor care using individual data (genes, lifestyle, environment) and safe AI tools ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=Precision%20health%20is%20an%20approach,people%27s%20genes%2C%20environments%2C%20and%20lifestyles)).  The Ministry of Health emphasizes that precision health technologies (including AI and genomics) must be **accessible, safe, effective, equitable and accountable**, with transparent evaluation of impacts ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,health%20of%20all%20New%20Zealanders)).  
- AI uptake in NZ healthcare remains in early stages: mainly **pilot projects and supportive applications** rather than frontline decision-making.  National bodies underscore that AI should *augment* – not replace – clinicians, and must operate under strict privacy and quality guardrails ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=Mr%20Freiberg%20said%20the%20new,medical%20specialty%20to%20examine%20patient)) ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,health%20of%20all%20New%20Zealanders)). For example, Te Whatu Ora (Health NZ) has explicitly warned staff **not to use** unvetted generative AI (e.g. ChatGPT) for patient care, noting that these tools “have not been validated as safe and effective” in a clinical context ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/651034/#:~:text=Te%20Whatu%20Ora%20advises%20against,using%20Generative%20AI%20in%20health)).  

## Current Developments  
- **National AI Strategy (July 2025):** The Government’s new AI strategy calls out healthcare as a key sector for AI innovation ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/705831/New-AI-strategy-highlights-potential-for-healthcare.htm#:~:text=technology%20Minister%20Shane%20Reti,in%20healthcare%2C%20Kiwi%20businesses%20can)).  Science Minister Shane Reti highlighted AI-powered diagnostics as an example of NZ businesses tailoring AI “to solve our unique challenges” in health ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/705831/New-AI-strategy-highlights-potential-for-healthcare.htm#:~:text=technology%20Minister%20Shane%20Reti,in%20healthcare%2C%20Kiwi%20businesses%20can)).  The strategy aims to accelerate AI adoption (across all industries) while removing legislative barriers and investing in public sector upskilling.  
- **Policy & Guidance:** In Sept 2023, Te Whatu Ora issued official guidance prohibiting staff from feeding any patient data into ChatGPT/LLMs or relying on them for clinical decisions, emphasizing the need for validated, secure tools ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/651034/#:~:text=Te%20Whatu%20Ora%20advises%20against,using%20Generative%20AI%20in%20health)).  Similarly, international best-practice statements (e.g. from radiology and medical colleges) stress responsible data use and transparency.  The Medical Council of NZ is also developing guidelines on AI for doctors, with new rules expected by end-2025 ([www.healthreformcollective.ac.nz](https://www.healthreformcollective.ac.nz/medical-council-working-on-new-guidelines-for-use-of-ai/page/78/?el_dbe_page=#:~:text=The%20Medical%20Council%20is%20expected,to%20board%20member%20Stephen%20Child)).  
- **AI Screening Pilot – Diabetic Retinopathy (Feb 2025):** Health New Zealand launched a South Auckland pilot (Māngere) where community workers use retinal cameras and an AI algorithm to pre-screen diabetic patients’ eye scans ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694795/AI-diabetes-screening-pilot-launches-in-South-Auckland-.htm#:~:text=Simeon%20Brown,Harriet%20Pauga%2C%20regional%20director%20northern)). Abnormal images are flagged in real time to ophthalmologists.  Over a 3-month pilot (~840 patients), the AI allows **real-time grading**, thereby expanding screening capacity and shortening waits ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694795/AI-diabetes-screening-pilot-launches-in-South-Auckland-.htm#:~:text=result,Te%20Whatu%20Ora%2C%20CK%20Jin)).  If successful, the program will scale to ≥3,000 patients to fully validate performance – potentially reducing specialist load and improving early detection of sight-threatening disease ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694795/AI-diabetes-screening-pilot-launches-in-South-Auckland-.htm#:~:text=region%2C%20Pacific%20Health%2C%20Health%20New,to%20impaired%20or%20lost%20vision)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694795/AI-diabetes-screening-pilot-launches-in-South-Auckland-.htm#:~:text=result,Te%20Whatu%20Ora%2C%20CK%20Jin)).  
- **Radiology AI Pilots (May 2025):** Te Whatu Ora’s AI Lab is working with radiology teams to explore AI for high-volume imaging (e.g. chest X-rays, CT brain scans, and fracture detection) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=Health%20NZ%E2%80%99s%20AI%20Lab%20is,use%20in%20New%20Zealand%20help)). Pilot projects will use AI as a “co-pilot” – triaging cases and checking consistency – rather than replacing clinicians ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=Stuart%20Barnard%2C%20clinical%20director%20of,reduction%20in%20the%20radiation%20dose)). Early signals are promising: for instance, Canterbury DHB is testing tools that flag over 100 different abnormalities on X-rays and CTs, helping radiologists prioritize urgent cases ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThese%20solutions%20never%20get%20tired,could%20be%20integrated%20into%20our)). Leaders note that AI can “never get tired” of scanning images, which could improve safety and throughput ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThese%20solutions%20never%20get%20tired,could%20be%20integrated%20into%20our)).  
- **Clinical Documentation AI (April 2025):** Medow Health (a Kiwi startup) launched an AI “co-pilot” that listens to specialist–patient consultations and automatically drafts notes and reports ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=SECTOR%20UPDATE%20,one%20or%20two%20patients%20a)).  Early adopter specialists report regaining 2–3 hours of time per day, enabling 1–2 extra consults daily and alleviating burnout ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20uptake%20from%20specialists%20utilising,more%20than%2065%2C000%20patients%20waited)).  Joel Freiberg (Medow co-founder) says the goal is to “help doctors” by handling paperwork, so clinicians can focus on patients ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=Mr%20Freiberg%20said%20the%20new,medical%20specialty%20to%20examine%20patient)). The company has already processed 60,000 hospital consultations last quarter (in NZ/Australia) and plans rapid growth.  
- **Mental Health Chatbot (Apr 2024):** The No Duff Trust, a veterans’ mental health charity, built an AI chatbot trained on 9 years of peer-support conversations to assist veterans in crisis ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=No%20Duff%20Charitable%20Trust%20created,support%2C%20mindfulness%20and%20breathing%20modules)).  The chatbot offers crisis, mindfulness and breathing modules 24/7, with clinician oversight and built-in red flags for emergencies ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=,done%20and%20we%27re%20doing%20it)). The trust frames this “grassroots” AI tool as a **supplement** to formal care – “not designed to circumvent the mental health system, it’s designed to complement it” ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=,it%27s%20designed%20to%20complement%20it)). Early feedback suggests veterans welcome any additional support when human help is sparse.  
- **Regulatory Initiatives:**  Associate Health Minister David Seymour has encouraged **Pharmac and Medsafe** to leverage AI to streamline drug and device assessment processes ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/704851/Medical-device-decision-drags-on-as-Minister-encourages-use-of-AI.htm#:~:text=productivity%20and%20he%20wants%20to,I)).  Streamlining dossier reviews and adverse-effect analyses with AI aims to speed access to new therapies. Additionally, Health Research Council launched a **$5 million RFP (Feb 2025)** for investigator-led AI health research ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694879/#:~:text=Council%20will%20invest%20%245%20million,director%20investments%20Stacey%20Pene%20tells)), signaling strong public funding support.  

## Research Overview  
- **HRC AI Healthcare RFP (Feb 2025):** The Health Research Council committed **$5M** for AI-focused health projects ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694879/#:~:text=Council%20will%20invest%20%245%20million,director%20investments%20Stacey%20Pene%20tells)). Proposals must align with government health priorities and explicitly address safe, ethical AI use to improve outcomes.  Fundable topics include workflow efficiency (streamlining processes, reducing wait times) and enhanced diagnostics (early disease detection, predictive analytics) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694879/#:~:text=research%20the%20HRC%20is%20particularly,detection%20and%20predictive%20analytics%2C%E2%80%9D%20says)).  Grants range $100k–$700k (6–24 months), encouraging multi-disciplinary teams that include clinical stakeholder partnerships ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694879/#:~:text=research%20the%20HRC%20is%20particularly,of%20disciplines%20in%20the%20research)).  
- **International Collaborations:** MBIE’s Catalyst fund is co-financing a NZ–Singapore call (2025) for AI solutions in **healthy ageing**, targeting neurological and mental health conditions in older adults ([www.mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/funding-information-and-opportunities/investment-funds/catalyst-fund/new-zealand-singapore-leveraging-ai-for-healthy-ageing-2025-call-for-proposals#:~:text=The%20call%20seeks%20novel%2C%20scalable%2C,achieve%20this%2C%20we%20need%20to)). This reflects NZ’s interest in cross-border R&D to tackle shared demographic challenges.  
- **Coordination Network:** In response to the PM Chief Science Advisor’s 2023 AI Report, the **AI in Health Research Network** was established to unite experts nationwide ([www.ai-health-research.org.nz](https://www.ai-health-research.org.nz/#:~:text=The%20PMCSA%E2%80%99s%20AI%20in%20Health,R22)). This forum (under MedTech Aotearoa) brings together academics, clinicians, iwi/Māori and industry to co-develop Tiriti-aligned AI tools and to smooth the path from R&D to clinical use ([www.ai-health-research.org.nz](https://www.ai-health-research.org.nz/#:~:text=The%20objective%20of%20the%20Network,will%20consist%20of)). Its objectives include setting research principles (with Treaty obligations) and demonstrating real-world pilots as exemplars of best practice ([www.ai-health-research.org.nz](https://www.ai-health-research.org.nz/#:~:text=The%20objective%20of%20the%20Network,will%20consist%20of)).  
- **Policy Context:**  The Ministry’s “Precision Health, AI and Genomics” programme highlights AI as key to future diagnostics, planning and workforce support ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=Precision%20health%20is%20an%20approach,people%27s%20genes%2C%20environments%2C%20and%20lifestyles)).  It stresses that any AI roll-out must be transparent, supervised and evaluated for all NZ groups ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,health%20of%20all%20New%20Zealanders)). Health NZ is collaborating internationally (via advisory groups) to design regulatory guardrails before scaling new tools.  Notably, large language model applications remain on hold until proven safe in NZ care ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/651034/#:~:text=Te%20Whatu%20Ora%20advises%20against,using%20Generative%20AI%20in%20health)) ([www.1news.co.nz](https://www.1news.co.nz/2024/03/23/analysis-how-artificial-intelligence-will-change-nz-healthcare/#:~:text=But%20as%20the%20report%20suggests%2C,effective%20for%20use%20in%20healthcare)) – underscoring the cautious approach.  

## Case Studies  
- **Medow Health AI Assistant:** This NZ startup’s AI “scribe” records specialist consultations and drafts reports ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=SECTOR%20UPDATE%20,one%20or%20two%20patients%20a)).  In practice, doctors regain ~2–3 hours per day, enabling extra patient consults and cutting waitlists (currently >65,000 NZ patients wait >4 months for a specialist) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20uptake%20from%20specialists%20utilising,technology%20should%20supercharge%20a%20reduction)). Medow emphasizes the augmentation message: “We’re not trying to replace doctors, we’re trying to help them” ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=Mr%20Freiberg%20said%20the%20new,medical%20specialty%20to%20examine%20patient)).  The company is already deployed in NZ and Australia, illustrating how AI can scale efficiency under clinician oversight.  
- **AI Diabetes Retinal Screening (South Auckland Pilot):**  Seven GP clinics in Māngere are equipped with retinal cameras; trained techs capture diabetic patients’ images, which an AI tool grades in real time ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694795/AI-diabetes-screening-pilot-launches-in-South-Auckland-.htm#:~:text=Simeon%20Brown,Harriet%20Pauga%2C%20regional%20director%20northern)).  Abnormal scans trigger immediate specialist review.  The pilot (≈840 patients over 3 months) greatly boosts screening throughput at the community level.  Early data suggest it can **increase screening volumes and shorten wait times** while freeing ophthalmologists to focus on confirmed cases ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694795/AI-diabetes-screening-pilot-launches-in-South-Auckland-.htm#:~:text=result,Te%20Whatu%20Ora%2C%20CK%20Jin)).  This is one of NZ’s first real-world AI disease-screening deployments.  
- **Veterans’ AI Chat Therapy:** No Duff Trust’s chatbot (late 2024) demonstrates AI for tele-mental health.  Trained on 9 years of local data, it offers those in crisis access to peer-guided coping strategies anytime ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=No%20Duff%20Charitable%20Trust%20created,support%2C%20mindfulness%20and%20breathing%20modules)) ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=,done%20and%20we%27re%20doing%20it)).  Importantly, it falls under clinician review protocols – an “answer” to resource gaps, not a system bypass ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=,it%27s%20designed%20to%20complement%20it)). This case highlights AI’s potential to extend reach in underserved communities under human supervision.  
- **Ambient Scribes & Existing AI Tools:** Even beyond pilots, some AI-driven tools are quietly in use. For example, **Nuance/Microsoft Dragon Medical One** (speech recognition) is approved in NZ DHBs for dictating notes ([news.microsoft.com](https://news.microsoft.com/en-nz/2024/02/27/scripting-a-new-era-of-healthcare-the-rise-of-ai-enhanced-medicine/#:~:text=Other%20AI%20implementations%2C%20like%20the,one%20interactions)).  It listens to doctor–patient interviews and auto-fills medical records, saving hours of typing.  In radiology and pathology, computer-aided detection (CAD) software already flags possible abnormalities on imaging to assist clinicians ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20initial%20focus%20has%20been,reconstruction%20algorithms%20which%20reduce%20patient)).  These “narrow AI” applications work under established medical device regulations, offering efficiency gains without shocking the system.  

## Trends and Predictions  
- **Gradual, Cautious Adoption:** A 2025 AI Forum survey found ~65% of NZ health/social service organisations report **some AI use**, mainly in non-clinical tasks ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/#:~:text=overall%20reported%20some%20level%20of,other%20industries%2C%20the%20sector%20is)).  Overall, 80% of NZ organisations use AI in some capacity (93% say it boosts efficiency) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/#:~:text=following%20suit,other%20industries%2C%20the%20sector%20is)).  In healthcare, the focus remains on admin, data processing and management, not frontline decision-making ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/#:~:text=overall%20reported%20some%20level%20of,other%20industries%2C%20the%20sector%20is)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/#:~:text=business%20functions%20that%20everybody%20can,advanced%20uses%20for%20diagnostics%20and)). Stakeholders consistently say AI should augment – not replace – clinicians. As Reviewed Above, data sensitivity and patient trust are top concerns, so new tools undergo thorough validation before scaling.  
- **Efficiency Imperative:** Workforce shortages (RNZ reports NZ needs thousands more nurses and doctors by 2033 ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/493139/te-whatu-ora-launches-strategy-for-health-recruitment-and-retention#:~:text=New%20Zealand%20is%20estimated%20to,with%20demand%20out%20to%202033))) and long waitlists are driving AI interest.  Companies and DHBs are prioritizing AI solutions for bottlenecks: ambient voice scribe software, automated coding/triage systems, and pre-screening tools (like the above pilots) promise to **free clinician time** and reduce backlogs ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20uptake%20from%20specialists%20utilising,more%20than%2065%2C000%20patients%20waited)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694795/AI-diabetes-screening-pilot-launches-in-South-Auckland-.htm#:~:text=result,Te%20Whatu%20Ora%2C%20CK%20Jin)). Public and private sector leaders expect AI triage (for imaging, labs, etc.) and diagnostics support to spread, starting with well-defined tasks where AI excels (pattern recognition, anomaly detection) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/702056/Health-NZ-explores-use-of-AI-in-radiology.htm#:~:text=Stuart%20Barnard%2C%20clinical%20director%20of,reduction%20in%20the%20radiation%20dose)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/#:~:text=GP%2C%20and%20the%20AI%20will,clinical%20care%20brings%20real%20world)).  
- **Regulation and Ethics:** New Zealand is developing “guardrails” for AI in health. Research networks and MoH strategy emphasize Tiriti-aligned, equitable practices ([www.ai-health-research.org.nz](https://www.ai-health-research.org.nz/#:~:text=The%20objective%20of%20the%20Network,will%20consist%20of)) ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,health%20of%20all%20New%20Zealanders)). The health regulator (Medical Council) and professional colleges are issuing guidance on safe, ethical AI use. Liability questions are emerging: NZ’s no-fault ACC system covers patient injury, yet it’s unclear how accountability will work if AI errors go unchecked.  Public sentiment underscores caution – surveys find >80% of New Zealanders want stricter AI regulation ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/BU202507/S00053.htm#:~:text=figure%20of%2060,believing%20current%20safeguards%20are%20adequate)), reflecting anxieties about privacy and bias. Transparency and explainability will be essential to maintain public trust.  
- **Precision Health and Future Care:** NZ’s expanding genomic and data infrastructure is primed for AI-driven personalized healthcare. The country already offers genomic screening for newborns nationwide and is building a genomic health service ([www.1news.co.nz](https://www.1news.co.nz/2024/03/23/analysis-how-artificial-intelligence-will-change-nz-healthcare/#:~:text=New%20Zealand%20has%20a%20long,precision%20public%20health%20for%20everyone)). As such, experts foresee AI combining with genetics and wearables to enable truly “precision prevention” – where risk models guide individualized screening and treatment plans. The PMCSA and media analysis suggest such innovations are on the horizon ([www.1news.co.nz](https://www.1news.co.nz/2024/03/23/analysis-how-artificial-intelligence-will-change-nz-healthcare/#:~:text=New%20Zealand%20has%20a%20long,precision%20public%20health%20for%20everyone)). However, commentators caution that disparities (digital literacy, access) must not widen: equity must be baked into AI-enabled care.  For now, advanced GenAI applications (like LLM-based diagnostics or public-health chatbots) remain on hold pending robust safety reviews ([www.1news.co.nz](https://www.1news.co.nz/2024/03/23/analysis-how-artificial-intelligence-will-change-nz-healthcare/#:~:text=But%20as%20the%20report%20suggests%2C,effective%20for%20use%20in%20healthcare)).  

## Conclusion  
- AI is **progressively entering** New Zealand’s healthcare system through focused pilots and behind-the-scenes tools. Recent initiatives – from diabetic retinal screening to AI transcription assistants – are already yielding benefits by reducing workloads and wait times ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/694795/AI-diabetes-screening-pilot-launches-in-South-Auckland-.htm#:~:text=result,Te%20Whatu%20Ora%2C%20CK%20Jin)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=The%20uptake%20from%20specialists%20utilising,more%20than%2065%2C000%20patients%20waited)).  
- The consensus among clinicians and officials is that AI should **complement** human expertise, acting as a helpful “co-pilot” rather than supplanting providers ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698106/Cutting-edge-AI-technology-designed-for-doctors-to-reduce-patient-wait-times-launched-in-NZ.htm#:~:text=Mr%20Freiberg%20said%20the%20new,medical%20specialty%20to%20examine%20patient)) ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/2022/10/31/ai-in-healthcare-nz-leading-the-world/#:~:text=AI%20is%20not%20about%20replacing,of%20massive%20health%20skills%20shortages)).  All stakeholders stress that new AI tools must be proven safe, equitable and transparent before wide deployment ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/651034/#:~:text=Te%20Whatu%20Ora%20advises%20against,using%20Generative%20AI%20in%20health)) ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,health%20of%20all%20New%20Zealanders)).
