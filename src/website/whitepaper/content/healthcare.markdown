# AI Adoption in Healthcare in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper

## Introduction  
- New Zealand’s healthcare system faces mounting pressure from an aging population and chronic workforce shortages.  The AI Forum NZ notes pervasive nurse and doctor shortages and says “AI – artificial intelligence – is the answer” to amplify scarce expertise by automating diagnostics and other tasks ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/2022/10/31/ai-in-healthcare-nz-leading-the-world/#:~:text=The%20AI%20Forum%20NZ%20says,artificial%20intelligence%2C%20is%20the%20answer)).  
- The Government’s **Precision Health** strategy highlights using genomics and personal data with AI to tailor care, but stresses technologies must be safe, effective, equitable and transparent ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,health%20of%20all%20New%20Zealanders)).  In practice NZ’s AI use is still early-stage, focusing on pilot projects and non-clinical applications to boost access and efficiency ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,health%20of%20all%20New%20Zealanders)).  
- Health leaders take a cautious-but-engaged stance.  For example, Te Whatu Ora (Health NZ) has **blocked access** to ChatGPT and similar LLM tools as of 2023, while developing policies (via an AI advisory group) to manage AI use in line with values, privacy and safety ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/639523/ChatGPT-blocked-while-Te-Whatu-Ora-considers-its-use.htm#:~:text=ChatGPT%20have%20been%20blocked%20by,these%20platforms%20safely%2C%E2%80%9D%20he%20says)). Overall policymakers insist any AI in health be transparent, validated and delivered under robust ethical and Tiriti-aligned oversight.

## Recent Developments  
- **Chemo Chatbots & Triage:** Veteran charity *No Duff Trust* has launched a proof-of-concept AI chatbot (trained on nine years of peer-support conversation data) to provide 24/7 crisis support for veterans ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=,done%20and%20we%27re%20doing%20it)).  This clinician-supervised service reflects growing interest in AI for mental health.  (Notably, a Dartmouth College study found a well-trained AI therapy chatbot could match human therapists in treating anxiety/depression ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=Meanwhile%2C%20a%20recent%20clinical%20trial,in%20treating%20mental%20health%20issues)).) Such tools aim to extend care access where human resources are limited.  
- **AI-Assisted Retinal Screening (Feb 2025):** An Auckland pilot uses AI to drastically speed up diabetic eye screening ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA2502/S00193/new-ai-diabetes-screening-pilot-programme-a-gamechanger-for-wait-times.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20use%20of%20AI%20in,time%20screening%20results.%E2%80%9D)) ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA2502/S00193/new-ai-diabetes-screening-pilot-programme-a-gamechanger-for-wait-times.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20pilot%20uses%20community%20workers,up%20review%20by%20an%20ophthalmologist)). Seven South Auckland clinics now have retinal cameras: technicians capture images which an AI model immediately grades for signs of retinopathy.  Abnormal scans are auto-referred to ophthalmologists, enabling “real-time” results instead of multi-month waits.  Officials report this greatly increases screening volumes and cuts specialist waitlists, while freeing doctors to focus on confirmed cases ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA2502/S00193/new-ai-diabetes-screening-pilot-programme-a-gamechanger-for-wait-times.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20use%20of%20AI%20in,time%20screening%20results.%E2%80%9D)) ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA2502/S00193/new-ai-diabetes-screening-pilot-programme-a-gamechanger-for-wait-times.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20pilot%20uses%20community%20workers,up%20review%20by%20an%20ophthalmologist)).  
- **Clinical Documentation “Co-Pilots” (Apr 2025):** NZ startup Medow Health has rolled out an AI “scribe” that listens to doctor–patient consultations and drafts the notes autonomously.  Early reports indicate specialists recover **2–3 hours per day** of clinical time, allowing more patient consults and reducing burnout.  Indeed, in Australia/NZ trials Medow’s co-pilot handled over 60,000 consultations in one quarter (with use doubling soon) ([www.pulseit.news](https://www.pulseit.news/australian-digital-health/patients-over-paperwork-as-specialists-adopt-ai/#:~:text=Specialist%20doctors%20across%20Australia%20and,%E2%80%A6)). Such tools are a prime example of AI augmenting (not replacing) physicians by automating paperwork.  
- **Radiology AI Exploration (2025):** Health NZ’s AI Lab is collaborating with radiologists to pilot AI on routine imaging (e.g. chest X-rays, CT scans). The vision is an “AI co-pilot” that flags critical findings and triages cases, improving patient safety and efficiency.  (For example, Canterbury DHB is testing AI algorithms that detect dozens of abnormalities on an X-ray to prioritize sicker patients.) These studies emphasize AI assisting radiologists rather than replacing them, aligning with radiology bodies’ calls for best-practice AI use in NZ.  
- **Industry Events & Guidance:** The digital health community is actively engaging on AI.  HiNZ’s 2025 Digital Health AI Summit (Wellington, May 2025) convened clinicians, policymakers and tech experts to share pilots and develop action plans.  Professional groups are issuing guidelines: e.g. radiologists (RANZCR) are working on safe AI frameworks. These forums signal a readiness to adopt proven AI tools (like enhanced imaging and workflow automation) under careful regulation.

## Research & Initiatives  
- **Government Funding:** In Feb 2025 the NZ Health Research Council (HRC) announced a NZ$5 million RFP for healthcare AI projects ([www.hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/news-and-events/new-ai-healthcare-rfp#:~:text=The%20Health%20Research%20Council%20of,quality%20healthcare%20for%20New%20Zealanders)).  Grants (NZ$100k–700k) will fund investigator-led studies (starting Aug 2025) that address national health priorities via *safe, ethical* AI solutions.  Areas of interest include analytics to reduce wait times and boost efficiency, improve diagnostics/predictions, and tailor interventions in alignment with the Health NZ strategy ([www.hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/news-and-events/new-ai-healthcare-rfp#:~:text=The%20Health%20Research%20Council%20of,quality%20healthcare%20for%20New%20Zealanders)) ([www.hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/news-and-events/new-ai-healthcare-rfp#:~:text=A%20total%20funding%20pool%20of,all%20these%20amounts%20exclude%20GST)). This shows New Zealand investing in evidence-based AI innovation.  
- **Expert Networks:** Following the Chief Science Adviser’s December 2023 report on AI in health, an “AI in Health Research Network” is being established to connect NZ experts.  Its goals include sharing knowledge, upskilling clinicians in AI, and co-developing AI tools under Tiriti-guided ethics.  This coordination aims to streamline efforts and ensure that NZ projects translate rapidly into practice.  
- **Policy & Strategy:** AI is now formally part of NZ’s health policy landscape.  The Ministry of Health’s Precision Health strategy explicitly identifies AI for applications from diagnostics to workforce support, under a framework of transparency and equity ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,health%20of%20all%20New%20Zealanders)).  Meanwhile, international benchmarking is under way: New Zealand agencies are engaging with Australian and global partners to develop oversight, data governance and liability frameworks before wider clinical rollouts.  For example, NZ’s no-fault patient injury system (ACC) is expected to cover AI-related errors, though details are pending review.  

## Case Studies  
- **Medow Health AI Assistant:** Medow Health (a NZ startup) exemplifies AI augmenting clinicians.  Its AI co-pilot listens to specialist consultations and auto-generates reports.  In practice it has dramatically shortened admin work: doctors report saving hours per day and seeing more patients ([www.pulseit.news](https://www.pulseit.news/australian-digital-health/patients-over-paperwork-as-specialists-adopt-ai/#:~:text=Specialist%20doctors%20across%20Australia%20and,%E2%80%A6)). By mid-2024 the tool was used in 60K patient visits in Australia/NZ, with usage growing rapidly ([www.pulseit.news](https://www.pulseit.news/australian-digital-health/patients-over-paperwork-as-specialists-adopt-ai/#:~:text=Specialist%20doctors%20across%20Australia%20and,%E2%80%A6)).  Medow’s model – built by clinicians for clinicians – demonstrates how AI can enhance capacity without replacing human care ([www.pulseit.news](https://www.pulseit.news/australian-digital-health/patients-over-paperwork-as-specialists-adopt-ai/#:~:text=Specialist%20doctors%20across%20Australia%20and,%E2%80%A6)).  
- **Diabetic Retinal Screening Pilot:** The Māngere trial described above is one of NZ’s first AI healthcare screening programs ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA2502/S00193/new-ai-diabetes-screening-pilot-programme-a-gamechanger-for-wait-times.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20use%20of%20AI%20in,time%20screening%20results.%E2%80%9D)) ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA2502/S00193/new-ai-diabetes-screening-pilot-programme-a-gamechanger-for-wait-times.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20pilot%20uses%20community%20workers,up%20review%20by%20an%20ophthalmologist)).  It leverages community techs and scooters to reach underserved areas.  Early feedback suggests more patients are getting screened closer to home and earlier interventions are possible, validating AI’s promise as a “force multiplier” in preventive health.  
- **Veterans’ AI Chat Therapy:** New Zealand’s No Duff charity trained an in-house chatbot on nine years of veteran support transcripts ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=,done%20and%20we%27re%20doing%20it)).  Though still in alpha testing, it aims to give veterans immediate access to coping tools (guided mindfulness, breathing exercises, etc.) day or night, supervised by clinicians.  This grassroots initiative highlights AI’s potential to fill gaps – a Dartmouth study even found such a curated mental-health chatbot can rival human therapy for anxiety/depression ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/558481/charity-launches-ai-chatbot-therapy-service-for-veterans#:~:text=Meanwhile%2C%20a%20recent%20clinical%20trial,in%20treating%20mental%20health%20issues)). Early NZ feedback suggests veterans welcome this supplementary support when traditional services are inaccessible.  
- **Existing AI Tools in Practice:** NZ health services are quietly adopting proven “narrow AI” solutions.  For instance, many District Health Boards use speech-recognition systems (e.g. Dragon Medical) to transcribe doctor notes, dramatically saving documentation time.  Similarly, computer-aided detection algorithms for X-rays/CT scans (already FDA-approved overseas) are running in the background in some NZ hospitals, helping to reduce diagnostic delays.  These tools are considered medical devices under existing rules, illustrating NZ’s incremental, risk-managed AI uptake.

## Trends and Outlook  
- **Cautious Expansion:** A 2025 AI Forum survey found ~65% of NZ health and social services organizations report *some* AI use (versus ~80% overall) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=following%20suit,areas%20such%20as%20administration%20and)).  Most applications are back-office (scheduling, paperwork, coding) rather than clinical, reflecting privacy and safety cautions ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=following%20suit,areas%20such%20as%20administration%20and)).  Importantly, only ~7% foresee AI replacing health workers; instead, stakeholders emphasize AI as a tool to boost an overstretched workforce ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=that%20trust%2C%E2%80%9D%20he%20says,how%20far%20we%20want%20to)). As one AI Leader notes, “We cannot deliver healthcare the way we have for 50 years” – AI is about augmenting people to meet rising demand ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=that%20trust%2C%E2%80%9D%20he%20says,how%20far%20we%20want%20to)).  
- **Efficiency and Access:** Workforce shortages (estimated ~8,000 vacancies) and long waitlists remain top pressures. AI is being positioned as a productivity lever.  Current pilots target bottlenecks: automated transcription frees clinician time, AI-assisted triage directs patients faster, and automated scans/screenings expand throughput.  The success of pilot programs (like the retinal screening) is driving interest in scaling such applications.  NZ companies (e.g. Medow, Sectaur) and international partners see NZ as a proving ground for AI tools that can alleviate workforce strain.  
- **Regulation, Ethics and Trust:** The consensus is “move fast, but with guardrails.”  NZ’s AI approach stresses transparency, privacy and equity at every step ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,health%20of%20all%20New%20Zealanders)). Regulatory bodies (Ministry of Health, ACC, professional colleges) are defining standards for data use, model validation and clinician training.  For example, any AI system for diagnostics will require clinical trials and reporting codes before rollout.  Māori health leaders insist on Tiriti-informed practices to ensure AI helps close – not widen – health gaps.  Public trust is recognized as critical; all AI deployments so far have been framed as augmenting clinicians under human oversight.  
- **Future Directions:** A growing theme is **precision health**: combining genomics, wearables and AI to predict and prevent disease.  Newborn genome sequencing and expanded genetics services are laying the data groundwork.  Experts envision AI-driven personalized screening schedules and treatment plans in coming years.  However, there are cautions: NPIs note AI’s energy/environmental costs and the risk that tech-savvy groups benefit first.  Policymakers are watching global developments (e.g. the UK’s and EU’s AI health regulations) to inform NZ’s path.  Overall, the message is clear: NZ’s current health model is unsustainable.  AI is rapidly moving from concept to reality in targeted areas, and all stakeholders are actively preparing – from digital health conferences to new research consortia – to integrate AI as a core component of future healthcare.

## Conclusion  
New Zealand’s healthcare sector is in the early but accelerating phase of AI adoption.  Recent pilots – from AI-assisted eye screenings to AI medical scribes – have demonstrated measurable benefits in reducing workload and wait times ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA2502/S00193/new-ai-diabetes-screening-pilot-programme-a-gamechanger-for-wait-times.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20use%20of%20AI%20in,time%20screening%20results.%E2%80%9D)) ([www.pulseit.news](https://www.pulseit.news/australian-digital-health/patients-over-paperwork-as-specialists-adopt-ai/#:~:text=Specialist%20doctors%20across%20Australia%20and,%E2%80%A6)). The prevailing strategy is to **complement** clinicians: AI aids diagnosis, logistics and administration, enabling doctors and nurses to focus on patient care ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/2022/10/31/ai-in-healthcare-nz-leading-the-world/#:~:text=The%20AI%20Forum%20NZ%20says,artificial%20intelligence%2C%20is%20the%20answer)) ([www.hinz.org.nz](https://www.hinz.org.nz/news/698110/AI-adoption-drives-productivity--new-report.htm#:~:text=that%20trust%2C%E2%80%9D%20he%20says,how%20far%20we%20want%20to)). Success hinges on doing this safely and equitably.  Government and professional bodies emphasize that any AI tool must prove its accuracy and fairness, with accountability mechanisms (e.g. ACC coverage) in place ([www.health.govt.nz](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/precision-health-ai-and-genomics#:~:text=The%20safe%20adoption%20of%20precision,health%20of%20all%20New%20Zealanders)) ([www.hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/news-and-events/new-ai-healthcare-rfp#:~:text=The%20Health%20Research%20Council%20of,quality%20healthcare%20for%20New%20Zealanders)).  

Ongoing investments – such as multi-million-dollar research grants ([www.hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/news-and-events/new-ai-healthcare-rfp#:~:text=The%20Health%20Research%20Council%20of,quality%20healthcare%20for%20New%20Zealanders)) and new health AI networks – show NZ is actively building its AI ecosystem.  Stakeholders from industry, academia, iwi and clinicians are collaborating to navigate this transition. As pilots mature into wider programs, this *Living Whitepaper* will be updated with new data and case studies. Our aim is to keep health leaders informed so they can leverage AI strategically for better outcomes in Aotearoa – while ensuring technology ever serves people.
