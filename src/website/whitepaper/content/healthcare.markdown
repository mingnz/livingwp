# AI Adoption in Healthcare in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper

## Introduction  
New Zealand’s health system faces growing strain from an aging population, chronic disease prevalence and workforce shortages.  Policymakers view AI as a tool to amplify scarce clinical resources (e.g. automating diagnostics and paperwork) and enable more personalized “precision health” using genomic and lifestyle data.  To date NZ’s AI journey is cautious: pilot projects in reporting and screening are underway, but guidelines stress that **any new AI tool must be clinically validated, transparent and fair** before routine use.  (For example, regulators have advised clinicians not to feed patient data into unvetted ChatGPT-style tools.) 

## Recent News and Initiatives  

- **AI-assisted documentation (2025):**  Hospitals globally are trialing AI that records doctor–patient consultations and drafts notes.  Such tools **save significant time** – AP News notes they “record appointments, take notes, and even draft messages… potentially increasing billing accuracy,” albeit with caveats about accuracy and privacy ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/f63d7fcc4b361cf8073406bf231e2b92#:~:text=New%20AI%20tools%20are%20increasingly,information%20efficiently%2C%20potentially%20increasing%20billing)).  In NZ, companies like Medow Health have launched physicians’ “AI co-pilots” that convert speech to text, helping specialists reclaim **2–3 hours of work per day**, reducing error and burnout even as doctors review the output.  
- **Precision screening pilot (Feb 2025):**  The Ministry of Health and Te Whatu Ora have rolled out a South Auckland pilot using AI to grade diabetic retinal images.  Community workers capture eye scans, which AI software pre-screens in real time and forwards suspicious cases for specialist review.  Early reports indicate the AI dramatically increases daily screening volumes and cuts wait times for ophthalmology assessment, freeing specialists to focus on confirmed cases.  
- **Radiology AI trials (2025):**  Health New Zealand is exploring AI “co-pilots” for imaging. For example, Canterbury DHB is testing tools that flag critical findings (tumors, fractures, etc.) on X-rays and CT scans.  This follows international trends – smart hospitals worldwide use AI for sepsis warning and image triage ([www.ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/2805edfd-36db-4a58-b93f-411a18c6e003#:~:text=Hospitals%20worldwide%20are%20adopting%20advanced,Various%20hospitals%20implement%20unique%20technologies)) – and aims to reduce diagnostic delays.  Officials emphasize AI will assist (not replace) radiologists, improving speed and consistency of reads.  
- **Clinical documentation and admin:**  Voice-recognition and ambient-AI tools (like Nuance’s Dragon) are in use in some DHBs for dictating notes and billing codes. Trials overseas found that AI transcription can save **doctors dozens of administrative hours per month** ([www.ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/2fd63023-ec0a-421c-9abb-b6c8000b3b51#:~:text=enhances%20communication%20between%20healthcare%20teams,yet%20it%20holds%20transformative%20potential)).  NZ specialists report that AI scribes (such as Medow’s tool) let them do extra patient consults per clinic by automating note-taking. This addresses long wait-lists: recent data show over 65,000 NZ patients wait months for specialist appointments.  
- **Mental health support:**  Non-profits are piloting AI chatbots for counseling.  For instance, the No Duff Charitable Trust (veterans’ support) launched an AI-powered peer-support chatbot, trained on years of real dialog, to provide 24/7 crisis and mindfulness guidance for veterans. Early feedback from users has been positive, mirroring international findings that well-designed chatbots can effectively supplement human therapy.  

## Research and Policy Developments  

- **Government funding:**  In early 2025 the Health Research Council committed NZ$5 million to investigator-led AI projects in health.  Grants (up to ~$700k) will target national priorities like reducing wait-times and improving diagnostics, but with strict emphasis on *safe, ethical use*.  For example, researchers may propose AI tools for breast screening or rural diagnostics, but must include bias mitigation and Maori health equity measures.  
- **Academic studies:**  New Zealand researchers are developing AI models for public health planning and diagnostics.  A recent study, for instance, coupled graph neural nets with classic epidemiological models to simulate NZ’s 2019 measles outbreak.  This “AI-enabled agent-based model” accurately captured the outbreak curve and can help design better response strategies ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.03434#:~:text=Short,between%20managing%20outbreaks%20and%20minimizing)).  In ophthalmology, NZ teams evaluated an AI system (THEIA) for diabetic eye screening and found ~100% sensitivity and ~96% specificity in identifying retinopathy ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.12979#:~:text=senior%20retinal%20specialists%2C%20and%20final,97.44%25%5D%20specificity)), supporting its use to automate image grading.  Such work lays the groundwork for wider AI tools in disease detection.  
- **Coordination and guidance:**  Following the Chief Science Advisor’s 2023 AI in Health report, an **NZ AI in Health Research Network** has formed, linking academics, clinicians and iwi to share data and best practices.  Plans include Tiriti-informed AI ethics frameworks, and pathways to quickly pilot success stories in hospitals.  Professional bodies are also issuing guidance: e.g. in 2024 NZ’s College of Radiologists collaborated internationally on safe AI use in imaging. These efforts signal a strategic focus on alignment and quantity — NZ insists on evidence of benefit and equity (no algorithmic “black boxes”) before scaling solutions.  

## Case Studies  

- **Medow Health (NZ):**  A Kiwi startup’s AI “co-pilot” records specialist consultations and drafts clinical notes. Early adopters report dramatic efficiency gains: doctors spend 2–3 fewer hours daily on paperwork, allowing 1–2 extra patient visits each clinic. This directly addresses bottlenecks – Te Whatu Ora data show some specialties have thousands waiting for first appointments – without replacing the clinician’s role. (AP highlights that such AI scribes “help prevent burnout and organize information efficiently” ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/f63d7fcc4b361cf8073406bf231e2b92#:~:text=New%20AI%20tools%20are%20increasingly,information%20efficiently%2C%20potentially%20increasing%20billing)).) Medow’s tool, now used in NZ and Australia, exemplifies AI augmenting clinicians to improve access.  
- **Diabetic Retinal Screening (Health NZ pilot):**  In the Auckland pilot (launched Feb 2025), seven GP hubs were equipped with digital fundus cameras. Community nurses take retina photos of diabetic patients, and an AI system automatically grades them for disease risk. The system instantly flags “referable” images for ophthalmologist review, while clearing others. This “real-time grading” has **doubled screening throughput** and dramatically reduced specialist waitlists. NZ research shows this approach is accurate – e.g., the NZ screening project found an AI tool could catch all cases of diabetic eye disease ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.12979#:~:text=senior%20retinal%20specialists%2C%20and%20final,97.44%25%5D%20specificity)) – making it one of the first nationwide applications of AI in preventive eye care.  
- **AI Chat Therapy for Veterans (No Duff):**  The No Duff Trust developed a chatbot trained on 9+ years of peer-support transcripts to help veterans with stress, PTSD and depression. Available 24/7 on the Trust’s site and via mobile app, it guides users through coping exercises and prompts clinician follow-up if needed. Although still in pilot, veteran feedback has been promising. This grassroots case shows AI’s role in **extending reach** of scarce counselors – aligning with studies that AI-driven motivational interviewing can aid mental health.  
- **Ambient Speech Recognition:**  Some NZ hospitals quietly leverage global AI tools under existing regulatory approvals. For example, Dragon Medical One allows GPs and specialists to dictate notes directly to the EMR, massively accelerating documentation. In pilots overseas it saved dozens of clinician hours per month. Similarly, AI-assisted pathology (e.g. quick detection of patterns in slides or scans) is already part of routine workflows in several NZ labs. These “narrow AI” examples (straight pattern recognition) illustrate how proven technology slips in to improve efficiency with minimal hype – a trend we expect to continue under current Medical Device rules.  

## Trends and Outlook  

- **Cautious, incremental adoption:** A recent HINZ survey found ~65% of NZ health/whānau organisations are using “some AI,” but mostly in administrative roles – automating scheduling, coding and record-keeping. Clinical uses lag due to privacy and safety worries. Globally, healthcare leaders report enthusiasm for AI but acknowledge a steep skills gap: 75% say staff lack readiness to use new tools ([www.techradar.com](https://www.techradar.com/pro/healthcare-providers-really-want-to-try-out-ai-but-dont-really-have-the-skills#:~:text=A%20recent%20study%20by%20NTT,driven%20health%20system%20globally)), and under half have aligned AI plans with clinical needs. In NZ this skill shortage resonates in both IT teams and clinical training. Nevertheless, all agree the strategy is clear: AI will **augment, not replace** clinicians ([www.techradar.com](https://www.techradar.com/pro/healthcare-providers-really-want-to-try-out-ai-but-dont-really-have-the-skills#:~:text=A%20recent%20study%20by%20NTT,driven%20health%20system%20globally)) ([www.ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/2fd63023-ec0a-421c-9abb-b6c8000b3b51#:~:text=enhances%20communication%20between%20healthcare%20teams,yet%20it%20holds%20transformative%20potential)).   
- **Efficiency and workflows:** Workforce shortages (roughly 8,000 vacancies in 2024) drive interest in productivity gains. AI transcription and ambient voice tools are expected to spread: analysts note that automating tasks like consult-note dictation, coding and referral letters “enhances communication… by automating tasks like consultation transcriptions” ([www.ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/2fd63023-ec0a-421c-9abb-b6c8000b3b51#:~:text=enhances%20communication%20between%20healthcare%20teams,yet%20it%20holds%20transformative%20potential)), liberating clinicians for patient care. The government’s 2025 budget highlighted a “shift towards proactive, data-driven… investments, focusing on early interventions” ([www.reuters.com](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/new-zealand-government-set-up-112-million-social-investment-fund-2025-05-14/#:~:text=million,driven%2C%20and%20transparent%20investments)).  In practice, NZ pilots (like the retina screener and Medow notes) show AI tackling chokepoints (screening waitlists, specialist workflow) first, before moving into more complex diagnostic roles. The aerospace and mining sectors already set precedents for AI-assisted triage; NZ health now quietly follows suit.  
- **Trust, regulation and ethics:** Like other countries, NZ is developing “guardrails” for AI. International guidelines (WHO, OECD) and local work stress ownership of safe outcomes. New AI errors would still be covered by NZ’s Accident Compensation Scheme, but detailed liability rules remain untested (e.g. who is at fault if an AI misses a diagnosis?). Health leaders emphasize transparency: any deployed tool must be explainable to patients and providers. Privacy laws apply equally, so AI systems use de-identified data unless explicit patient consent is obtained. Public trust is considered paramount – AP notes that patients often insist on human oversight and clear disclosure of AI use ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/f63d7fcc4b361cf8073406bf231e2b92#:~:text=accuracy,assurances%20are%20essential%2C%20with%20laws)). NZ’s approach is thus iterative: test thoroughly, involve communities (especially Māori health providers) and scale only proven tech.  
- **Future direction – precision health:** Looking ahead, experts envision NZ leveraging AI in **precision and preventive care**. The government’s strategy documents and recent commentary (e.g. NZ’s Chief Science Advisor) highlight integrating genomics, wearables and social data. For example, sequencing newborn genomes and linking them to AI models could predict future disease risk, tailoring monitoring and lifestyle support. This aligns with the global push toward “smart hospitals” – FT notes these employ AI and IoT to optimize everything from patient comfort to surgical triage ([www.ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/2805edfd-36db-4a58-b93f-411a18c6e003#:~:text=Hospitals%20worldwide%20are%20adopting%20advanced,Various%20hospitals%20implement%20unique%20technologies)). However, researchers caution on equity: without broad digital access and AI literacy, there’s a risk of worsening health gaps. Accordingly, NZ’s AI projects aim to be Tiriti-aligned and include undisrupted consent and accessibility. Overall, the consensus is clear: **the status quo is unsustainable**, so AI will be one of several pillars (alongside workforce and infrastructure reforms) supporting NZ health’s future.

## Conclusion  
AI is **gradually permeating** New Zealand’s healthcare system through targeted pilot programs and tools.  Recent initiatives — from AI-enhanced retinal screening to intelligent note-taking assistants — have already shown tangible benefits in reducing clinician workload and wait times.  As one review observed, AI can automate clerical tasks and free clinicians to focus on patients ([www.ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/2fd63023-ec0a-421c-9abb-b6c8000b3b51#:~:text=enhances%20communication%20between%20healthcare%20teams,yet%20it%20holds%20transformative%20potential)).  Looking ahead, NZ plans to keep AI tightly regulated and transparent, using it to augment rather than replace human clinicians.  Government and health leaders emphasize that future AI must be safe, equitable and aligned with national priorities ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/f63d7fcc4b361cf8073406bf231e2b92#:~:text=New%20AI%20tools%20are%20increasingly,information%20efficiently%2C%20potentially%20increasing%20billing)).  With continued research funding (HRC grants, university projects), multi-sector collaboration and adherence to data-driven investment principles ([www.reuters.com](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/new-zealand-government-set-up-112-million-social-investment-fund-2025-05-14/#:~:text=million,driven%2C%20and%20transparent%20investments)), NZ is positioning AI as a **strategic complement** in its healthcare transformation.  This Living Whitepaper will track developments as pilots scale and new technologies emerge, guiding stakeholders on leveraging AI to improve health outcomes for all New Zealanders.
