# AI in Creative Industries in Aotearoa New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper  
**Updated: March 31, 2026**

## Introduction

AI adoption across Aotearoa New Zealand’s creative industries has moved beyond scattered experimentation and into a more operational phase. The clearest new evidence since early March 2026 is a fresh Ministry for Culture and Heritage survey showing that **69% of creators use digital tools in their creative process**, and **65% of those creators use generative AI**. At the same time, government strategy, broadcaster rules, screen-funding processes, and privacy/safety guidance are all becoming more explicit about how AI should be used. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/new-zealanders-cultural-participation-2025))

The result is a sector that is adopting AI unevenly but materially: screen, journalism, and commercial creative are the most visible operational users; music remains highly mobilised around rights and remuneration; games and creative tech are scaling fast but with fewer public AI case studies; and cultural storytelling institutions are beginning to use AI for preservation, discoverability, and access. ([nzfilm.co.nz](https://www.nzfilm.co.nz/news/people-first-navigating-ai-our-screen-sector))

## Executive Snapshot

- **The biggest new development is sector-wide creator uptake data.** Published on **March 18, 2026**, Manatū Taonga’s Cultural Participation Survey found that 69% of creators use digital tools, and 65% of those digital-tool users have used generative AI. The most common uses were idea exploration/refinement, producing creative work, and sharing work more widely or accessibly. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/new-zealanders-cultural-participation-2025))
- **Governance is becoming operational, not just aspirational.** The NZ Film Commission’s AI principles are now embedded into 2026 funding and trade documents, while TVNZ published a fuller AI principles document in **March 2026** covering news, content, advertising, disclosure, and commissioned productions. ([nzfilm.co.nz](https://www.nzfilm.co.nz/assets/resources/FEATURE-FILM-DEVELOPMENT-GUIDELINES-DOCUMENTARY-Jan-2026_FINAL.pdf))
- **Journalism remains one of the most advanced but most trust-constrained use cases.** JMAD’s 2026 baseline report shows AI is already used in day-to-day newsroom workflows, but only 8% of New Zealanders are comfortable with news produced mostly by AI with some human oversight. ([jmadresearch.com](https://www.jmadresearch.com/_files/ugd/a95e86_0f7588b42f2b46c5a4bb8e0f76dfbd37.pdf))
- **Commercial creative adoption is normalising fastest.** Marketing bodies are now running AI-focused events and workshops, and the 2026 NZ Marketing Awards added a dedicated AI category, signalling that AI use in marketing is shifting from novelty to judged practice. ([iab.org.nz](https://www.iab.org.nz/all-events/ai-the-future-of-marketing-event?utm_source=openai))
- **Rights, provenance, and cultural integrity are now central strategic issues.** This is most explicit in music, screen, and policy documents that emphasise copyright, consent, remuneration, data sovereignty, Te Tiriti, and Māori cultural integrity. ([apraamcos.co.nz](https://www.apraamcos.co.nz/about/supporting-the-industry/research-papers/aiandmusic?utm_source=openai))
- **Deepfakes and synthetic imagery have become a live regulatory and reputational issue.** In early 2026, the Privacy Commissioner co-signed an international statement on AI-generated imagery, while Netsafe and the Classification Office updated guidance clarifying the harms and legal exposure around deepfakes and objectionable AI-generated content. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/tuhono-connect/statements-media-releases/joint-statement-on-ai-generated-imagery/))

## What Changed Since the March 9, 2026 Edition

- **New population-level adoption evidence:** the Ministry’s 2025 Cultural Participation Survey added, for the first time, questions about creators’ digital-tool and genAI use. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/new-zealanders-cultural-participation-2025))
- **New broadcaster governance:** TVNZ published **Artificial Intelligence Principles & Guidance** on **March 13, 2026**, clarifying acceptable and unacceptable uses in newsroom, content, and advertising workflows. ([corporate.tvnz.co.nz](https://corporate.tvnz.co.nz/assets/Uploads/TVNZ-AI-PRINCIPLES-FINAL.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- **Screen-sector rules hardened further:** 2026 NZFC funding guidelines now require applicants to outline any proposed AI use and address cultural authenticity, creative integrity, transparency, and industry impact. ([nzfilm.co.nz](https://www.nzfilm.co.nz/assets/resources/FEATURE-FILM-DEVELOPMENT-GUIDELINES-DOCUMENTARY-Jan-2026_FINAL.pdf))
- **Commercial adoption signals strengthened:** the Marketing Association introduced a new AI category in the **2026** NZ Marketing Awards, while IAB NZ scheduled a March 2026 event specifically on “The Future of Marketing & AI.” ([marketing.org.nz](https://marketing.org.nz/news/youtube-nz-marketing-awards-2026-call-for-entries-is-open?utm_source=openai))
- **Ecosystem activity continues to scale:** the **AI and Creativity Summit 2026** is scheduled for **May 5–6, 2026** in Auckland and Wellington, its third year, spanning journalism, film, VFX, gaming, design, and creative tech. ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/event/ai-and-creativity-summit-2026/?utm_source=openai))

## Current State of AI Adoption

### 1) Cross-sector baseline

The strongest new cross-sector signal is that AI use is no longer confined to large firms or showcase projects. According to Manatū Taonga’s 2025 survey, most creators who use digital tools are already using generative AI, mainly for efficiency and ideation; among non-users of digital tools, **36%** cited lack of tech skills or knowledge as a barrier. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/new-zealanders-cultural-participation-2025))

In the wider economy, Datacom’s 2025 State of AI Index reported **87%** of New Zealand organisations using AI, but only **12%** had rolled it out across the whole business. A reasonable reading is that creative industries are following the same pattern: broad experimentation, but uneven depth and maturity. That second point is an inference from the national data and sector case studies. ([datacom.com](https://datacom.com/nz/en/solutions/artificial-intelligence/ai-insights/state-of-ai-index-2025?utm_source=openai))

### 2) Screen, film, TV and VFX

The screen sector remains the most structured creative-AI segment in New Zealand. NZFC’s March 2025 AI Guiding Principles set a “people first” direction grounded in human creativity, cultural integrity, ethical responsibility, Te Tiriti, and respect for Indigenous Cultural and Intellectual Property. ([nzfilm.co.nz](https://www.nzfilm.co.nz/news/people-first-navigating-ai-our-screen-sector))

What is new is operationalisation. In NZFC’s 2026 development and production funding guidelines, applicants must now disclose proposed AI use and explain how it will protect **cultural authenticity**, **creative integrity**, **transparency**, and the broader sector. NZFC’s updated terms of trade also explicitly incorporated reference to its AI principles. ([nzfilm.co.nz](https://www.nzfilm.co.nz/assets/resources/FEATURE-FILM-DEVELOPMENT-GUIDELINES-DOCUMENTARY-Jan-2026_FINAL.pdf))

TVNZ’s March 2026 principles show similar maturation. TVNZ says AI is already used in editorial workflows for research, transcription, translation, graphics, images, audio enhancement, recommendations, and advertising operations, but **“we do not generate new editorial content for news using AI.”** It requires human verification, senior editorial review, audience disclosure when AI materially contributes to a story, and written approval for planned genAI use in local commissioned content that may appear on screen. ([corporate.tvnz.co.nz](https://corporate.tvnz.co.nz/assets/Uploads/TVNZ-AI-PRINCIPLES-FINAL.pdf))

Public evidence still shows practical workflow value. RNZ’s August 2025 reporting on Fathom VFX described AI materially reducing time spent on repetitive post-production tasks such as rain removal from footage, while also surfacing fears about reduced team sizes and fewer training pathways for new entrants. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/media-technology/570668/nz-film-tv-industry-weighs-pros-and-cons-of-artificial-intelligence?utm_source=openai))

**Assessment:** Screen has progressed from “principles” to “process.” In Aotearoa, AI in screen is no longer just debated; it is being disclosed, assessed, and governed through funding, commissioning, and editorial systems. ([nzfilm.co.nz](https://www.nzfilm.co.nz/assets/resources/FEATURE-FILM-DEVELOPMENT-GUIDELINES-DOCUMENTARY-Jan-2026_FINAL.pdf))

### 3) Journalism, publishing and media

Journalism remains one of the clearest operational AI segments. JMAD’s **AI, Journalism and News Media in Aotearoa New Zealand** baseline report, published in February 2026, says AI tools are already used in daily newsroom work, with Stuff and NZME experimenting more widely than RNZ and TVNZ. ([openrepository.aut.ac.nz](https://openrepository.aut.ac.nz/items/cbea9155-0239-4658-b706-28039c7b0bb6?utm_source=openai))

The leading named case study remains **Stuff’s Democracy.AI**, which scans council material, surfaces local issues, and drafts story components so journalists can focus on reporting and public-interest angles. Stuff said a two-month Waikato Times pilot covering 11 councils more than doubled digital subscriptions and lifted subscriber net promoter score by 19 points; IAB reported the tool was later rolled out to more regional newsrooms. ([iab.org.nz](https://www.iab.org.nz/members-hub/how-stuff-uses-ai-to-power-local-journalism?utm_source=openai))

But public trust remains the limiting factor. JMAD found only **8%** of New Zealanders are comfortable with news produced mostly by AI with some human oversight, compared with roughly **26%** comfortable with journalism produced mainly by humans with AI assistance. ([jmadresearch.com](https://www.jmadresearch.com/_files/ugd/a95e86_0f7588b42f2b46c5a4bb8e0f76dfbd37.pdf))

That helps explain why public-service broadcasters remain conservative. RNZ’s AI Principles say RNZ will generally not knowingly publish work created by generative AI and that generative AI should not be used to create news, current affairs, or factual journalism except in tightly limited circumstances. TVNZ’s newer policy is slightly more operationally flexible, but still prohibits AI-generated editorial news content and requires human verification and disclosure. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/assets/cms_uploads/000/000/490/AI_Principles.pdf))

**Assessment:** New Zealand journalism has reached “high utility, low trust” AI adoption. The tools are useful; the social licence is conditional. ([jmadresearch.com](https://www.jmadresearch.com/_files/ugd/a95e86_0f7588b42f2b46c5a4bb8e0f76dfbd37.pdf))

### 4) Advertising, marketing and commercial creative

Advertising and marketing continue to look like the fastest-normalising AI users in the creative economy. The latest public survey signal still appears to be 2025 research reported by Campaign Brief NZ, which found **76% of New Zealand marketers** were already using or planning to implement AI tools. ([campaignbrief.co.nz](https://campaignbrief.co.nz/2025/06/11/tbwanew-zealand-launches-tbwai-a-new-practice-bringing-people-product-and-process-together-to-unlock-ais-potential-for-brands/?utm_source=openai))

What has changed in 2026 is the strength of institutional adoption signals. IAB New Zealand’s March 5, 2026 event focused on AI’s impact on marketing, discoverability, measurement, privacy, and brand safety; the Marketing Association is running a dedicated April 2026 “AI Meets Marketing” workshop; and the 2026 NZ Marketing Awards introduced a new AI category to recognise thoughtful, responsible AI use. ([iab.org.nz](https://www.iab.org.nz/all-events/ai-the-future-of-marketing-event?utm_source=openai))

AUT’s advertising research still provides the clearest local qualitative picture of how practitioners think about this shift: agencies saw near-term value in ideation, mockups, and programmatic creativity, but also expected pressure on pricing, more output with fewer resources, and ongoing concerns about bias and workforce effects. ([aut.ac.nz](https://www.aut.ac.nz/news/stories/ai-re-shapes-adverting-creativity-in-nz))

**Assessment:** In commercial creative, the question is no longer whether AI will be used. It is how agencies, in-house teams, and creators retain differentiation when access to tools is becoming universal. That synthesis is supported by the sector’s training, awards, and practitioner research. ([marketing.org.nz](https://marketing.org.nz/news/youtube-nz-marketing-awards-2026-call-for-entries-is-open?utm_source=openai))

### 5) Music

Music remains one of the most engaged but least celebratory AI segments. The strongest public signals are still about rights, consent, transparency, and remuneration rather than productivity case studies. APRA AMCOS and Recorded Music NZ have continued to push for stronger government requirements around AI training disclosure and creator protection. ([nzmusic.org.nz](https://nzmusic.org.nz/music-industry-news/recorded-music-nz-and-apra-amcos-share-joint-call-to-government-on-requirements-for-ai-companies/?utm_source=openai))

The APRA AMCOS survey base remains important: **38%** of respondents had already used AI in music-related work, **54%** agreed AI can support the human creative process, but **82%** worried AI could stop creators making a living, and very large majorities wanted credit, consent, remuneration, and stronger policymaker attention. APRA AMCOS also highlights specific Māori member concerns about cultural integrity. ([apraamcos.co.nz](https://www.apraamcos.co.nz/about/supporting-the-industry/research-papers/aiandmusic?utm_source=openai))

Government cultural analysis continues to treat music as a meaningful adoption domain: Manatū Taonga’s long-term briefing says research across Australia and New Zealand found **50% of New Zealand music companies** had implemented AI technologies. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-06/Long-term-Insights-Briefing-2025.pdf?utm_source=openai))

**Assessment:** Music is adopting AI, but on New Zealand’s public evidence base, it is still framing the debate through creator rights and cultural protection rather than through “AI success story” branding. ([apraamcos.co.nz](https://www.apraamcos.co.nz/about/supporting-the-industry/research-papers/aiandmusic?utm_source=openai))

### 6) Games, interactive media and creative tech

Games remain strategically important but under-documented from an AI case-study perspective. NZGDA’s 2025 annual survey reported **NZ$759.57 million** in industry pre-tax income for 2024/25, **1,418 FTE jobs**, and **95%** of revenue coming from international consumer sales. That makes games one of New Zealand’s most export-driven creative industries. ([nzgda.com](https://www.nzgda.com/blog/new-zealand-game-development-industry-breaks-records?utm_source=openai))

At the ecosystem level, AI Forum’s 2026 summit explicitly includes gaming and creative technology, while Creative HQ is offering an AI Sandbox capability programme and an accelerator for creative-tech founders spanning arts, design, storytelling, music, and software tools. ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/event/ai-and-creativity-summit-2026/?utm_source=openai))

Publicly named local AI deployment examples in games are still much thinner than in journalism or screen. Based on the source base, the best-supported conclusion is that games are high-potential and capability-rich, but not yet highly transparent in public-facing AI use cases. That is an inference, not a claim that adoption is absent. ([nzgda.com](https://www.nzgda.com/blog/new-zealand-game-development-industry-breaks-records?utm_source=openai))

### 7) Cultural storytelling, archives and live creative practice

A noteworthy development is the spread of AI into cultural storytelling and preservation. Manatū Taonga’s 2025 long-term briefing says institutions including **Te Papa** and **Ngā Taonga Sound & Vision** are developing AI tools to catalogue collections into te reo Māori and other underrepresented languages, and cites **Te Hiku Media** using AI to transcribe and document oral histories at risk of being lost. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/culture-digital-age-long-term-insights-briefing-2025?utm_source=openai))

A practical heritage-storytelling example is **PrideNZ’s “A Rather Queer Cuba Street”**, which used AI transcription, AI-assisted quote selection, and AI human avatars to turn archival audio into a richer site-based historical experience. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/events/public-history-talk-making-rather-queer-cuba-street-ai?utm_source=openai))

Live performance is also experimenting publicly. In February 2026, Touch Compass staged **ABILITOPIA**, a disability-led performance work featuring a live AI-enabled robot alongside dancers, framing AI less as automation and more as a question of participation, agency, and identity. ([touchcompass.org.nz](https://www.touchcompass.org.nz/work/?utm_source=openai))

**Assessment:** Outside the more commercial segments, AI is emerging as a tool for access, preservation, and new forms of performance — but always under stronger scrutiny around authenticity, cultural stewardship, and narrative control. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/culture-digital-age-long-term-insights-briefing-2025?utm_source=openai))

## Research and Policy Environment

The government’s key sector framework is still **Amplify**, released in 2025 and explicitly described as a living document. Its Action 3.5 commits government to support the creative and cultural sectors’ uptake of new technology, including the responsible use and development of AI, while addressing misinformation, workforce effects, privacy, IP, and data sovereignty. Amplify also targets growth in the sector’s GDP contribution from **$17.5 billion in 2024** to **$22 billion by 2030**. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/amplify-creative-and-cultural-strategy-new-zealand))

The deeper strategic framing comes from **Culture in the Digital Age – Long-term Insights Briefing 2025**, which argues that by 2040 New Zealand’s cultural system will be deeply intertwined with AI and other emerging technologies. It presents AI as both an opportunity for storytelling, translation, preservation, and productivity, and a risk for cultural misappropriation, distortion of history, entrenched bias, and erosion of trust. It also places Māori-led innovation, kaitiakitanga, and data sovereignty at the centre of future policy responses. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/culture-digital-age-long-term-insights-briefing-2025?utm_source=openai))

In the wider national setting, the government’s first AI Strategy, released on **July 8, 2025**, and its accompanying business guidance take a light-touch, enabling approach intended to support adoption rather than impose heavy ex ante regulation. ([beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-ai-strategy-boost-productivity?utm_source=openai))

## Governance, Rights and Risk

New Zealand’s governance picture is now clearer than it was a year ago, but still distributed across sector bodies and existing law rather than one dedicated creative-AI regime. ([beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-ai-strategy-boost-productivity?utm_source=openai))

Key features of that environment are:

- **Funding and commissioning controls are strengthening.** NZFC requires AI disclosure in funding applications, and TVNZ requires notification and written approval for planned on-screen genAI use in local commissioned content. ([nzfilm.co.nz](https://www.nzfilm.co.nz/assets/resources/FEATURE-FILM-DEVELOPMENT-GUIDELINES-DOCUMENTARY-Jan-2026_FINAL.pdf))
- **Editorial trust rules are explicit.** RNZ and TVNZ both require human verification, with RNZ taking the stricter line against generative AI in news content. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/assets/cms_uploads/000/000/490/AI_Principles.pdf))
- **Content law already applies to AI-generated material.** The Classification Office states that AI-generated content is treated the same as any other content under New Zealand law; what matters is the content itself, not how it was made. ([classificationoffice.govt.nz](https://www.classificationoffice.govt.nz/classification-info/what-we-classify/ai-generated-content/?utm_source=openai))
- **Privacy and deepfake scrutiny is rising.** In February 2026, the Office of the Privacy Commissioner joined an international statement calling for safeguards, transparency, and rapid removal mechanisms around harmful AI-generated imagery. Netsafe’s January 2026 guidance similarly stresses that deepfakes can be actionable under existing New Zealand law and advocates clearer offences for AI-generated intimate imagery. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/tuhono-connect/statements-media-releases/joint-statement-on-ai-generated-imagery/))

## Key Trends

### Trend 1: AI is becoming ordinary creative infrastructure
The strongest March 2026 update is that AI is now visible not just in flagship companies but in creator practice itself. The new Ministry survey makes that shift measurable. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/new-zealanders-cultural-participation-2025))

### Trend 2: Workflow augmentation is still advancing faster than fully synthetic content
The most credible New Zealand use cases remain transcription, summarisation, document analysis, audio cleanup, recommendation, ideation, archival surfacing, and VFX/post-production acceleration. ([iab.org.nz](https://www.iab.org.nz/members-hub/how-stuff-uses-ai-to-power-local-journalism?utm_source=openai))

### Trend 3: Governance is moving into forms, contracts, and approval gates
The most important shift in 2026 is procedural: AI is being built into funding criteria, broadcaster rules, commissioning approvals, and disclosure expectations. ([nzfilm.co.nz](https://www.nzfilm.co.nz/assets/resources/FEATURE-FILM-DEVELOPMENT-GUIDELINES-DOCUMENTARY-Jan-2026_FINAL.pdf))

### Trend 4: Trust and provenance are separating the leaders from the laggards
High-utility AI use is acceptable when provenance, consent, and human oversight are clear; it is much less acceptable when audiences fear deception, plagiarism, or synthetic substitution. Journalism and music show this most clearly. ([jmadresearch.com](https://www.jmadresearch.com/_files/ugd/a95e86_0f7588b42f2b46c5a4bb8e0f76dfbd37.pdf))

### Trend 5: Māori cultural integrity and data sovereignty are not peripheral issues
Across NZFC, Amplify, APRA AMCOS, and the LTIB, responsible AI in Aotearoa is increasingly defined through Te Tiriti, mātauranga Māori, cultural IP, and sovereignty over data and stories. ([nzfilm.co.nz](https://www.nzfilm.co.nz/news/people-first-navigating-ai-our-screen-sector))

## Conclusion

As of **March 31, 2026**, AI adoption in Aotearoa New Zealand’s creative industries is best described as **real, mainstreaming, and increasingly governed**. The most important new evidence is not a single corporate pilot, but a sector-level signal: a majority of New Zealand creators who already use digital tools are now also using generative AI. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/new-zealanders-cultural-participation-2025))

The current state of play is uneven:

- **Screen and journalism** have the clearest governance and workflow evidence. ([nzfilm.co.nz](https://www.nzfilm.co.nz/assets/resources/FEATURE-FILM-DEVELOPMENT-GUIDELINES-DOCUMENTARY-Jan-2026_FINAL.pdf))
- **Advertising and marketing** are normalising AI fastest in commercial practice. ([iab.org.nz](https://www.iab.org.nz/all-events/ai-the-future-of-marketing-event?utm_source=openai))
- **Music** is highly engaged, but through a rights-and-remuneration lens. ([apraamcos.co.nz](https://www.apraamcos.co.nz/about/supporting-the-industry/research-papers/aiandmusic?utm_source=openai))
- **Games and creative tech** are growing quickly, but public AI case studies remain relatively thin. ([nzgda.com](https://www.nzgda.com/blog/new-zealand-game-development-industry-breaks-records?utm_source=openai))
- **Heritage and live creative practice** are emerging as important, culturally sensitive AI domains. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/culture-digital-age-long-term-insights-briefing-2025?utm_source=openai))

The decisive battlegrounds for the next phase are now clear: **capability, rights, disclosure, trust, and cultural integrity**. In the New Zealand context, the organisations most likely to lead sustainably will be those that combine productivity gains with transparent practice, human oversight, and a distinctly Aotearoa approach to culture, consent, and stewardship. ([mch.govt.nz](https://www.mch.govt.nz/publications/amplify-creative-and-cultural-strategy-new-zealand))
