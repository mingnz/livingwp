# AI Adoption in Education in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper  
**Updated: 8 March 2026**

## Executive Summary

AI in New Zealand education has moved from novelty and cheating anxiety to structured adoption. In schools, the Ministry of Education now has detailed guidance covering privacy, bias, assessment authenticity, age limits, and the use of AI in marking. In national assessment, NZQA is already using automated text scoring for writing assessments and GenAI-enhanced student support. In tertiary education, universities are shifting toward explicit governance, disclosure rules, AI literacy, and institution-specific support tools rather than blanket prohibition. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai?utm_source=openai))

The strongest evidence of adoption is on the teacher side. OECD TALIS 2024 found that 69% of New Zealand teachers reported using AI in their work, far above the OECD average of 36%. Teachers most often used AI for lesson planning or activities (75%), summarising topics (74%), and drafting feedback or parent communications (52%). At the same time, capability remains uneven: among teachers not using AI, 71% said they lacked the knowledge and skills to teach with it. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html?utm_source=openai))

New Zealand research shows the same pattern: use is growing faster than system support. NZCER’s 2024 national primary teacher survey found 46% of teachers were already using AI in teaching, and 53% identified AI as a future professional learning priority. A separate NZCER AI-focused study found teachers and students were already using GenAI in primary settings, but school policy, premium-tool access, and guidance were lagging behind. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/Primary%20school%20teachers%E2%80%99%20perspectives%20from%20the%202024%20National%20Survey%20Uiuinga%20%C4%81-Motu%20o%20ng%C4%81%20Kura%20%5BFull%20report%5D.pdf))

The overall picture is that New Zealand is becoming an early, practical adopter of AI in education, but not yet a fully coordinated one. The system is ahead in some operational areas, especially assessment infrastructure and teacher experimentation, and behind in others, especially consistent curriculum integration, equity of access, and sector-wide capability building. That is an inference from current policy, survey, and case-study evidence. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

## Introduction

This update focuses on verified developments through **8 March 2026**. Compared with the earlier version, the clearest shift is that AI in education is now showing up in three layers at once:  
- **classroom practice** through teacher use and school policies,  
- **system operations** through NZQA marking and support services, and  
- **institutional governance** through university AI policies, academic-integrity frameworks, and student support assistants. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai?utm_source=openai))

## 1) Current State of AI Adoption

### A. Schools: from experimentation to guarded normalisation

The Ministry of Education’s current GenAI guidance tells schools to check outputs, avoid entering personal data, account for cultural bias, build school AI policies, and keep human teachers central. It also states that NCEA external assessments do **not** permit GenAI use, and that school authenticity policies must cover acceptable AI use. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai?utm_source=openai))

The Ministry has also updated guidance on **AI tools for marking**, built around three principles: teacher responsibility and professional judgement, human oversight, and appropriate use cases. The Ministry explicitly says AI marking should support, not replace, teachers’ judgement. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai?utm_source=openai))

### B. Assessment: New Zealand is unusually advanced operationally

NZQA says it used automated text scoring on more than **55,000 Writing assessments in May 2025**, with human check-marking for quality assurance. NZQA also reported that in 2024 it tested the AI tool on **36,000 writing samples** and found it was as reliable as human markers, and that use of the tool allowed results to be returned **3.5 weeks earlier** than the prior year. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/nzqas-responsible-use-of-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

This is a major differentiator in the New Zealand context: AI is not only a classroom tool here, but already part of national assessment operations. RNZ reported the Minister describing NZ’s use of digital exams and AI marking as unusually advanced internationally. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

### C. Tertiary education: governance is catching up fast

Universities are increasingly codifying AI rather than treating it as a temporary exception. AUT says it is weaving AI into learning and teaching across disciplines, changing assessment design, and building student and staff capability. Massey has a formal GenAI-in-assessment policy based on allowed-use levels and disclosure requirements. Otago has adopted a university-wide AI governance policy covering risk assessment, Te Tiriti obligations, data sovereignty, and human oversight. Waikato has embedded AI into academic-integrity modules and staff guidance. ([aut.ac.nz](https://www.aut.ac.nz/about/ai-at-aut/ai-in-learning-and-teaching))

## 2) Policy and Frameworks

### Ministry of Education guidance

The Ministry’s current guidance is now fairly mature and practical. It tells schools to:  
- **not use personal data** in AI tools, because prompts may be reused,  
- warn staff and students about **bias and inaccurate information**,  
- include GenAI in school **assessment authenticity policies**,  
- keep **teachers central**, and  
- avoid relying on AI to mark work without strong human oversight. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

The Ministry also points schools to the Office of the Privacy Commissioner’s AI expectations, reinforcing that privacy law applies to AI use in New Zealand. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### Privacy and data governance

The Privacy Commissioner’s updated AI guidance says agencies should obtain senior leadership approval, assess necessity and proportionality, conduct privacy impact assessments, be transparent, engage with Māori on risks to taonga information, ensure human review, and prevent improper retention or disclosure of personal information. That matters directly for schools and tertiary providers handling student data. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/assets/New-order/Resources-/Publications/Guidance-resources/AI-Guidance-Resources-/AI-and-the-Information-Privacy-Principles.pdf?utm_source=openai))

### Curriculum and national policy direction

At the school-curriculum level, the Ministry is currently consulting on **Years 0–10 draft curriculum content**, with consultation open until **24 April 2026** and final curriculum release planned for **mid-2026**. The implementation timeline says Years 9–10 learning areas will be used from **Term 1, 2027**, while Years 0–8 Technology content is slated for **2028**. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/have-your-say/consultation-year-0-10-draft-curriculum-content/details))

Separately, in September 2025 the Government said it wanted a stronger AI emphasis in senior secondary subjects and asked the Ministry to investigate a new **Year 13 specialist subject on generative AI**. As of 8 March 2026, that remains an announced direction for later development, not a fully implemented subject. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/572844/professor-calls-for-government-to-begin-ai-education-earlier))

### Tertiary governance and academic integrity

- **Massey University** allows AI only within assessment-specific rules, requires disclosure of AI use, and frames AI as support for student work rather than a substitute for it. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))
- **University of Otago** now has a formal AI governance policy covering responsible oversight, Te Tiriti obligations, data sovereignty, bias, privacy, security, and approval pathways for higher-risk use. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/administration/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy))
- **University of Waikato** has redeveloped academic-integrity modules to include AI in assessment and student research, requires annual completion, and is developing staff guidelines for teaching, curriculum design, and assessment in the AI era. ([waikato.ac.nz](https://www.waikato.ac.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Governance-and-reporting/Academic-Audit/Waikato-Cycle-6-Audit-1-year-Follow-Up.pdf))

## 3) Latest News and Developments

### NZQA is scaling operational AI use

NZQA’s current public position is “human at the helm.” It says it is already using or testing:  
- an AI-powered website chatbot,  
- document recognition for international qualification assessments,  
- automated text scoring for writing, and  
- possible future tools for detecting exam-rule breaches and supporting moderation. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/nzqas-responsible-use-of-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

NZQA’s student-facing chatbot **Awhina** has been in place since 2023, and NZQA says it began rolling out a GenAI upgrade in **April 2025** so it could interpret a wider range of questions using public website content. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/our-website-chatbot-awhina-is-growing/?utm_source=openai))

### AI literacy activity is broadening outside formal curriculum reform

A notable near-term development is **Day of AI Aotearoa**. According to EdTechNZ, after a Term 4 pilot across ten schools that was independently evaluated by NZCER, the programme is scheduled to roll out nationally on **9 March 2026**. Since today is **8 March 2026**, that rollout is effectively imminent, not yet completed. ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2026/02/16/redefining-the-purpose-of-education-in-the-age-of-ai-and-why-the-teacher-matters-more-than-ever/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a broader ecosystem effort around educator collaboration. AI Forum-linked education activity includes a growing AI in Education community of practice, and the Education Review Office has an active inquiry into how schools and students are using AI. ([nztech.org.nz](https://nztech.org.nz/2024/08/20/join-the-ai-in-education-community/?utm_source=openai))

### Universities are building internal AI infrastructure, not just rules

AUT’s internal reporting shows a move beyond policy into platform-building: staff GenAI training modules, an **AUTGPT v2.0** pilot as an in-house alternative to commercial tools, work on offline AI transcription for privacy and **Māori data sovereignty**, and testing of AI features in Canvas such as rubric generation and chat agents. ([aut.ac.nz](https://www.aut.ac.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0017/1036061/Part-A-Papers-Open-28-July-2025.pdf))

The University of Auckland launched the **UoA Assistant** in 2024 as a GenAI-powered chatbot drawing on university sites, course outlines, and AskAuckland to support students. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/students/student-support/uoa-assistant.html))

## 4) Research Overview

### OECD TALIS 2024: New Zealand teachers are heavy AI users

TALIS 2024 is the strongest comparative datapoint available. For New Zealand teachers:  
- **69%** reported using AI in their work, versus **36%** across the OECD average.  
- Among users, top uses were **lesson plans/activities (75%)**, **summarising topics (74%)**, and **student feedback/parent communications (52%)**.  
- Lower-use cases were **assessing or marking student work (14%)** and reviewing student-participation or performance data (**10%**).  
- Among non-users, **71%** said they lacked the knowledge and skills to teach using AI. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html?utm_source=openai))

This suggests New Zealand teachers are relatively willing to use AI for preparation and communication, but still cautious about handing over assessment judgement. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html?utm_source=openai))

### NZCER national teacher survey: AI is mainstream enough to become a top PLD priority

NZCER’s 2024 national survey of primary teachers found:  
- **46%** of responding teachers were using AI in teaching,  
- among AI users, the most common use was developing learning materials (**85%**),  
- **36%** of non-users cited data privacy and security concerns, and  
- **53%** of teachers named AI as a future professional-learning priority. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/Primary%20school%20teachers%E2%80%99%20perspectives%20from%20the%202024%20National%20Survey%20Uiuinga%20%C4%81-Motu%20o%20ng%C4%81%20Kura%20%5BFull%20report%5D.pdf))

That is an important signal: AI is no longer a fringe interest area. It has become a mainstream capability gap. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/Primary%20school%20teachers%E2%80%99%20perspectives%20from%20the%202024%20National%20Survey%20Uiuinga%20%C4%81-Motu%20o%20ng%C4%81%20Kura%20%5BFull%20report%5D.pdf))

### NZCER AI in primary schools study: student use is already present, but guidance is thin

NZCER’s 2025 AI in primary schools report used responses from **266 primary teachers** and **147 students** across four case-study schools. It found that teachers were using AI for lesson planning, assessment design, and personalising materials; most relied on free tools; three-quarters lacked school-funded premium access; and **85%** wanted more training. On the student side, roughly nine in 10 had heard of AI and more than half had used GenAI, mostly outside school; many were unclear on school rules and did not recall adult guidance on use. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools))

This is one of the clearest warnings in the current evidence base: student adoption is outrunning formal guidance, especially in primary and intermediate settings. That is an inference from NZCER’s reported student awareness/use and low recall of adult guidance. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools))

## 5) Case Studies

### Aotea College: school-wide governance first

The Ministry’s March 2025 case study on **Aotea College** shows a practical governance model: a staff AI group, updated authenticity statements, staff handbook guidance, professional learning, classroom discussions with students, and explicit rules around referencing GenAI in assessment. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Aotea%20College_MAR2025.pdf))

**Why it matters:** this is a model of AI adoption as policy and pedagogy change, not just tool adoption. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Aotea%20College_MAR2025.pdf))

### Hobsonville Point Secondary School: authenticity through checkpoints, not retreat to pen-and-paper

The Ministry’s **Hobsonville Point Secondary School** case study says the school responded to plagiarism concerns by building an AI strategy aligned to school values, updating assessment policy, requiring checkpoints, and avoiding a blanket return to pen-and-paper because that would undermine inclusive assessment design. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Hobsonville%20Point%20Secondary%20School_MAR2025.pdf?VersionId=.9dochWl4SsdLrmFkwawNSFNaw_SoYAW&utm_source=openai))

**Why it matters:** it shows one likely NZ direction of travel—assessment redesign and staged evidence collection instead of trying to “ban AI back into the bottle.” ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Hobsonville%20Point%20Secondary%20School_MAR2025.pdf?VersionId=.9dochWl4SsdLrmFkwawNSFNaw_SoYAW&utm_source=openai))

### Toi Ohomai Institute of Technology: course-specific chatbots for 24/7 support

Toi Ohomai reports that chatbots embedded in its LMS can provide extra information, generate quizzes from kaiako resources, reword content, answer questions **24/7**, and reduce teacher workload outside class hours. It also describes a postgraduate student using a customised chatbot for feedback on course work. ([toiohomai.ac.nz](https://www.toiohomai.ac.nz/life/news/ai-chatbots-enhance-learning))

**Why it matters:** this is a concrete example of AI as a learning-support layer rather than a grading engine. ([toiohomai.ac.nz](https://www.toiohomai.ac.nz/life/news/ai-chatbots-enhance-learning))

### University of Auckland: AI-enabled student support

The **UoA Assistant** gives students GenAI-powered answers using university web content, course outlines, and AskAuckland resources. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/students/student-support/uoa-assistant.html))

**Why it matters:** tertiary AI adoption in New Zealand is not confined to teaching and assessment; it is also reshaping front-line student services. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/students/student-support/uoa-assistant.html))

### AUT: institution-wide AI integration

AUT says its goal is for all graduates to be confident in an AI-integrated workforce. Its AI Taskforce report describes multilingual 24-hour AI support, academic learning-support agents, free access to required AI resources for students, and AI literacy training for all students. ([aut.ac.nz](https://www.aut.ac.nz/about/ai-at-aut/ai-in-learning-and-teaching))

**Why it matters:** AUT is one of the clearest NZ examples of AI moving from isolated pilots to institution-level strategy. ([aut.ac.nz](https://www.aut.ac.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0003/1082091/AI-Taskforce-Report-Our-AI-Future-FINAL-1.pdf))

### NZQA: AI in assessment and student enquiry handling

NZQA is using AI both in assessment operations and learner support. Its public materials point to automated text scoring in writing assessment and GenAI upgrades to Awhina for better student support. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

**Why it matters:** this is the most system-level, scaled deployment of AI in New Zealand education at present. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

## 6) Key Trends

### 1. Teacher workflow augmentation is now the dominant use case
The evidence consistently shows teachers are using AI most for planning, drafting, summarising, and creating materials, not for final judgement tasks like grading. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html?utm_source=openai))

### 2. Assessment is the highest-governance frontier
New Zealand is experimenting boldly with AI in assessment infrastructure, but policy language remains cautious and human-centred. Schools must maintain authenticity; external NCEA assessments do not allow GenAI; and Ministry guidance says AI marking must support, not replace, teachers. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai?utm_source=openai))

### 3. Tertiary institutions are shifting from detection to disclosure and design
Massey’s framework levels, Waikato’s AI-integrity modules, Otago’s governance policy, and AUT’s capability-building all point the same way: less reliance on “detect AI” thinking, and more emphasis on permitted-use models, evidence trails, redesign of assessment, and institutional governance. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))

### 4. Māori data sovereignty and cultural appropriateness are becoming core, not peripheral
The Ministry warns that mainstream AI tools are weak on mātauranga and te reo Māori. The Privacy Commissioner explicitly tells agencies to engage with Māori on risks to taonga information. AUT and Otago both embed Māori data sovereignty in AI governance. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai?utm_source=openai))

### 5. AI literacy is the next big gap
The strongest bottleneck is no longer awareness, but capability. Teachers are using AI, students are already encountering it, and institutions are responding—but professional learning, policy clarity, and safe access to better tools are still uneven. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html?utm_source=openai))

## 7) Strategic Implications for Stakeholders

### For school leaders
- Treat AI as a **whole-school governance issue**, not just an ICT issue. Use explicit policy, authenticity rules, and staff PLD. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai?utm_source=openai))
- Prioritise **privacy and due diligence** when selecting tools, especially where student data is involved. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/what-consider-when-selecting-and-onboarding-new-software))

### For teachers
- Near-term value is highest in **planning, differentiation, drafting, and feedback support**, not unsupervised marking. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html?utm_source=openai))
- The evidence base suggests AI literacy should include disclosure, source-checking, prompt judgement, bias awareness, and knowing when **not** to use AI. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai?utm_source=openai))

### For tertiary institutions
- The sector is moving toward **frameworked permission**, not binary yes/no rules. Clear disclosure requirements, assessment redesign, and risk-governed internal tools appear to be best practice in the NZ context. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))

### For policymakers
- New Zealand has credible AI activity in education, but the current pattern is still somewhat fragmented. A stronger national approach would likely connect curriculum, AI literacy, privacy, teacher PLD, and equity more explicitly. That is an inference from the coexistence of strong local pilots, growing teacher use, and ongoing curriculum consultation. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html?utm_source=openai))

## Conclusion

As of **8 March 2026**, AI adoption in New Zealand education is real, broadening, and increasingly institutionalised. The sector is no longer asking whether AI is present; it is deciding where AI belongs, under what rules, and with what safeguards. The strongest current uses are teacher workflow support, student support services, and assessment operations. The weakest areas remain consistency, AI literacy, and equitable, culturally grounded implementation. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html?utm_source=openai))
