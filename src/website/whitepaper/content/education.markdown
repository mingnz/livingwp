# AI in Education in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper  
*Updated for publication on 31 March 2026*

## Executive Snapshot

New Zealand’s education sector is now in a **governed adoption** phase of AI rather than a purely experimental one. In schools, AI use is most established in lesson planning, resource creation, feedback, and student-facing literacy work. In tertiary education, the leading edge has shifted toward assessment redesign, institutional rules for acceptable AI use, and secure enterprise tools. The most mature production deployment remains at system level, where NZQA has already used AI at national scale for writing assessment marking and continues to expand AI-assisted service delivery. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/teaching-and-learning-international-survey/talis-2024-publications/talis-2024-first-report))

Since the previous update on **9 March 2026**, the most important changes are not flashy new classroom pilots but stronger infrastructure around AI: the Privacy Commissioner launched new **education-sector privacy guidance** on **11 March 2026**; the Ministry’s school-facing **Generative AI** guidance was updated on **16 March 2026**; and on **18 March 2026** the Ministry highlighted both the launch of the **Safer Technologies for Schools (ST4S) catalogue** and the new privacy guidance as tools for safer digital and AI adoption. At tertiary level, the University of Otago approved a formal **AI Governance Policy** on **10 March 2026**, signalling a sharper move from local experimentation to institution-wide governance. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/tuhono-connect/statements-media-releases/education-sector-guidance-will-help-frontline-workers/?utm_source=openai))

The overall picture is that AI adoption in New Zealand education is now **active, uneven, and hardening into policy**. The sector has moved past the first-wave “AI as cheating problem” framing, but it has not yet reached a settled national operating model. Instead, it is building one through policy, curriculum revision, privacy controls, AI literacy initiatives, and carefully bounded operational deployments. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai))

## Overall State of Adoption

### Schools: widespread use, but still uneven and teacher-led

The strongest national benchmark remains **TALIS 2024**. It shows that **69% of New Zealand Year 7–10 teachers** had used AI, compared with **36%** of lower-secondary teachers across the OECD. Among New Zealand teachers who had used AI, the most common uses were **generating lesson plans (78%)** and **learning about or summarising a topic (73%)**; the least common were **reviewing student performance data (10%)** and **assessing student work (12%)**. This confirms that AI use is strongest where teachers retain clear oversight and where the stakes are lower. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/teaching-and-learning-international-survey/talis-2024-publications/talis-2024-first-report))

Primary-sector evidence points in the same direction, while also showing more variation. NZCER’s 2024 national survey found that **46% of primary teachers** reported using AI tools in teaching, most commonly to develop learning materials, while **74% of non-users** said lack of AI knowledge and training was the main barrier. NZCER’s separate AI-in-primary-schools study found broad experimentation among an AI-interested teacher cohort, but also that **fewer than half felt confident teaching responsible AI use** and **85% wanted more training**. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/Primary%20school%20teachers%E2%80%99%20perspectives%20from%20the%202024%20National%20Survey%20Uiuinga%20%C4%81-Motu%20o%20ng%C4%81%20Kura%20%5BExecutive%20Summary%5D.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Leadership is moving, but capability is still catching up. NZCER reports that **two-thirds of primary principals** were already having conversations about AI, and **56%** identified a need for AI-related professional learning. The Ministry has also flagged that ERO is actively surveying schools about how teachers, leaders, and students are using AI, which suggests system monitoring is still trying to catch up with practice on the ground. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/national-survey-2024-primary-principals?utm_source=openai))

### Tertiary: governance and assessment redesign are becoming more formal

The tertiary sector is no longer just reacting to student misuse; it is building operating models. Massey University’s **Generative Artificial Intelligence in Assessment Policy**, effective from **May 2025**, sets four explicit AI use levels through the **Artificial Intelligence Use Framework**, requires students to keep records of tools and prompts, and makes clear that AI-generated content cannot simply be submitted as a student’s own work. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))

The University of Otago’s **AI Governance Policy**, approved on **10 March 2026**, goes further by creating a university-wide governance structure. It explicitly requires AI use to align with Te Tiriti obligations, protect data sovereignty, manage bias and privacy risks, and remain transparent and explainable. In parallel, Otago’s teaching guidance says the university is taking a balanced approach that supports innovation while leaving room for discipline-specific implementation. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/humanresources/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy))

Operational deployment is also growing in tertiary settings. The University of Auckland says its **AI Assistant** now handles **more than 9,000 conversations and 60,000 searches each month**, and the university provides staff and students with institutionally managed GenAI tools rather than leaving use entirely to public consumer tools. That is a sign that tertiary adoption is maturing from ad hoc individual use to managed service infrastructure. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/10/07/ai-assistant-enhances-digital-experience-at-uoa.html?utm_source=openai))

### System agencies: still the most mature and consequential AI users

NZQA remains the clearest example of AI in real production use. It says its **Automated Text Scoring** system marked **more than 55,000 writing literacy assessments** in **May 2025**, with results returned **3.5 weeks earlier** than the previous year, after a 2024 test on **36,000 writing samples** found the system as reliable as human markers. NZQA also says experienced human markers double-checked more than a third of the 2025 results, especially around achievement boundaries. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

NZQA is also using AI for customer service. Its chatbot **Awhina**, first launched in 2023, began a **GenAI upgrade in April 2025** to search NZQA’s public website and generate clearer answers, with safeguards to protect personal information and prevent learning from personally identifiable input. NZQA’s 2024/25 annual report describes the generative AI-powered chatbot as part of a wider digital-services strategy. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/our-website-chatbot-awhina-is-growing/?utm_source=openai))

## Current News and Developments

### 1) Privacy has become a front-line AI issue

The most meaningful March development is the privacy stack now sitting around AI use in education. On **11 March 2026**, the Privacy Commissioner launched new guidance for the education sector, including a chapter on **digital technologies** and broader guidance on protecting children and young people’s information. On **18 March 2026**, the Ministry amplified that guidance and told schools to use it when making decisions about digital tools. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/tuhono-connect/statements-media-releases/education-sector-guidance-will-help-frontline-workers/?utm_source=openai))

This matters because the Ministry also highlighted the **ST4S catalogue**, which gives schools a more structured way to assess privacy and security risks in digital products. The Ministry’s ST4S page explicitly notes that reports include information about student safety features, including **AI features**, and practical recommendations to reduce risk. AI adoption in schools is therefore becoming less about “which chatbot is interesting” and more about **which products can be safely procured and governed**. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/bulletins/digital-download/18-03-26))

### 2) The Ministry’s school guidance is now more operational

The Ministry’s **Generative AI** guidance, last updated on **16 March 2026**, continues to push a principles-based model: don’t enter personal data, check outputs, recognise cultural bias, keep teachers central, and ensure schools have policies covering purpose, scope, privacy, risk mitigation, and professional development. The guidance also reiterates that **GenAI is not permitted in NCEA external assessment** and that schools with consent to assess must have an authenticity policy that includes acceptable GenAI use. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai))

The Ministry’s updated marking guidance is also clear that AI for marking should be governed by **teacher responsibility**, **human oversight**, and **appropriate use cases**. In other words, the official direction is not “ban AI” or “automate teaching,” but **use AI where it can assist while preserving professional judgement and legal accountability**. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### 3) AI literacy has moved into live rollout, not just advocacy

The Day of AI Aotearoa hub says its goal is to ensure **every school, every teacher, and every learner in Aotearoa** can access free foundational AI literacy by the end of **Term 1 2026**. RNZ reported on **13 March 2026** that the programme was being rolled out nationally that week, with local adaptation including a te ao Māori perspective. ([dayofai.org](https://dayofai.org/program-hubs/new-zealand-aotearoa?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, Kōtui Ako’s nationwide **AI Digital Literacy** offering began classes on **9 March 2026**, covering AI fundamentals, chatbots, bias, algorithms, facial recognition, and plagiarism. The significance here is systemic: AI literacy is no longer confined to isolated innovators. It is increasingly being packaged as a mainstream capability for teachers and learners. ([gazette.education.govt.nz](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/notices/1HAqHJ-ai-digital-literacy-online/?utm_source=openai))

### 4) Curriculum architecture now gives AI a clearer place

Consultation on the draft **Years 0–10 curriculum content** remains open until **24 April 2026**, with final release targeted for **mid-2026** and broad implementation beginning from **2027**. In the draft Technology learning area, the **Years 9–10 Phase 4** content includes explicit AI-related learning in “futures literacies,” such as understanding how intelligent systems work, recognising bias and misinformation risks, evaluating fairness and accuracy, and applying AI tools in simple applications. The current Technology curriculum remains in force until **1 January 2028**. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/have-your-say/consultation-year-0-10-draft-curriculum-content/details?utm_source=openai))

This is important because it shifts AI from a side conversation about cheating into the formal architecture of what schools are expected to teach. New Zealand is beginning to institutionalise AI as both a **technical topic** and a **social-ethical literacy** topic. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/have-your-say/consultation-year-0-10-draft-curriculum-content/details?utm_source=openai))

## Research Overview

### TALIS 2024: New Zealand teachers are ahead on use, but still need support

TALIS shows New Zealand teachers are ahead of OECD peers in AI use and AI-related professional learning, especially at Years 9–10. But the same data also show strong ongoing demand for AI PLD: **63% of secondary teachers** and **53% of primary teachers** reported needing professional learning on using AI for teaching and learning. This suggests the key constraint is no longer awareness; it is **capability, confidence, and implementation quality**. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/teaching-and-learning-international-survey/talis-2024-publications/talis-2024-first-report))

### NZCER: primary schools are using AI, but support is thin

NZCER’s nationally representative primary teacher survey found that AI use is already meaningful, with almost half of teachers reporting classroom use and **53%** identifying AI as a priority area for future PLD. The same survey shows that practical adoption is outrunning formal support structures. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/Primary%20school%20teachers%E2%80%99%20perspectives%20from%20the%202024%20National%20Survey%20Uiuinga%20%C4%81-Motu%20o%20ng%C4%81%20Kura%20%5BExecutive%20Summary%5D.pdf?utm_source=openai))

NZCER’s AI-in-primary-schools report adds a deeper warning: teachers were often relying on free public chatbots, many were not confident teaching responsible AI use, and the report called for **privacy-protected access to better tools**, stronger policy, and age-appropriate AI critical literacy in the curriculum. That is a strong signal that adoption is happening, but not yet on fully equitable or well-supported terms. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools?utm_source=openai))

### Governance readiness is improving faster than classroom consistency

Recent evidence across Ministry, Privacy Commissioner, NZCER, and university sources points to a common pattern: institutions are getting more serious about governance, but actual practice remains uneven. Schools and tertiary providers increasingly have rules, frameworks, or procurement guidance, yet staff capability, confidence, and consistency still vary sharply by institution and by individual educator. That gap between **policy maturity** and **practice maturity** is one of the defining features of the current moment. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai))

## Case Studies

### 1) NZQA: AI in national assessment operations

NZQA is still the most important case study because it shows AI operating in a high-trust, high-volume, nationally significant workflow. Automated Text Scoring has already been used at scale for writing assessments, with human quality assurance layered on top. That makes NZQA less a pilot story than a case of **controlled production deployment**. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

### 2) New Windsor School and the Ako Hiko cluster: primary-sector implementation

Education Gazette’s profile of New Windsor School shows what school-level AI adoption looks like when it is deliberately integrated into pedagogy rather than bolted on. Students are using tools such as **Brisk**, **Adobe Express**, and image-generation tools to support writing, creativity, and critical engagement; staff capability has been supported through cluster PLD and AI micro-credential work. The school is also exploring how the approach can scale across other local schools. ([gazette.education.govt.nz](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/articles/ai-approach-at-new-windsor-school-prepares-akonga-for-the-future/?utm_source=openai))

### 3) Aotea College and Hobsonville Point Secondary School: assessment authenticity redesign

The Ministry’s school case studies continue to matter because they model a practical response to AI-enabled misconduct without relying on weak detectors. Aotea College built a school-wide approach including authenticity statements, staff PLD, referencing guidance, and verbal questioning; Hobsonville Point built checkpoints, version-history expectations, standard templates, and restorative processes. These are still among the clearest New Zealand examples of **assessment redesign replacing detector-led enforcement**. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### 4) University of Auckland: AI as service infrastructure and culturally grounded learning innovation

The University of Auckland’s AI Assistant shows how universities are using AI as a student-service layer at scale, while the university’s secure GenAI tool access shows a move toward safer institutional provision rather than unmanaged reliance on public tools. Separately, Auckland research teams are developing AI tools to support **te reo Māori pronunciation** and other Māori-language technologies, showing that in New Zealand, some of the most interesting educational AI work sits at the intersection of pedagogy, language revitalisation, and Indigenous data concerns. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/10/07/ai-assistant-enhances-digital-experience-at-uoa.html?utm_source=openai))

### 5) University of Otago: from guidance to institution-wide governance

Otago’s March 2026 AI Governance Policy is a useful case study not because it is a classroom deployment, but because it shows what institutional maturity looks like. The university has moved from local teaching guidance to a formal governance framework with committee oversight, explicit risk categories, cultural obligations, and review mechanisms. That is likely a preview of where more tertiary providers will head next. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/humanresources/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy))

## Emerging Trends

### 1) Augmentation still dominates substitution

Across the evidence base, AI is being used most successfully where it **augments** human work: planning, drafting, summarising, tutoring, service support, and targeted feedback. Full substitution of teacher or assessor judgement remains rare, tightly controlled, or contested. Even NZQA’s most advanced deployment keeps humans in the loop for quality assurance. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/teaching-and-learning-international-survey/talis-2024-publications/talis-2024-first-report))

### 2) Privacy, procurement, and safety are moving to the center

The big March shift is that AI adoption is increasingly being governed through **privacy guidance**, **procurement due diligence**, and **tool-risk evaluation**, not just classroom norms. The new education privacy guidance and the ST4S catalogue are signs that New Zealand is building the infrastructure needed for more durable AI adoption. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/tuhono-connect/statements-media-releases/education-sector-guidance-will-help-frontline-workers/?utm_source=openai))

### 3) Assessment redesign is replacing AI detection

New Zealand’s stronger responses to generative AI are now based on authenticity design: checkpoints, oral validation, version history, explicit use frameworks, and task redesign. Massey’s policy structure, Ministry case studies, and university guidance all point in the same direction. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))

### 4) AI literacy is becoming a mainstream educational capability

Day of AI Aotearoa, Kōtui Ako’s AI Digital Literacy programme, and the draft curriculum’s explicit AI components all show that AI literacy is becoming part of mainstream capability-building rather than a specialist technology add-on. ([dayofai.org](https://dayofai.org/program-hubs/new-zealand-aotearoa?utm_source=openai))

### 5) New Zealand’s distinctive frame remains culture, sovereignty, and language

A notable difference from many international AI-in-education narratives is that New Zealand sources repeatedly foreground **Te Tiriti**, **Māori data sovereignty**, **Pacific values**, and **te reo Māori**. Otago’s governance policy, the Ministry’s warnings about cultural bias, and Auckland’s te reo Māori AI projects all point to a model in which responsible AI is not only about risk control but also about cultural legitimacy and language revitalisation. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/humanresources/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy))

## Conclusion

As of **31 March 2026**, AI adoption in education in New Zealand is best described as **broadening, formalising, and still uneven**. Schools are using AI widely enough that the issue is no longer whether AI is present, but how well it is governed and taught. Tertiary institutions are moving from reactive integrity concerns toward formal policies, governance structures, and managed platforms. System agencies, especially NZQA, remain the most operationally advanced adopters. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/teaching-and-learning-international-survey/talis-2024-publications/talis-2024-first-report))

The biggest shift since **9 March 2026** is that the sector’s centre of gravity has moved further toward **privacy, procurement, policy, and literacy**. March’s privacy guidance, Ministry updates, ST4S rollout, and Otago governance policy all indicate that New Zealand education is building the institutional scaffolding needed for longer-term AI use. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/tuhono-connect/statements-media-releases/education-sector-guidance-will-help-frontline-workers/?utm_source=openai))

The strategic direction is now clearer than it was even three weeks ago: New Zealand is not moving toward a model of AI replacing educators. It is moving toward a model where AI supports planning, literacy, service delivery, and selected assessment processes, while human judgement, authenticity, privacy, and cultural legitimacy become explicit design constraints. That is a more credible and durable path than either hype or prohibition, but it remains a transition rather than a finished state. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai))
