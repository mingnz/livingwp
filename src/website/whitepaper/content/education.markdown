# AI Adoption in Education in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper (2025 Update)

## Introduction  
Generative AI tools (e.g. ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini) are rapidly transforming education globally. UNESCO even themed the 2025 International Day of Education on “AI and education – preserving human agency in a world of automation” ([www.unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/international-day-education-2025-event-new-york-artificial-intelligence-and-education-challenges-and?hub=66925#:~:text=International%20Day%20of%20Education%202025,and%20education%3A%20challenges%20and%20opportunities)). In New Zealand, early coverage of AI in schools focused on plagiarism fears, but the dialogue has shifted toward **integration and pedagogy** ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/amp/#:~:text=)). NZ faces persistent literacy and engagement challenges, and AI is seen as offering *personalized learning support* to help address these. Educators stress that AI should *augment* teachers (acting as digital tutors or assistants) rather than replace them, ensuring that human-led values and relationships remain central in classrooms ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2024/12/11/integrating-ai-and-academia.html#:~:text=Generative%20AI%20is%20a%20disruptive,are%20grappling%20with%20adjusting%20assessments)) ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2024/12/11/integrating-ai-and-academia.html#:~:text=One%20of%20the%20core%20functions,Many%20great)). For example, University of Auckland researchers describe generative AI as potentially “the most significant democratization of education since the printing press,” yet emphasize that universities must double down on human mentorship and critical learning communities ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2024/12/11/integrating-ai-and-academia.html#:~:text=Generative%20AI%20is%20a%20disruptive,are%20grappling%20with%20adjusting%20assessments)) ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2024/12/11/integrating-ai-and-academia.html#:~:text=One%20of%20the%20core%20functions,Many%20great)).  

## Policy & Frameworks  
- **Existing guidance:** In 2023 the Ministry of Education and NZQA issued joint advice on generative AI. They recommend that teachers *verify AI outputs*, avoid entering student personal or sensitive data into AI tools, and be vigilant for cultural or algorithmic bias ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai/#:~:text=Schools%20should%20be%20considering%20whether,some%20other%20really%20useful%20resources)) ([www.privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/ai/generative-artificial-intelligence/#:~:text=,is%20accurate%20or%20contains%20bias)). Schools are urged to update their privacy, tech-use, and data policies for AI – for instance, Netsafe provides a template AI-use policy schools can adapt ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai/#:~:text=Schools%20should%20be%20considering%20whether,some%20other%20really%20useful%20resources)).  
- **Academic integrity:** NZQA makes AI use an explicit part of NCEA authenticity policies. All schools *assessing NCEA standards* must include AI guidelines in their academic integrity rules. The guidance stresses teaching students to cite sources (including any AI-generated content) and warns about AI’s inaccuracies and biases ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/ncea/ncea-for-teachers-and-schools/managing-national-assessment-in-schools/ai-guidance/#:~:text=It%27s%20important%20that%20kura%20and,or%20students%20that%20focuses%20on)). NZQA also advises strategies like frequent check-ins and reflective questioning to ensure work is truly the student’s own ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/ncea/ncea-for-teachers-and-schools/managing-national-assessment-in-schools/ai-guidance/#:~:text=It%27s%20important%20that%20kura%20and,or%20students%20that%20focuses%20on)).  
- **Professional development:** Generative AI training for teachers is expanding quickly. The Ministry notes that many providers and universities now offer short courses on AI – from general introductions to discipline-specific AI applications ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai/#:~:text=PLD%20providers%20and%20tertiary%20institutions,specific%20learning%20areas%20or%20approaches)). Private initiatives (e.g. EDConnect workshops, AI “Master Trainer” programs) are likewise helping educators build AI literacy and critical digital skills.  
- **Privacy & compliance:** NZ’s Office of the Privacy Commissioner cautions that agencies (including schools) must *avoid feeding sensitive personal data* into AI models, since hidden training data can raise privacy and bias issues ([www.privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/ai/generative-artificial-intelligence/#:~:text=,is%20accurate%20or%20contains%20bias)). Complementing this, the Ministry’s **Security & Privacy in Digital Tech** reports (ST4S) now evaluate AI apps against Schüler privacy standards. In early 2025 the NZ Government also adopted a national AI strategy – aligning with OECD principles – under which it released new public-sector GenAI guidance that explicitly highlights fairness, transparency, and Māori/Pasifika and disability considerations ([www.loc.gov](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2025-05-15/new-zealand-new-guidance-released-on-generative-ai-use-in-the-public-service/#:~:text=On%20February%203%2C%202025%2C%20the,service%20published%20in%20July%202023)) ([www.loc.gov](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2025-05-15/new-zealand-new-guidance-released-on-generative-ai-use-in-the-public-service/#:~:text=It%20also%20addresses%20%E2%80%9Ckey%20considerations,Maori%20and%20Pacific%20peoples%20communities)). Education policymakers will draw on these high-level AI policies as they develop schools’ AI frameworks.  

## Current News  
- **Government initiatives:** In Feb 2025 Education Minister Erica Stanford announced a cross-government working group to steer AI in teaching and learning ([nztech.org.nz](https://nztech.org.nz/2025/02/25/teachers-voice-kicking-off-2025-with-ai-in-education/#:~:text=Hon,use%20of%20AI%20in%20teaching)).  The AI Forum (industry alliance) has likewise launched an education sub-group to promote safe, innovative AI use in schools. MOE has recently added AI content to its national curriculum portal (*Tahūrangi*), covering definitions and challenges, though no formal AI curriculum exists yet ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2024/10/23/teacher-voice-ai-in-education-in-aotearoa-reflections-and-research-insights/#:~:text=Last%20month%20MoE%20added%20resources,have%20developed%20a%20Generative%20AI)).  
- **Educator forums:** Early 2025 saw major AI-in-education events. EDConnect’s January tour (featuring author Holly Clark) reached over 900 NZ teachers and leaders from across the country ([nztech.org.nz](https://nztech.org.nz/2025/02/25/teachers-voice-kicking-off-2025-with-ai-in-education/#:~:text=What%20an%20incredible%20way%20to,enhanced%20learning%20experiences)).  Notably, Holly Clark will conduct an “AI Master Trainer” course in NZ (July 2025) to train local educators on AI integration.  These professional development events reflect strong teacher interest.  
- **Surveys:** The NZ Council for Educational Research (NZCER) has launched nationwide online surveys (Nov 2024 onward) of primary and secondary teachers (and students) to gather views on generative AI in classrooms ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/news-and-blogs/nz-primary-school-teachers-years-5-8-share-your-insights-ai-classroom#:~:text=NZCER%20is%20conducting%20online%20surveys,will%20contribute%20to%20this%20research)).  Early responses will map current AI use, concerns, and training needs, informing future policy.  
- **Thought leadership:** Commentators note NZ’s formal AI plan is still evolving. One analysis observes NZ is “falling behind” peers like Australia and Singapore in AI-in-education strategy ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=The%20New%20Zealand%20education%20system,to%20the%20depth%20or%20level)).  Meanwhile, EdTech and teacher leaders have been vocal that AI could *tailor learning* and *reduce teacher workload* (automating routine tasks) if thoughtfully applied ([theeducationhub.org.nz](https://theeducationhub.org.nz/generative-ai-two-years-on-implications-for-teaching-and-learning-2/#:~:text=productivity%20gains,with%20a%20range%20of%20needs)).  A recent Teacher’s Blog (Susana Tomaz, EdTechNZ) reports that 75% of educators surveyed were **unaware** of MOE’s 2023 generative-AI guidelines, highlighting a need for wider dissemination and training ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2024/10/23/teacher-voice-ai-in-education-in-aotearoa-reflections-and-research-insights/#:~:text=AI%20www,a%20culturally%20responsive%20national%20framework)).  The same analysis warned of an “AI divide”: some schools are early adopters while others simply ban AI tools, underscoring equity concerns ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2024/10/23/teacher-voice-ai-in-education-in-aotearoa-reflections-and-research-insights/#:~:text=Last%20month%20MoE%20added%20resources,have%20developed%20a%20Generative%20AI)).  

## Research & Analysis  
- **Educator surveys:** Practitioner research (2024) shows NZ teachers broadly cautious but curious. In an EdTechNZ blog-survey, 3 out of 4 principals and teachers hadn’t even seen official AI guidance from MOE ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2024/10/23/teacher-voice-ai-in-education-in-aotearoa-reflections-and-research-insights/#:~:text=AI%20www,a%20culturally%20responsive%20national%20framework)) and many expressed a desire for a culturally-responsive national framework.  Academic integrity topped their worries, but thought-leaders argue for shifting toward innovation. For example, an education blogger noted we should “stop looking for evidence of cheating and start looking for evidence of learning” with ChatGPT ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2024/10/23/teacher-voice-ai-in-education-in-aotearoa-reflections-and-research-insights/#:~:text=Academic%20integrity%20is%20still%20stealing,the%20focus%20on%20AIED%20innovation)).  
- **Educational analyses:** Thought pieces by NZ educators and analysts emphasize AI’s practical uses. A report from *The Education Hub* (Mar 2025) summarizes that generative tools can provide “considerable administrative relief” (e.g. automating marking or report writing), and enable personalized tutoring, data analytics, and enhanced engagement ([theeducationhub.org.nz](https://theeducationhub.org.nz/generative-ai-two-years-on-implications-for-teaching-and-learning-2/#:~:text=productivity%20gains,with%20a%20range%20of%20needs)).  This aligns with MOE’s goal of data-driven, differentiated teaching.  At a NZ symposium in mid-2025, experts stressed new assessment models: Prof. Naomi Cogger (Massey) coined a “two-lane” approach (secure tests for basic skills vs. AI-augmented projects for higher-order learning) and warned against the “illusion of knowledge” if students rely solely on AI answers ([www.studiosity.com](https://www.studiosity.com/blog/key-insights-from-the-nz-perspectives-on-genai-in-higher-education-symposium#:~:text=A%20recurring%20theme%20was%20the,to%20develop%20evaluative%20skills)) ([www.studiosity.com](https://www.studiosity.com/blog/key-insights-from-the-nz-perspectives-on-genai-in-higher-education-symposium#:~:text=The%20discussion%20highlighted%20the%20need,education%20and%20the%20collective%20effort)).  Canterbury Uni’s Deputy VC shares that UC is revising exams to focus on critical thinking, reflecting this trend ([www.studiosity.com](https://www.studiosity.com/blog/key-insights-from-the-nz-perspectives-on-genai-in-higher-education-symposium#:~:text=mitigated%20by%20rethinking%20the%20way,on%20critical%20thinking%20and%20questioning)).  
- **Global policy influences:** International frameworks are informing NZ thinking. UNESCO’s global GenAI guidance (Sept 2023, updated 2025) calls for a **human-centered** approach — immediate actions and long-term policies to build teacher capacity and ethical AI use ([www.unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/guidance-generative-ai-education-and-research#:~:text=UNESCO%E2%80%99s%20first%20global%20guidance%20on,vision%20of%20these%20new%20technologies)).  NZ experts have likewise urged adapting UNESCO’s forthcoming AI competency framework for teachers to local needs, establishing ethical AI guidelines in schools, and involving teachers/students as co-designers of AI tools ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,to%20suit%20specific%20national%20contexts)).  Such recommendations echo NZ’s values of participatory, inclusive education and highlight the need for continual professional development.  
- **Cultural and linguistic context:**  Aotearoa-specific research highlights indigenous imperatives.  Symposium panelists (2025) and Maori technologists emphasize integrating Te Tiriti obligations and Māori worldview in AI. For instance, Massey University’s GenAI lead, Prof. Cogger, noted that NZ must account for Māori knowledge systems and data sovereignty in AI models ([www.studiosity.com](https://www.studiosity.com/blog/key-insights-from-the-nz-perspectives-on-genai-in-higher-education-symposium#:~:text=While%20acknowledging%20that%20New%20Zealand,led%20initiatives)) ([www.studiosity.com](https://www.studiosity.com/blog/key-insights-from-the-nz-perspectives-on-genai-in-higher-education-symposium#:~:text=Professor%20Martin%20Carroll%20echoed%20this%2C,from%20%204%20Te%20Hiku)).  Practical projects like Te Hiku Media’s suite of AI tools (e.g. *Kaituhi* voice-transcriber and *Reo* text-to-speech for te reo Māori) demonstrate efforts to make AI resources accessible and relevant to Māori learners ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Te%20Reo%20Irirangi%20o%20Te,example%2C%20see%20the%20following%20products)).  

## Case Studies  
- **University of Auckland – “Sofia” AI tutor:** A project in the Business School trialled “Sofia,” an anthropomorphic AI agent for marketing students ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Sofia%20is%20an%20anthropomorphic%20AI,been%20mentioned%20in%20The%20Conversation)). Sofia can answer course questions, generate quizzes, and even converse at a basic level. Crucially, it provides teachers with analytics on student queries and answers, helping identify concept gaps. (The Sofia team won a regional AI-in-education award and published their findings.)  
- **Toi Ohomai Institute – Cogniti AI Agents:** This polytechnic built several course-specific AI bots using the Cogniti platform ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=,that%20provides%20feedback%20on%20work)). Examples include a “Beauty Therapy Revision” agent, a nursing mentor for support and exam prep, a virtual legal advisor for law scenarios (using NZ case studies), and a feedback bot for applied studies. Content is localized (Aotearoa law cases, te reo terms) to ensure relevance.  
- **AUT – Research Proposal Assistant:** Auckland University of Technology created an AI assistant (with Cogniti) for Master’s students in nursing and science ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Auckland%20University%20of%20Technology%20has,with%20feedback%20and%20possible%20edits)). The agent aggregates a library of past thesis abstracts and provides feedback and suggestions on students’ proposal drafts. While still a prototype, it exemplifies how AI can scaffold academic writing in HE.  
- **Maori Language AI Tools:** Te Hiku Media (NZ) has launched AI/NLP products for te reo Māori ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Te%20Reo%20Irirangi%20o%20Te,example%2C%20see%20the%20following%20products)). For example, *Kaituhi* transcribes spoken Māori and English audio into text, and *Reo* synthesizes spoken Māori utterances. *Reo Ora* gives pronunciation feedback. These tools support bilingual and Māori-medium education by embedding Māori language capability into AI platforms.  
- **Vocational Oral Exams (Food & Fibre):** The Food & Fibre Centre of Vocational Excellence funded a project (Scarlatti Research) to build an AI agent for oral skill assessment ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Phase%3A%20In%20development)). This voice-driven tutor can interview students (asking domain-specific questions by voice) and automatically grade their spoken answers. It aims to offer more practice to rural and vocational learners while saving teacher resources.  
- **NZQA AI Tools:** NZQA is quietly piloting AI in two ways. First, it trialled a generative tool to provide *preliminary grades* for Year 10 literacy and numeracy exams ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=The%20NZQA%20have%20just%20finished,10%20literacy%20and%20numeracy%20assessments)), showing openness to using AI to support secondary assessment. Second, NZQA’s student helpline already has a chatbot for queries, and plans are underway to upgrade it into a generative-AI-powered conversational agent ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=NZQA%20already%20have%20a%20chatbot,it%20can%20be%20more%20conversational)), demonstrating cautious innovation in learner support.  
- **Secondary-school pilot – Rosehill College:** In 2024 Rosehill College used an AI-driven diagnostic tool (from Education Perfect) in senior science classes ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=In%202024%2C%20Rosehill%20College%20trialled,approach%20needs%20to%20be%20adjusted)). The system gave real-time quizzes to find each student’s current level and then tracked progress with formative assessments. Teachers reported this helped quickly identify who was struggling with key concepts and adjust instruction on the fly.  
- **Teacher training agent – NIMO:** Future Makers NZ is trialling *NIMO*, an AI simulation for teacher professional development ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Future%20Makers%20are%20currently%20working,where%20the%20teacher%20could%20improve)). In this system, teachers role-play challenging conversations (with a parent or student) using a virtual agent. NIMO records the dialogue and then analyzes it, providing a transcript and feedback on communication skills. It helps teachers practice de-escalation and coaching in a safe, repeatable way.  

## Trends and Predictions  
- **Personalized learning & teacher facilitation:** As generative AI matures, NZ classrooms are likely to offer more tailored learning pathways. Adaptive AI “tutors” could modify content to each student’s pace and style. In this model, teachers become mentors/coaches, focusing on guiding critical thinking and social learning. Analysts predict AI will increasingly handle routine tasks (e.g. grading, drill practice), freeing teachers for high-value interactions ([theeducationhub.org.nz](https://theeducationhub.org.nz/generative-ai-two-years-on-implications-for-teaching-and-learning-2/#:~:text=productivity%20gains,with%20a%20range%20of%20needs)) ([www.studiosity.com](https://www.studiosity.com/blog/key-insights-from-the-nz-perspectives-on-genai-in-higher-education-symposium#:~:text=The%20discussion%20highlighted%20the%20need,education%20and%20the%20collective%20effort)). However, experts caution against a purely “computer-led” model and emphasize blending tech with human oversight.  
- **Surge in upskilling:** Widespread professional development will be essential. Many NZ commentators urge systemic teacher training in AI literacy and pedagogy (mirroring UNESCO’s calls). Initiatives like NZ’s AI Master Trainer courses, university certificates, and emergent communities of practice will proliferate. Support networks (online forums, school clusters) are expected to grow so teachers can share experiences. The goal is to build confidence so educators use AI critically and ethically ([www.unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/guidance-generative-ai-education-and-research#:~:text=UNESCO%E2%80%99s%20first%20global%20guidance%20on,vision%20of%20these%20new%20technologies)) ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,to%20suit%20specific%20national%20contexts)).  
- **Policy and framework evolution:** We anticipate the formalization of national AI-in-education policies in the next few years. The recent cross-ministry group, AI Forum Blueprint, and upcoming education strategy reviews suggest NZ is moving toward an official AI roadmap. This may include standards or curriculum modules on AI. Without clear leadership, NZ risks lagging behind peers; as one analysis notes, stronger centralized strategy (like in Singapore/Australia) is needed to avoid an “AI divide” ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=The%20New%20Zealand%20education%20system,to%20the%20depth%20or%20level)) ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,to%20suit%20specific%20national%20contexts)).  
- **Equity and cultural focus:** Equity will shape uptake. New Zealand is committed to inclusive schooling, so AI must be accessible to all students. Predictions include subsidizing AI tools in low-decile schools and embedding Māori and Pasifika context in AI resources ([www.studiosity.com](https://www.studiosity.com/blog/key-insights-from-the-nz-perspectives-on-genai-in-higher-education-symposium#:~:text=While%20acknowledging%20that%20New%20Zealand,led%20initiatives)) ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Te%20Reo%20Irirangi%20o%20Te,example%2C%20see%20the%20following%20products)). For example, using Māori-language AI agents (like Kaituhi) or developing Pasifika content will be key to avoid widening achievement gaps. Ongoing monitoring of the “AI gap” in access and outcomes is likely.  
- **Assessment transformation:** AI-enabled assessment is on the rise. We expect more automated grading (e.g. essay scorers, oral exam bots) to supplement traditional tests. At the same time, we foresee a “two-lane” model emerging: keep some high-stakes exams locked down (ensuring foundational skills) while allowing projects where AI use is permitted (focusing on process). The NZ Qualifications Authority and schools will invest in detection tools and integrity systems, but New Zealand’s approach will likely emphasize redesigning assessments (as Canterbury Uni and others are doing) rather than outright bans ([www.studiosity.com](https://www.studiosity.com/blog/key-insights-from-the-nz-perspectives-on-genai-in-higher-education-symposium#:~:text=A%20recurring%20theme%20was%20the,to%20develop%20evaluative%20skills)) ([www.studiosity.com](https://www.studiosity.com/blog/key-insights-from-the-nz-perspectives-on-genai-in-higher-education-symposium#:~:text=The%20discussion%20highlighted%20the%20need,education%20and%20the%20collective%20effort)).  
- **Global alignment:** NZ will remain plugged into international developments. Partnerships with UNESCO, OECD and ed-tech consortia will inform local best practices. We expect more pilot collaborations with major tech companies (e.g. Microsoft 365 Copilot for Schools) and learning from global research on what works. NZ’s small size allows agile trials – promising lessons for both local refinement and global contribution.  

## Conclusion  
AI adoption in NZ education is still **nascent and uneven** – many students have ready access to ChatGPT and other tools, yet formal school programs are just emerging ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/amp/#:~:text=)) ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2024/10/23/teacher-voice-ai-in-education-in-aotearoa-reflections-and-research-insights/#:~:text=AI%20www,a%20culturally%20responsive%20national%20framework)).  However, the sector’s active initiatives signal strong momentum. The Ministry and NZQA have updated policies and guidelines, and a range of pilot projects and case studies (universities, colleges, skill centers) are experimenting with AI tutors, assessment bots and support chatbots. 

**Key insights:**  
- *Promise:* AI offers clear benefits – from personalized practice to relief of routine workload. For example, studies note AI can automate grading and tailor tutoring to each student’s pace ([theeducationhub.org.nz](https://theeducationhub.org.nz/generative-ai-two-years-on-implications-for-teaching-and-learning-2/#:~:text=productivity%20gains,with%20a%20range%20of%20needs)). This aligns with NZ’s focus on data-driven, differentiated learning and could help lift achievement by targeting individual needs.  
- *Challenges:* Uptake must overcome hurdles. Without strong leadership, NZ could fall behind international peers ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=The%20New%20Zealand%20education%20system,to%20the%20depth%20or%20level)). Ensuring academic integrity, data privacy and cultural safety are major concerns. A consistent theme is that AI tools should not entrench existing inequalities. Incorporating Māori and Pasifika perspectives (as called for by local scholars ([www.studiosity.com](https://www.studiosity.com/blog/key-insights-from-the-nz-perspectives-on-genai-in-higher-education-symposium#:~:text=While%20acknowledging%20that%20New%20Zealand,led%20initiatives))) will be crucial so that AI truly serves all learners.  
- *Next steps:* Stakeholders must collaborate on a clear roadmap. Suggested actions include piloting promising tools more widely, investing in ongoing teacher PD, and co-designing AI solutions with educators and communities (per UNESCO’s advice ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,to%20suit%20specific%20national%20contexts))). This whitepaper should evolve – we will update it with new case study outcomes and research. The goal is a strategic, human-centered approach: as former PM Jacinda Ardern and NZ thought-leaders advocate, NZ should see AI as an **opportunity** to enhance learning (not a threat), and adapt proactively with education and support.  

In summary, the AI journey in Aotearoa’s schools and universities is just beginning. Early pilots and research give cautious optimism: AI could personalize learning and reduce teacher drudgery, but only if balanced with equity, ethics and sound pedagogy. The coming years will test how well New Zealand can transform these tools into better educational outcomes while keeping people at the center of education.
