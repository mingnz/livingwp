# AI in Education in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper  
*Updated for publication on 10 March 2026*

## Executive Snapshot

New Zealand’s education sector is now clearly in its **post-novelty phase** of AI adoption. The debate has shifted from whether AI should be acknowledged in education to how it should be governed, taught, constrained, and productively used. The strongest evidence of current adoption sits in teacher workflow support, school assessment-policy redesign, national assessment operations at NZQA, and fast-growing AI literacy initiatives aimed at teachers and learners. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

The system remains **uneven rather than fully transformed**. Adoption is strongest where human oversight is easy to preserve: lesson planning, summarisation, drafting, tutoring, service support, and curriculum development. It remains far more cautious in high-stakes assessment, credentialing, and formal teacher judgement. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

As of **Tuesday, 10 March 2026**, the biggest near-term signals are:  
- Ministry guidance is now explicit on school policy, NCEA constraints, and principles for AI-assisted marking. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))  
- Draft curriculum content keeps AI visible, especially in **Years 9–10 Technology and Computer Science**, with consultation open until **Friday, 24 April 2026**. ([newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/5637296078.p))  
- National AI literacy activity is accelerating through **Day of AI Aotearoa New Zealand** and **Kōtui Ako’s AI Digital Literacy** programme, both active from **9 March 2026**. ([dayofai.org](https://dayofai.org/news/australian-and-new-zealand-students-receive-free-curriculum-aligned-ai-literacy-through-tailored-local-programs))  
- ERO is now actively gathering system evidence on how AI is being used by leaders, teachers, and students in schools. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/bulletins/school-leaders/03-03-26?utm_source=openai))

Compared with the version dated **9 March 2026**, no major national policy reset was identified in the 24 hours to **10 March 2026**. The most material new development is a fresh **9 March 2026** announcement from Day of AI confirming the national launch of free, curriculum-aligned AI literacy resources for New Zealand schools, reporting pilot gains in student understanding and strong interest from more than 100 schools. This suggests the whitepaper’s earlier assessment remains directionally correct, with the clearest immediate movement now in AI literacy scale-up rather than in regulation. This is an inference based on the latest source review. ([dayofai.org](https://dayofai.org/news/australian-and-new-zealand-students-receive-free-curriculum-aligned-ai-literacy-through-tailored-local-programs))

## Overall State of Adoption

### Schools: widespread experimentation, gradual formalisation

Official **TALIS 2024** data shows AI use is already mainstream among many New Zealand teachers. Across Years 1–10, **63%** of teachers reported using AI in the prior year; among Years 7–10 teachers the figure was **69%**, well above the OECD lower-secondary average of **36%**. Among New Zealand teachers who had used AI, the most common uses were generating lesson plans (**78%**) and learning about or summarising a topic (**73%**), while only **12%** said they used AI to assess student work. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf?utm_source=openai))

That pattern is important: current school adoption is concentrated in **augmentation**, not substitution. New Zealand teachers are using AI mostly where it saves time or supports preparation, while keeping professional judgement and responsibility with the teacher. That aligns closely with Ministry guidance, which states teachers must remain central and that AI should support, not replace, teaching and learning. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

NZCER’s 2025 primary-school research shows the same pattern at a more granular level. In its surveyed cohort, **18%** of teachers used AI daily and **26%** several times a week. The most common uses were lesson planning (**82%**), assessment design (**66%**), personalising learning (**65%**), research for teaching (**59%**), and writing student feedback reports (**51%**). At the same time, **68%** had observed inaccuracies in AI-generated content. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/Generative%20artificial%20intelligence%20in%20Aotearoa%20New%20Zealand%20primary%20schools%E2%80%94Teacher%20and%20student%20survey%20findings.pdf))

### Tertiary: from detection tools to assessment redesign

In universities, the center of gravity has shifted away from confidence in AI-detection software and toward redesigning assessments around oral defence, in-person validation, secured tasks, and clearer AI-use rules. RNZ reported on **30 September 2025** that several universities had stopped endorsing or relying on AI-detection tools, with institutions such as Massey prioritising prevention and AI literacy instead. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/574517/universities-give-up-using-software-to-detect-ai-in-students-work/?utm_source=openai))

The sector is still unsettled. On **6 May 2025**, Victoria University of Wellington’s law faculty required some exams to be handwritten because of concerns about AI-enabled cheating on laptops. On **27 August 2025**, Lincoln University required around **120** postgraduate finance students to explain code in person after widespread concerns that generative AI had been used. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/560059/victoria-law-students-not-allowed-laptops-in-exams-to-prevent-ai-cheating?utm_source=openai))

The practical takeaway is that tertiary education is not rejecting AI; it is abandoning the idea that weak forensic detection can carry the integrity burden on its own. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/574517/universities-give-up-using-software-to-detect-ai-in-students-work/?utm_source=openai))

### System agencies: the most mature operational AI use

The most advanced production deployment of AI in New Zealand education remains at **NZQA**. NZQA says it used an AI-powered **Automated Text Scoring** tool to mark more than **55,000** writing literacy assessments in **May 2025**, enabling results to be returned **3.5 weeks earlier** than the previous year. NZQA also states that a **2024** trial on **36,000** writing samples found the tool as reliable as human markers, and that more than a third of the May 2025 results were double-checked by experienced human markers, with human judgement used where scores differed. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

NZQA is also expanding AI in service delivery. Its chatbot **Awhina**, launched in 2023, began rolling out a generative AI upgrade in **April 2025** to better answer questions by searching and synthesising public website information. NZQA’s 2024/25 annual report says a generative AI-powered chatbot was launched on international webpages, and its current performance documents show AI-enabled customer-service channels are now part of NZQA’s operating direction. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/our-website-chatbot-awhina-is-growing/?utm_source=openai))

## Policy, Governance, and Curriculum

### Ministry of Education guidance is now clearer and more operational

The Ministry’s school guidance, last updated on **28 October 2025**, now gives schools more explicit direction on acceptable use, data privacy, cultural bias, age restrictions, NCEA constraints, and policy design. It says schools should have policies covering purpose, scope, risk mitigation, professional development, data privacy, and review. It also repeats that **GenAI is not permitted in NCEA external assessment**. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

On marking, the Ministry now promotes a principles-based approach built around **teacher responsibility and professional judgement**, **human oversight**, and **appropriate use cases**. The position is clear: AI may support marking workflows, but it should not replace teacher judgement, especially where assessment decisions carry formal or credentialing weight. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### NZQA’s position remains restrictive in high-stakes student-authored work

For school assessment, the core rule has not changed: students must submit their **own work**, and GenAI is not permitted in NCEA external assessment. For tertiary providers, NZQA’s academic-integrity guidance emphasises supporting staff understanding, maintaining robust integrity systems, and addressing AI through pedagogy and assessment design rather than simplistic enforcement tools alone. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### Privacy and Māori data sovereignty are becoming central readiness tests

The Office of the Privacy Commissioner’s AI guidance remains a key framing document for education organisations. It expects agencies using generative AI to secure senior approval, test necessity and proportionality, conduct privacy impact assessments, be transparent, engage with affected communities including Māori, ensure human review, and prevent inappropriate retention or disclosure of personal information. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/ai/generative-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

Tāhūrangi’s AI guidance adds a specifically Aotearoa lens, stating that Māori data is a taonga and that Māori data sovereignty must be considered under Te Tiriti o Waitangi. It also states AI cannot replace engagement with local whānau, hapū, and iwi. This is a distinctive feature of the New Zealand education conversation, and it increasingly shapes what “responsible AI” means in practice. ([newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/introduction-to-artificial-intelligence/5637235331.p?utm_source=openai))

### Curriculum direction: AI now has live structural visibility

AI is no longer just an optional sidebar in curriculum discussion. In the draft **Years 9–10 Technology** material now out for consultation, the **Computer Science** strand explicitly addresses algorithms, data, logic, intelligent systems, and AI-related ethical issues. It includes statements such as AI adoption involving human oversight and accountability, the role of data and model design in shaping AI capability and fairness, and the use of AI tools to create or adapt simple applications. ([newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/5637296078.p))

Consultation on the draft **Years 0–10 curriculum content** is open until **Friday, 24 April 2026**. The Ministry says the full and final national curriculum is targeted for release by **mid-2026**, with all Years 9–10 learning areas to be used from **Term 1, 2027**. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/have-your-say/consultation-year-0-10-draft-curriculum-content/details?utm_source=openai))

For senior secondary, Tāhūrangi says draft **Years 11–13** subject curricula are to be released in **Term 1, 2026** for feedback and familiarisation, with no formal requirement to use the new content until **2028 for Year 11**, **2029 for Year 12**, and **2030 for Year 13**. A possible specialist Year 13 generative AI subject remains investigatory rather than settled. ([tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://tahurangi.education.govt.nz/national-curriculum-timeline-changes?utm_source=openai))

## Research Overview

### National benchmark evidence: New Zealand is ahead on teacher use, but cautious on risk

TALIS 2024 shows New Zealand teachers are comparatively active AI users, especially in Years 7–10. The same dataset also shows caution: New Zealand Year 7–10 teachers were more likely than OECD peers to see AI as helpful for lesson planning and adapting materials, but also more likely to worry that it could enable students to submit others’ work as their own or generate incorrect recommendations. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

This combination of relatively high use and relatively high concern helps explain the emerging policy pattern in New Zealand: **adopt, but constrain; use, but verify; teach, but redesign assessment**. This is an inference from the TALIS results and the Ministry/NZQA guidance. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

### NZCER primary-school evidence: use is real, governance is incomplete

NZCER’s 2025 primary-school report describes a sector already experimenting, but still under-supported. It reports that most surveyed teachers relied on free chatbots, that three-quarters had no school-funded premium AI access, and that **85%** wanted more training. The report also points to policy gaps: among teachers whose schools had or were developing AI policy, **62%** were unsure whether Māori data sovereignty had been considered. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools?utm_source=openai))

The same report suggests student exposure is already difficult for schools to contain. NZCER found that more than half of surveyed students reported using generative AI, with use more common outside school than inside school, and many students uncertain about the rules. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools?utm_source=openai))

### System evidence gathering is still catching up

The Ministry’s **3 March 2026** school leaders bulletin confirmed that ERO is now surveying schools on how AI is being used by teachers, leaders, and students. ERO’s own research pipeline describes this work as examining the current uses, challenges, benefits, and impacts of generative AI in schools. That is a sign the system is still moving from local experimentation toward an evidence-based national operating model. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/bulletins/school-leaders/03-03-26?utm_source=openai))

## Case Studies

### 1) Aotea College: policy-led assessment redesign

The Ministry’s case study on **Aotea College** shows one of the clearest secondary-school responses to generative AI. The school established an AI working group, updated authenticity statements, built staff professional learning, created a handbook on referencing and verbal questioning, and encouraged practices such as supervised assessment stages, checkpointing, and familiarity with each student’s voice and style. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Aotea%20College_MAR2025.pdf?utm_source=openai))

**Why it matters:** Aotea represents the move from teacher-by-teacher improvisation to institution-level governance and process design. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Aotea%20College_MAR2025.pdf?utm_source=openai))

### 2) Hobsonville Point Secondary School: authenticity through process evidence

The Ministry’s **Hobsonville Point Secondary School** case study highlights the use of checkpoints, standard templates, original working documents with version history, and verbal checks of understanding. The Ministry reports this approach helped reduce inappropriate AI use in assessments. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

**Why it matters:** This is a durable model because it relies less on trying to “detect AI” after submission and more on designing assessments so authentic learning is visible throughout the process. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### 3) New Windsor School and the Ako Hiko cluster: primary AI capability building

Education Gazette reported on **31 March 2025** that **New Windsor School**, as part of the Ako Hiko cluster, was piloting AI to support literacy, critical thinking, creativity, and cyber-safety, while also exploring administration use cases and cluster-wide rollout. Staff had received professional learning support, and the school deliberately chose not to treat AI as something that could simply be banned away. ([gazette.education.govt.nz](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/articles/ai-approach-at-new-windsor-school-prepares-akonga-for-the-future/?utm_source=openai))

**Why it matters:** This is one of the best primary-sector examples of AI moving from isolated classroom curiosity into a broader capability-building model. ([gazette.education.govt.nz](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/articles/ai-approach-at-new-windsor-school-prepares-akonga-for-the-future/?utm_source=openai))

### 4) NZQA: high-stakes operational deployment

NZQA’s automated scoring programme is the most consequential AI deployment in New Zealand education because it is already operating at national scale in assessment infrastructure. NZQA frames the deployment around quality, consistency, speed, privacy, and cultural fit, and says future expansion will depend on AI continuing to perform at least as well as humans. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

**Why it matters:** This is not pilot theatre; it is controlled production use in a nationally significant workflow. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

### 5) University of Auckland: service AI and culturally grounded learning AI

The University of Auckland says its **AI Assistant** now handles more than **9,000 conversations** and **60,000 searches per month**, providing 24/7 support across more than 90 digital domains. Separately, the University announced a **$1 million** three-year project to develop an AI-powered **te reo Māori pronunciation coaching tool** with real-time personalised feedback, in partnership with **Te Hiku Media** and with explicit grounding in tikanga Māori and Indigenous data sovereignty. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/10/07/ai-assistant-enhances-digital-experience-at-uoa.html?utm_source=openai))

**Why it matters:** These examples show tertiary AI adoption splitting into two strong tracks: operational service automation and culturally grounded educational innovation. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/10/07/ai-assistant-enhances-digital-experience-at-uoa.html?utm_source=openai))

## National AI Literacy Initiatives

### Day of AI Aotearoa New Zealand

Day of AI’s New Zealand hub states that the programme launched in **2025** through collaboration between **TENZ**, **EPIT**, and **NZCER**, with the goal of reaching every school, teacher, and learner by the end of **Term 1 2026**. On **9 March 2026**, Day of AI announced that teachers in New Zealand could begin using free, country-specific, curriculum-aligned AI literacy lessons. It also reported pilot results showing student understanding increasing from **20% to 64%**, and said more than **100 schools** had expressed interest ahead of launch. ([dayofai.org](https://dayofai.org/program-hubs/new-zealand-aotearoa?utm_source=openai))

### Kōtui Ako’s AI Digital Literacy programme

Kōtui Ako’s nationwide **AI Digital Literacy online** programme was advertised in Education Gazette as covering AI fundamentals, bias, algorithms, facial recognition, plagiarism, and classroom impacts, with classes beginning on **Monday, 9 March 2026**. Kōtui Ako’s own 2025 newsletter also said it had developed AI guidelines for teaching and learning alongside this learner programme. ([gazette.education.govt.nz](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/notices/1HAqHJ-ai-digital-literacy-online/?utm_source=openai))

**Why this matters overall:** AI literacy in New Zealand is no longer just a policy aspiration; it is becoming an organised delivery effort with curriculum alignment, teacher support, and locally adapted framing. ([dayofai.org](https://dayofai.org/news/australian-and-new-zealand-students-receive-free-curriculum-aligned-ai-literacy-through-tailored-local-programs))

## Emerging Trends

### 1) Augmentation is winning over automation

The most credible, fastest-growing uses of AI in New Zealand education are those that extend human capability rather than replace it: planning, drafting, tutoring, summarising, support services, and selected low-risk workflow tasks. High-stakes judgement remains human-led. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

### 2) Assessment redesign is replacing detector-led enforcement

Across both schools and universities, the most durable response to AI misuse is becoming clear: checkpoints, oral explanation, supervised production, version history, and task redesign are displacing confidence in AI detectors. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Aotea%20College_MAR2025.pdf?utm_source=openai))

### 3) AI literacy is moving toward mainstream provision

With Day of AI national launch activity, Kōtui Ako’s live programme, and draft curriculum material embedding AI-related knowledge, New Zealand is moving toward treating AI literacy as a general education capability rather than a niche digital topic. ([dayofai.org](https://dayofai.org/news/australian-and-new-zealand-students-receive-free-curriculum-aligned-ai-literacy-through-tailored-local-programs))

### 4) Culture, sovereignty, and local legitimacy are differentiators

The New Zealand approach is increasingly distinct because AI adoption is being tied to Māori data sovereignty, tikanga, Te Tiriti considerations, and te reo Māori revitalisation. That makes the conversation broader than productivity alone. ([newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/introduction-to-artificial-intelligence/5637235331.p?utm_source=openai))

### 5) National practice is still ahead of the full operating model

The system has guidance, pilots, scaled initiatives, and some major operational deployments, but it is still building a settled national model. ERO’s active school surveys, open curriculum consultation through **24 April 2026**, and the phased senior-secondary implementation timeline all point to a sector still in structured transition. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/bulletins/school-leaders/03-03-26?utm_source=openai))

## Conclusion

As of **10 March 2026**, AI adoption in education in New Zealand is best described as **active, uneven, and maturing**. It is active because teacher use is now widespread, NZQA has production AI in assessment and customer-service workflows, and national AI literacy programmes are scaling. It is uneven because classroom use, policy maturity, and staff capability still vary widely by institution and education level. And it is maturing because the system is shifting from novelty and panic toward governance, pedagogy, infrastructure, and cultural legitimacy. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The strategic direction is clearer than it was a year ago. New Zealand is not moving toward an “AI replaces educators” model. It is moving toward a model where AI supports planning, service delivery, selected assessment operations, and learner capability-building, while human judgement, authenticity, privacy, and Māori data sovereignty become more explicit design requirements. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

The defining question for the next phase is no longer whether AI has entered New Zealand education. It has. The real question is whether the sector can scale use in ways that are educationally credible, culturally grounded, operationally trustworthy, and assessment-safe. On the evidence available by **10 March 2026**, New Zealand has begun that transition in earnest, but it has not yet completed it. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/have-your-say/consultation-year-0-10-draft-curriculum-content/details?utm_source=openai))
