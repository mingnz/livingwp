# AI in Education in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper  
*Updated for publication on 9 March 2026*

## Executive Snapshot

New Zealand’s education sector is no longer in the first-wave “AI panic” phase. As of **9 March 2026**, the sector is moving into a more governed phase: schools are formalising acceptable-use rules, tertiary providers are redesigning assessment around disclosure and validation rather than detector software, and national agencies are already running AI in production for assessment operations and service delivery. The adoption story is still uneven, but it is now unmistakably systemic rather than fringe. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai))

Three features define the current moment. First, **teacher use is already mainstream**, especially for planning and content preparation rather than grading. Second, **assessment integrity has become the central design challenge**, pushing institutions toward checkpoints, oral defence, prompt disclosure, version history, and secure tasks. Third, the New Zealand conversation is increasingly shaped by **privacy, Te Tiriti, and Māori data sovereignty**, which gives local AI adoption a distinct character compared with more generic international models. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

## What’s New Since the Previous Version

- **ERO has moved from observation to active evidence gathering.** In a Ministry bulletin published on **3 March 2026**, schools were directed to live ERO leader and teacher surveys on “AI in schools,” showing that national monitoring is now trying to catch up with practice on the ground. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/bulletins/school-leaders/03-03-26))
- **Tertiary governance has become more explicit.** Massey’s **May 2025** GenAI assessment policy formalised a framework ranging from “No AI” to “AI Exploration,” while the University of Otago’s new **AI Governance Policy** is due to take effect on **10 March 2026**, explicitly covering teaching, service delivery, risk, data sovereignty, and staff capability. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))
- **AI literacy initiatives have shifted from aspiration to live delivery.** Kōtui Ako’s nationwide **AI Digital Literacy** programme begins on **Monday, 9 March 2026**, and Day of AI Aotearoa continues to target access for every school, teacher, and learner by the end of Term 1 2026. ([gazette.education.govt.nz](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/notices/1HAqHJ-ai-digital-literacy-online/?utm_source=openai))

## Overall State of Adoption

### Schools: widespread use, uneven maturity

The strongest national benchmark remains **TALIS 2024**. It shows that **63% of New Zealand Year 1–10 teachers** had used AI in the prior year; among the internationally comparable lower-secondary group, **69% of New Zealand Year 7–10 teachers** had used AI versus an **OECD average of 36%**. Among New Zealand teachers who had used AI, the most common tasks were generating lesson plans and activities (**78%**) and learning about or summarising a topic (**73%**), while only **12%** said they had used AI to assess or mark student work. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

That pattern is important. In schools, AI adoption is strongest where teacher oversight is easy to maintain and the risk of credential distortion is lower. New Zealand teachers are using AI primarily as a planning, drafting, and support tool—not as a substitute for final judgement. Ministry guidance now makes that explicit: teachers must remain central, take responsibility for students’ learning, and treat AI marking as support rather than replacement. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

### Tertiary: from detection to controlled use

In tertiary education, the center of gravity has shifted away from “catching” AI use and toward **designing assessment for an AI-saturated environment**. RNZ reported in September 2025 that Auckland, Massey, and Victoria were not using AI-detection tools, with Massey explicitly describing detection as unreliable and prioritising secured assessments, oral work, laboratories, studios, and exams where AI use can be prevented rather than inferred afterward. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/574517/universities-give-up-using-software-to-detect-ai-in-students-work/))

This move is being formalised in policy. Massey’s GenAI assessment policy says AI may support a student’s own intellectual work but not substitute for it, requires disclosure of use, and allows institutions to ask for prompt records, version histories, and other evidence of authorship. At Otago, a university-wide AI Governance Policy taking effect on **10 March 2026** frames AI as an opportunity across research, teaching, and service delivery, but requires governance, risk assessment, Māori data sovereignty consideration, and ongoing AI literacy for staff and Council members. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))

### System agencies: the most mature operational deployment

The most operationally mature AI use in New Zealand education is still outside the classroom. NZQA reported that in **May 2025** it used an AI-powered **Automated Text Scoring** system to mark more than **55,000** writing literacy assessments, enabling results to be returned **3.5 weeks earlier** than in the previous year. NZQA says its 2024 large-scale trial on around **36,000** writing responses found the tool as reliable as human markers, and human re-checking remained in place for a substantial share of results close to the achievement boundary. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

NZQA is also using AI in customer service and internal governance. Its public statements say AI has supported service interactions since 2023 and now underpins more than **250,000 student and customer interactions each year**, while its responsible-use framework and toolkit are intended to support safe end-to-end adoption with attention to security, data ethics, explainability, and sovereignty. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

## Policy, Governance, and Curriculum

### Ministry guidance is now clearer and more operational

The Ministry’s school guidance, last updated on **28 October 2025**, now goes well beyond general caution. It tells schools to avoid entering personal data into public tools, account for cultural bias, build a school AI policy, and ensure any assessment use preserves authenticity. For marking, the Ministry’s updated framework rests on three principles: **teacher responsibility and professional judgement, human oversight, and appropriate use cases**. It also states that for **NCEA external assessment, the use of GenAI is not permitted**. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai))

The detailed marking guidance is especially revealing about the current policy mood. Teachers remain fully responsible for marking decisions; AI use for complex, summative, or credential-related work requires caution; and use of AI to mark **NCEA internal assessments** is explicitly discouraged because the assessor’s judgement carries legal standing. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-10/1373%20GenAI%20Marking%20Tools%20flyer_V2.pdf?VersionId=Oz_o7IGHBcO7x7DAX6SXlIT2JF60QBmv))

### Curriculum and literacy work are moving forward

The curriculum picture is also changing. Consultation on the draft **Years 0–10** curriculum is open until **Friday, 24 April 2026**, with final release planned for mid-2026. In the draft Technology learning area for Years 9–10, **Computer Science** explicitly focuses on “algorithms, data, and logic in digital systems and AI,” and includes ethical implications, bias, fairness, and the role of intelligent systems. ([newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/nzc---years-0-10-draft-curriculum-consultation/5637305076.p))

Alongside curriculum design, AI literacy is being built through sector initiatives. Day of AI Aotearoa was launched through TENZ, EPIT, and NZCER with the goal of reaching every school, teacher, and learner by the end of Term 1 2026. Kōtui Ako’s AI Digital Literacy programme, funded and supported through the Ministry ecosystem, starts classes on **9 March 2026** and covers fundamentals, bias, algorithms, facial recognition, chatbots, and plagiarism. ([dayofai.org](https://dayofai.org/program-hubs/new-zealand-aotearoa))

### Privacy and Māori data sovereignty are central, not peripheral

New Zealand’s AI-in-education debate is increasingly distinguished by privacy and sovereignty concerns. The Office of the Privacy Commissioner says generative AI use remains fully subject to the Privacy Act, and Ministry guidance for schools directly reinforces the need to avoid entering personal or sensitive data into public tools. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/ai/generative-artificial-intelligence/))

Tāhūrangi’s AI guidance goes further by stating that **Te Tiriti o Waitangi must be considered** in educational use of AI, that Māori data is a taonga, and that AI cannot replace engagement with local whānau, hapū, and iwi. This framing is becoming one of the clearest markers of what “responsible AI” means in Aotearoa New Zealand. ([newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/introduction-to-artificial-intelligence/5637235331.p))

## Research Overview

### National evidence: strong use, strong caution

TALIS 2024 shows New Zealand teachers are ahead of many peers in experimenting with AI, but they are not naïve about its risks. New Zealand Year 7–10 teachers were especially likely to agree that AI enables students to misrepresent others’ work as their own (**88%**) and that AI can make recommendations that are not appropriate or correct (**81%**). This is a sector that is using AI while simultaneously building a strong risk consciousness around it. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

### NZCER: primary schools are already well into the adoption curve

NZCER’s 2025 primary-school study provides the clearest granular picture. Its sample included **266 teachers** and **147 students** across four case-study schools, with the important caveat that participating teachers were disproportionately interested in AI. Even so, the findings are striking: **69%** of responding teachers used AI at least weekly, including **18% daily** and **26% several times a week**. The most common teacher uses were lesson planning (**82%**), assessment design (**66%**), personalising learning (**65%**), teaching research (**59%**), and student report writing (**51%**). ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools))

The same report shows why governance remains incomplete. **68%** of surveyed teachers had observed inaccuracies in AI-generated content, **85%** wanted more training, and most teachers in schools with an AI policy were still unsure whether Māori data sovereignty had been considered; **62%** selected “I don’t know.” Students are also already in the picture: NZCER found that most had heard of AI, more than half reported using generative AI tools, and use was more common outside school than inside. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/Generative%20artificial%20intelligence%20in%20Aotearoa%20New%20Zealand%20primary%20schools%E2%80%94Teacher%20and%20student%20survey%20findings.pdf))

## Case Studies

### Aotea College: school-wide governance response

Aotea College created a staff AI working group, updated assessment policy, and built shared resources so that teachers, students, and whānau had a coherent cross-school understanding of acceptable GenAI use. This is a strong example of AI being treated as an institutional design issue rather than a series of isolated teacher decisions. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Aotea%20College_MAR2025.pdf))

### Hobsonville Point Secondary School: authenticity by process, not detectors

Hobsonville Point Secondary School responded to AI-linked plagiarism by building a school AI strategy, standardising task templates, requiring checkpoints and evidence trails, and reporting a significant reduction in problematic cases. The approach is notable because it preserves inclusive assessment design rather than reverting to blanket pen-and-paper restrictions. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Hobsonville%20Point%20Secondary%20School_MAR2025.pdf?VersionId=.9dochWl4SsdLrmFkwawNSFNaw_SoYAW))

### New Windsor School and the Ako Hiko cluster: primary-sector experimentation at cluster level

New Windsor School’s AI pilot integrates AI into a curriculum emphasising cybersafety, critical engagement, and creativity. Students are using tools such as **Brisk** for real-time writing feedback, and the cluster has invested in PLD and NZQA AI micro-credentials for leaders and kaiako. The school is also exploring AI for administration as well as classroom learning, suggesting movement from isolated practice to wider organisational adoption. ([gazette.education.govt.nz](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/articles/ai-approach-at-new-windsor-school-prepares-akonga-for-the-future/))

### University of Auckland: AI for service delivery and culturally grounded learning innovation

The University of Auckland’s AI Assistant now handles more than **9,000 conversations** and **60,000 searches each month**, providing 24/7 answers across more than 90 university digital domains. Separately, the University is leading a **three-year, $1 million** project to build an AI-powered te reo Māori pronunciation coach with real-time personalised feedback. Together, these show two tertiary AI tracks: operational service automation and culturally grounded learning design. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/10/07/ai-assistant-enhances-digital-experience-at-uoa.html?utm_source=openai))

## Emerging Trends

- **Augmentation is winning over substitution.** The most successful use cases are planning, drafting, feedback, tutoring, search, and service support—not autonomous judgement. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))
- **Assessment redesign is replacing detector-led integrity models.** Massey’s framework, Hobsonville’s checkpoints, and the wider university pullback from AI-detection tools all point in the same direction. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))
- **Governance is becoming more formal.** Ministry guidance is more operational, NZQA has a responsible-use framework, Massey has an assessment policy, and Otago is introducing institution-wide AI governance. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai))
- **AI literacy is moving into mainstream provision.** Curriculum consultation, Day of AI Aotearoa, and Kōtui Ako’s live programme all indicate that AI literacy is becoming a system priority rather than an optional innovation project. ([newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/nzc---years-0-10-draft-curriculum-consultation/5637305076.p))
- **Cultural legitimacy is becoming a differentiator.** Māori data sovereignty, te reo Māori capability, and Te Tiriti-aware design are not side issues; they are increasingly central to whether AI adoption is seen as credible in Aotearoa. ([newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/introduction-to-artificial-intelligence/5637235331.p))

## Conclusion

As of **9 March 2026**, AI adoption in education in New Zealand is best described as **active, uneven, and increasingly governed**. Schools are using AI widely but not uniformly; tertiary institutions are moving from prohibition and detection toward structured, disclosed, assessment-specific use; and system agencies such as NZQA are already operating AI at national scale in tightly controlled workflows. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

The strategic direction is clearer than it was a year ago. New Zealand is not moving toward a model in which AI replaces teachers, assessors, or academic judgement. It is moving toward a model in which AI supports planning, service delivery, selected assessment processes, and AI literacy development—while human oversight, authenticity, privacy, and cultural legitimacy become more explicit design requirements. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/generative-ai))

The next phase will be determined less by whether AI can be used, and more by whether it can be scaled in ways that are **educationally credible, operationally trustworthy, and culturally grounded**. On current evidence, New Zealand has clearly entered that transition. It has not yet finished it. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/bulletins/school-leaders/03-03-26))
