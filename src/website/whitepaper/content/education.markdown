# AI Adoption in Education in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper

**Introduction**  
AI is rapidly entering New Zealand’s education sector. UNESCO highlighted this trend by making the 2025 International Day of Education’s theme “AI in Education,” reflecting how digital-native students eagerly adopted generative AI tools like ChatGPT since 2023 ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=AI%20is%20making%20a%20significant,gain%20traction%20in%20early%202023)).  In our schools, early media coverage centered on students cheating with AI, but the conversation has since shifted to *how* AI can enhance learning.  Understanding AI’s educational impact is urgent as NZ grapples with achievement gaps; advocates caution that AI should **augment** teachers and focus on higher-order skills (critique, creativity, values) rather than simply automating instruction ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/12/04/from-hype-to-human-flourishing-reimagining-education-in-aotearoas-ai-future/#:~:text=match%20at%20L22%20thinking%2C%20and,Education%20systems%20need%20an%20%E2%80%9Cintelligence)) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=But%20there%20are%20also%20pitfalls,technology%20often%20demonstrates%20cultural%20bias)).  

**Policy & Frameworks**  
- **MoE/NZQA Guidelines (2023):**  In 2023 the Ministry of Education and NZQA released guidance on generative AI use.  Schools are urged to *“verify AI outputs, not submit personal data,”* and watch for bias or misleading terms (reflecting advice to check model outputs and safeguard privacy) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=One%20of%20the%20central%20concerns,own%20policies%20around%20the%20technology)) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=think%20about%20when%20using%20AI,These%20include)).  
- **Academic Integrity:**  NZQA requires schools to update assessment-authenticity policies for AI.  Educators are encouraged to teach responsible AI use and emphasize integrity in NCEA classrooms.  
- **Professional Development:**  Teacher training in AI is expanding.  Online courses and PLD workshops (from university short courses to EdTech consortium events) are helping teachers learn AI tools and critical literacy around them.  
- **Privacy & Compliance:**  NZ’s Privacy Commissioner and software guidelines (e.g. MoE’s *Security and Privacy in Software for Schools*) are being updated for AI.  Schools must adapt tech-use policies and leverage resources (like Netsafe’s AI policy templates) to protect student data.  

**Current News**  
- **Curriculum Overhaul (2028):**  In September 2025 Education Minister Erica Stanford announced new Year 11–13 subjects, including a planned **Year 13 GenAI/AI** course by 2028 ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/572737/new-push-for-ai-as-education-minister-erica-stanford-announces-curriculum-changes#:~:text=investigating%20having%20a%20new%20Year,for%20later%20development)).  This STEM-focused curriculum overhaul explicitly emphasizes AI, machine learning, cybersecurity, and digital ethics ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/572737/new-push-for-ai-as-education-minister-erica-stanford-announces-curriculum-changes#:~:text=investigating%20having%20a%20new%20Year,for%20later%20development)).  (Stanford has asked MoE to investigate an AI-specialist course, while educators urge starting AI education well before Year 13 ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/572844/professor-calls-for-government-to-begin-ai-education-earlier#:~:text=Canterbury%20University%20Associate%20Professor%20of,about%20AI%20before%20year%2013)) ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/572737/new-push-for-ai-as-education-minister-erica-stanford-announces-curriculum-changes#:~:text=investigating%20having%20a%20new%20Year,for%20later%20development)).)  
- **AI Working Groups & Strategy:**  The NZ Government convened a cross-ministry working group on AI in teaching and learning (Feb 2025), signaling strategy development.  The national AI Forum also formed an Education subgroup to promote safe, innovative AI use in schools.  
- **Surveys & Voices:**  NZCER and others are surveying teachers and students (late 2024 onward) to map attitudes toward AI.  Early outreach aims to capture ethics and impact concerns.  Sector commentators note NZ is behind peers (Australia, Singapore, OECD) in formal AI education policy, urging a strategic framework.  Local EdTech leaders highlight AI’s promise to **personalize learning** and automate routine tasks, while stressing that AI *should not* replace human mentorship ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/12/04/from-hype-to-human-flourishing-reimagining-education-in-aotearoas-ai-future/#:~:text=match%20at%20L22%20thinking%2C%20and,Education%20systems%20need%20an%20%E2%80%9Cintelligence)) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/11/new-zealand-teachers-use-ai-more-than-most-oecd-countries/#:~:text=commonly%20use%20it%20to%20summarise,AI%20to%20generate%20lesson%20plans)).  NZ educators recall CEO Jacinda Ardern’s call to treat AI as an “opportunity” supported by robust public education and safeguards.  

**Research Overview**  
- **Teacher & Student Surveys:**  International data show NZ teachers are heavy AI users.  OECD’s 2024 TALIS found 69% of NZ teachers use AI in class—well above the 36% OECD average ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/11/new-zealand-teachers-use-ai-more-than-most-oecd-countries/#:~:text=Over%20two,TALIS%29%202024)).  Common applications for NZ teachers include *summarizing topics (74%)* and generating lesson plans (75%) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/11/new-zealand-teachers-use-ai-more-than-most-oecd-countries/#:~:text=commonly%20use%20it%20to%20summarise,AI%20to%20generate%20lesson%20plans)).  By contrast, only 14% report using AI to mark student work (internationally 26%) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/11/new-zealand-teachers-use-ai-more-than-most-oecd-countries/#:~:text=Conversely%2C%20New%20Zealand%20teachers%20who,26%20percent)), suggesting caution in automated grading.  A NZCER study of 266 primary teachers already interested in AI found nearly half using AI several times weekly or daily ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/08/generative-ai-tools-used-frequently-by-interested-primary-teachers-in-nz/#:~:text=Of%20these%20respondents%2C%20almost%20half,or%20several%20times%20a%20week)).  These teachers integrated AI into regular practice – for example, 82% used AI tools for lesson planning and 66% for assessment design ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/08/generative-ai-tools-used-frequently-by-interested-primary-teachers-in-nz/#:~:text=The%20survey%20found%20that%20most,51%20percent)) – often relying on free (and thus less accurate) AI versions ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/08/generative-ai-tools-used-frequently-by-interested-primary-teachers-in-nz/#:~:text=The%20survey%20also%20found%20that,prone%20to%20error%20and%20bias)).  This underscores that enthusiastic educators find AI useful for saving preparation time and personalizing learning ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/08/generative-ai-tools-used-frequently-by-interested-primary-teachers-in-nz/#:~:text=The%20survey%20found%20that%20most,51%20percent)), even as they note the risks of older AI models.  
- **Academic Perspectives:**  NZ educational researchers and thought leaders advocate cautious innovation.  Experts highlight that an AI-rich classroom flips Bloom’s Taxonomy: students may begin with AI-generated drafts then focus on evaluating, refining, and creating, making critical thinking central ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/12/04/from-hype-to-human-flourishing-reimagining-education-in-aotearoas-ai-future/#:~:text=match%20at%20L40%20Research%20on,in%20critique%2C%20judgement%2C%20reflection%2C%20and)).  Leaders argue that school success in an AI era will hinge on nurturing creativity, empathy, and ethical judgment – equipping students with a “reliable compass” alongside AI tools ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/12/04/from-hype-to-human-flourishing-reimagining-education-in-aotearoas-ai-future/#:~:text=longer%20just%20about%20teaching%20students,and%20emotional%20resources%20to%20act)) ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/12/04/from-hype-to-human-flourishing-reimagining-education-in-aotearoas-ai-future/#:~:text=dynamic%2C%20something%20students%20work%20with%2C,strong%20baseline%20of%20AI%20literacy)).  Publications by NZ universities emphasize that AI could democratize learning (like the printing press) but only if bound by human-led community values and oversight ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/12/04/from-hype-to-human-flourishing-reimagining-education-in-aotearoas-ai-future/#:~:text=longer%20just%20about%20teaching%20students,and%20emotional%20resources%20to%20act)) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=But%20there%20are%20also%20pitfalls,technology%20often%20demonstrates%20cultural%20bias)).  
- **Global Context:**  NZ policy discussions draw on international models.  OECD and UNESCO advise shifting teachers’ roles toward facilitation: automating administrative tasks so educators can focus on students’ unique needs.  UNESCO’s AI in Education frameworks stress co-design with teachers and continuous PD – calls echoed in NZ’s “community of practice” although formal national frameworks are still emerging.  Locally, tech groups highlight incorporating AI ethics and digital literacy into teacher training, and tailoring AI to Māori/Pasifika contexts to avoid reinforcing existing inequities ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=But%20there%20are%20also%20pitfalls,technology%20often%20demonstrates%20cultural%20bias)).  

**Case Studies**  
- **University of Auckland – *Sofia* Tutor:**  UoA trialled “Sofia,” an anthropomorphic chatbot teaching marketing students.  Sofia answers FAQs, gives course info, quizzes learners, and provides analytics to staff on student engagement gaps.  This project won a Silver award for “Best Use of Generative AI in Oceania,” published its findings, and attracted national media attention ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Sofia%20is%20an%20anthropomorphic%20AI,in%20analytics%20give%20teachers)).  
- **Toi Ohomai Institute – Cogniti AI Agents:**  Toi Ohomai used the Cogniti platform to build multiple course-specific chatbots.  Teachers created AI agents for beauty therapy (on-demand revision and note-taking), nursing (mentoring and test prep), and legal studies (simulated law scenarios), using NZ-centric content such as local case studies ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=,agent%20for%20%2019%20simulating)).  These agents deliver 24/7 practice to students and free instructors’ time.  
- **Auckland University of Technology – Research Proposal Assistant:**  AUT implemented a Cogniti-based agent to aid Master’s students crafting research proposals.  The bot aggregates hundreds of example thesis abstracts, then gives students feedback and suggests edits on their draft proposals.  By providing timely review and personalized tips, this tool helps graduate students improve academic writing (outside formal marking) ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=create%20an%20agent%20which%20aids,with%20feedback%20and%20possible%20edits)).  
- **Māori Language AI – kahu.code:**  Aotearoa innovators are injecting te reo Māori into AI.  For example, *kahu.code* develops bilingual text and voice models focused on **reo hangarau** (Māori technical vocabulary).  Its goal is to enable seamless communication between Māori speakers and technology, ensuring AI tools can function accurately in Māori language and support Māori learners ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=kahu,humans%2C%20enabling%20effective%20interactions%20and)).  
- **Vocational Oral Exams (FFCoVE Agent):**  The Food & Fibre Centre of Vocational Excellence commissioned Scarlatti Research to build a voice-based AI interviewer.  This agent generates questions and interacts with students in real time, automatically assessing their spoken responses.  It serves as a low-cost alternative to live oral exams, providing extra practice for rural learners and allowing teachers to devote more time to instruction and pastoral care ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=The%20Food%20and%20Fibre%20Centre,based)).  
- **NZQA Student Chatbot:**  NZQA currently uses an AI-driven chatbot (and phone bot) to answer student enquiries.  The authority plans to upgrade this service with generative AI to enable more natural, conversational support.  The enhanced chatbot is intended to improve NZQA’s helpline efficiency and give students instant, more interactive guidance on qualifications ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=NZQA%20already%20have%20a%20chatbot,it%20can%20be%20more%20conversational)).  

**Trends and Predictions**  
- **Personalized Learning & Teacher Facilitation:**  Experts foresee AI powering highly tailored learning journeys.  Advanced “digital tutors” could adapt content to each student’s pace and interests, while teachers shift toward coaching roles.  In practice, NZ teachers already use AI for summarizing lessons and planning, freeing them for higher-level facilitation ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/11/new-zealand-teachers-use-ai-more-than-most-oecd-countries/#:~:text=commonly%20use%20it%20to%20summarise,AI%20to%20generate%20lesson%20plans)).  However, leaders warn we must “lean into” creative, empathetic, and ethical aspects of teaching – skills AI can’t replace ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/12/04/from-hype-to-human-flourishing-reimagining-education-in-aotearoas-ai-future/#:~:text=match%20at%20L22%20thinking%2C%20and,Education%20systems%20need%20an%20%E2%80%9Cintelligence)) ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/12/04/from-hype-to-human-flourishing-reimagining-education-in-aotearoas-ai-future/#:~:text=match%20at%20L40%20Research%20on,in%20critique%2C%20judgement%2C%20reflection%2C%20and)).  
- **Surge in Teacher Upskilling:**  As classroom AI use grows, systemic training is critical.  Many NZ educators call for wide-reaching professional development in AI literacy.  Universities and PLD providers are starting short courses (e.g. AI Essentials for Educators) and micro-credentials.  A national “community of practice” is forming, following UNESCO’s advice, so teachers can share experience, best practices, and co-design AI tools for their contexts ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/08/generative-ai-tools-used-frequently-by-interested-primary-teachers-in-nz/#:~:text=Of%20these%20respondents%2C%20almost%20half,or%20several%20times%20a%20week)) ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/12/04/from-hype-to-human-flourishing-reimagining-education-in-aotearoas-ai-future/#:~:text=dynamic%2C%20something%20students%20work%20with%2C,strong%20baseline%20of%20AI%20literacy)).  
- **Evolving Curriculum and Policy:**  National policies will evolve quickly.  The introduction of AI-focused subjects in 2028 (and perhaps earlier tech modules) is under way ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/572737/new-push-for-ai-as-education-minister-erica-stanford-announces-curriculum-changes#:~:text=investigating%20having%20a%20new%20Year,for%20later%20development)) ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/572844/professor-calls-for-government-to-begin-ai-education-earlier#:~:text=Canterbury%20University%20Associate%20Professor%20of,about%20AI%20before%20year%2013)).  In parallel, the NZQA and MoE are refining NCEA rules (e.g. allowing AI use with accountability).  Advocacy groups expect new guidance on AI ethics, equity and data use soon.  Without strong leadership and standards, NZ risks falling further behind.  
- **Equity and Māori/Pasifika Focus:**  Aotearoa’s commitment to equity shapes our AI adoption. Analysts emphasize that AI must **not** widen existing gaps – for instance, by ensuring all schools have the tech infrastructure and by subsidizing AI tools for under-resourced communities.  Digital divide concerns loom large; NZ’s current internet disparities could translate into an “AI divide” if unchecked ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=But%20there%20are%20also%20pitfalls,technology%20often%20demonstrates%20cultural%20bias)).  Māori leaders stress culturally appropriate AI content (e.g. the *kahu.code* Māori-language models) and training Māori educators as AI developers.  If done right, AI could help close achievement gaps by offering extra support to struggling learners at scale ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=But%20there%20are%20also%20pitfalls,technology%20often%20demonstrates%20cultural%20bias)).  
- **Next-Generation Assessments:**  From existing trials, we expect broader AI use in student assessment.  Automated essay scoring (already running for Year 10 writing in 2025 ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/#:~:text=In%20May%20this%20year%2C%20over,prepare%20for%20the%20next%20assessment))) and voice-assessment bots may complement traditional exams.  New tools will provide rapid feedback, but also require advanced integrity measures.  NZQA is already exploring AI to detect AI-written work in internal assessments.  Ultimately, assessment design will increasingly integrate AI (e.g. adaptive testing and real-world simulations), balanced by teacher oversight.  
- **Global Collaboration:**  NZ will continue aligning with international trends.  We will learn from global pilots like OpenAI’s teacher tools (e.g. *ChatGPT for Teachers*, free in the US) and UNESCO programs.  More partnerships (EdTech pilots with companies, and UNESCO/EU forums) are expected to guide evidence-based scaling.  Local stakeholders anticipate that ongoing research and public dialogue will inform how and which AI innovations are widely adopted.  

**Conclusion**  
AI in New Zealand education is at a turning point. **Current state:** Usage in schools is still emerging – while students freely experiment with ChatGPT, most teachers are cautiously exploring classroom applications ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/11/new-zealand-teachers-use-ai-more-than-most-oecd-countries/#:~:text=Over%20two,TALIS%29%202024)) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/08/generative-ai-tools-used-frequently-by-interested-primary-teachers-in-nz/#:~:text=Of%20these%20respondents%2C%20almost%20half,or%20several%20times%20a%20week)).  Nevertheless, official interest is high: MoE and NZQA have updated policies, and NZQA already uses AI for exam marking (e.g. 55,000 Year-10 scripts marked by AI in May 2025 ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/#:~:text=In%20May%20this%20year%2C%20over,prepare%20for%20the%20next%20assessment))). Universities and vocational institutes are piloting AI tutors, chatbots, and grading tools.  **Key insights:** AI’s potential is significant – it can personalize learning and ease teachers’ workloads ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/11/new-zealand-teachers-use-ai-more-than-most-oecd-countries/#:~:text=commonly%20use%20it%20to%20summarise,AI%20to%20generate%20lesson%20plans)) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/08/generative-ai-tools-used-frequently-by-interested-primary-teachers-in-nz/#:~:text=The%20survey%20found%20that%20most,51%20percent)) – but it must be wielded with care.  Equity and ethics are paramount: NZ must ensure all learners benefit (for instance by providing Māori-language AI tools and connectivity) and guard against bias ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=But%20there%20are%20also%20pitfalls,technology%20often%20demonstrates%20cultural%20bias)) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=AI%20is%20making%20a%20significant,gain%20traction%20in%20early%202023)).  Academic integrity also remains a concern even as tools like automated scoring show promise.  

**Next steps:** Stakeholders should collaborate on a strategic roadmap. This report is “living”: we will update it with new findings and case study results as they emerge.  Education leaders should share pilot outcomes widely, gather feedback from the classroom, and constantly revise guidelines.  Aligning with UNESCO’s vision, NZ must promote active, human-centered pedagogy alongside AI progress.  By focusing on comprehensive teacher training and an adaptive policy framework, NZ can turn AI from a buzzword into a practical aid – lifting achievement and readiness for a digital future.
