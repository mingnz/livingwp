# AI Adoption in Education in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper

**Introduction**  
Generative AI tools (ChatGPT, Copilot, etc.) have rapidly entered New Zealand (NZ) education. Early media coverage focused on issues like student cheating, but the conversation is now shifting toward integration and opportunity ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=Early%20news%20stories%20on%20AI,find%20its%20place%20in%20education)). UNESCO even themed International Day of Education 2025 around AI, underscoring global attention to this shift. NZ faces challenges (literacy gaps, equity, engagement), and stakeholders see AI as a way to personalize learning and streamline routine tasks. Crucially, educators emphasize AI must *augment* human teaching, not replace it, with humans “in the loop” and guiding outputs ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/08/04/language-learning-and-ai.html#:~:text=However%20Wang%20is%20clear%20that,a%20tool%2C%20not%20a%20teacher)). This whitepaper provides a snapshot of recent developments (late 2024–2025) in AI education in NZ: updates on policy, news highlights, research findings, case studies, and emerging trends.

## Policy & Frameworks  
- **Ministry/NZQA Guidance (2023–2025)**: In 2023 the NZ Ministry of Education and NZQA issued generative-AI guidelines. These urge teachers to *verify AI outputs*, avoid inputting personal or sensitive data, and be alert to cultural biases (since most AI models are trained on dominant-culture data and often lack Māori and Pasifika context) ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai#:~:text=New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20Office%20of%20the,keep%20these%20guidelines%20in%20mind)) ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai#:~:text=Cultural%20bias)). Schools are advised to adapt their digital-use policies: for example, NZ’s Privacy Commissioner issued expectations on safe AI use in schools ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai#:~:text=New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20Office%20of%20the,keep%20these%20guidelines%20in%20mind)), and Netsafe has published AI policy templates for education. All schools offering NCEA standards must include AI in their authenticity (academic integrity) policies ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=Schools%20who%20assess%20listed%20standards,are%20in%20ensuring%20academic%20integrity)) ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai#:~:text=GenAI%20and%20assessment)). The Ministry recommends AI policies cover purpose, scope, data privacy, risk mitigation and regular review, and to mandate teacher professional learning on AI ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CIt%20is%20best%20to%20adopt,Policy%20should%20include)) ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai#:~:text=Talk%20about%20it%20and%20make,a%20policy)). Guidance also suggests framing AI use as an *educative* tool (e.g. acknowledging AI sources in assignments) rather than simply banning it ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CIt%20is%20best%20to%20adopt,Policy%20should%20include)). Despite official guidance and online resources (e.g., the Ministry’s Tahūrangi site and Education Hub materials), many schools are still developing or refining their own AI policies ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=One%20of%20the%20central%20concerns,own%20policies%20around%20the%20technology)). 

- **Academic Integrity**: Emphasizing honesty, NZQA has advised schools to implement academic-integrity programs that include AI. Examples include teaching students about citation of AI-generated content, discussing bias and accuracy issues, and structuring assessments to discourage inappropriate AI use ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=NZQA%E2%80%99s%20school%20relationship%20managers%20are,that%20AI%20is%20prone%20to)) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=the%20school%E2%80%99s%20responsibilities%20are%20in,ensuring%20academic%20integrity)).

- **Teacher Professional Development (PD)**: MoE guidance explicitly lists teacher PD as a key component of responsible AI policy ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai#:~:text=Talk%20about%20it%20and%20make,a%20policy)). In practice, many training initiatives are underway. NGOs and unions have launched workshops and online courses; for example, the start of 2025 saw nationwide AI-in-education conferences featuring international experts, and plans for “AI Master Trainer” programs in NZ ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/02/24/teachers-voice-kicking-off-2025-with-ai-in-education/#:~:text=A%20huge%20thank%20you%20to,helped%20bring%20Holly%20to%20Aotearoa)). However, surveys suggest ongoing PD gaps: one NZCER study found 85% of interested primary teachers want more AI training ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=supplemented%20by%20AI%E2%80%91enabled%20content%20platforms,environmental%20impact%20generative%20AI%20might)). Education leaders are calling for AI literacy to be integrated into initial teacher education and ongoing PLD. 

- **Privacy and Ethics**: Ministry guidance warns against entering student data into AI tools; NZ’s privacy regulator stresses compliance with information principles. Schools are urged to consider ethics (e.g. “What is the purpose of this AI use?”) and Te Tiriti obligations when adopting AI. For instance, NZCER recommends AI frameworks be adapted to **“honour Te Tiriti o Waitangi”** and support Māori/Pasifika learners ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=This%20report%20suggests%20a%20need,tamariki%20in%20Aotearoa%20New%20Zealand)) (recognizing that AI content generation can perpetuate bias). 

## Current News

- **Government Initiatives**: In early 2025 the government signaled increased focus on AI in schools. Education Minister Erica Stanford announced a cross-ministry working group to develop a program on AI in teaching and learning ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/02/24/teachers-voice-kicking-off-2025-with-ai-in-education/#:~:text=AI%20Update%20from%20Our%20Minister,So%20watch%20this%20space)). In August 2025 she publicly endorsed continuing to use AI for assessment, noting NZ’s advanced position on AI marking. Stanford pointed out that NZQA already uses AI grading in literacy and numeracy tests, and that AI-assisted marking allows the new NCEA replacement to proceed without an impossible workload ([www.1news.co.nz](https://www.1news.co.nz/2025/08/05/ai-will-aid-with-ncea-replacement-education-minister-says/#:~:text=Stanford%20said%20New%20Zealand%20will,literacy%20and%20numeracy%20corequisite%20exams)). Concurrently, the national **AI Forum** launched an Education workstream and community-of-practice, and non-government groups (e.g. EDConnect) ran large professor-led AI-in-schools workshops (900+ teachers) featuring international voices like Holly Clark ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/02/24/teachers-voice-kicking-off-2025-with-ai-in-education/#:~:text=What%20an%20incredible%20way%20to,enhanced%20learning%20experiences)) ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/02/24/teachers-voice-kicking-off-2025-with-ai-in-education/#:~:text=AI%20Update%20from%20Our%20Minister,So%20watch%20this%20space)). In short, official signals and industry enthusiasm for AI in education are growing.

- **New Qualifications and Curriculum**: In midschool 2025, NZ announced major NCEA reforms (dropping Level 1, moving to 100-point A–E grades). AI is written into this transition: the proposals rely on AI marking to handle increased assessment volume ([www.1news.co.nz](https://www.1news.co.nz/2025/08/05/ai-will-aid-with-ncea-replacement-education-minister-says/#:~:text=Stanford%20said%20New%20Zealand%20will,literacy%20and%20numeracy%20corequisite%20exams)). In curriculum news, the government revealed new senior subjects including a planned Year-13 Generative AI course ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/572844/professor-calls-for-government-to-begin-ai-education-earlier#:~:text=Speaking%20to%20media%20announcing%20the,AI)). Educators are urging a broader approach, with strength of voice from academics like Kathryn MacCallum, who argues AI and digital literacy should be taught from primary years onward, not just at the final year of school ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/572844/professor-calls-for-government-to-begin-ai-education-earlier#:~:text=Canterbury%20University%20Associate%20Professor%20of,about%20AI%20before%20year%2013)).

- **Surveys and Research Launches**: Research bodies have begun mapping attitudes. Late 2024 saw the NZ Council for Educational Research (NZCER) launch nationwide online surveys of primary (Years 5–8) teachers and students on generative AI use ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/news-and-blogs/nz-primary-school-teachers-years-5-8-share-your-insights-ai-classroom#:~:text=We%20want%20to%20hear%20what,you%20think%20about%20AI)). Early results (reported in 2025) show that many primary teachers are already experimenting with AI (mostly free chatbots) in planning and content generation. Meanwhile, media outlets reported NZ teachers’ high AI usage: an OECD Teaching & Learning International Survey (TALIS) 2024 found **69%** of NZ teachers use AI in education (compared to 36% OECD average) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/11/new-zealand-teachers-use-ai-more-than-most-oecd-countries/#:~:text=Over%20two,TALIS%29%202024)), placing NZ third globally. NZ educators most commonly use AI to summarize topics and generate lesson plans, but far fewer report using it for grading (14% vs 26% internationally) ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/11/new-zealand-teachers-use-ai-more-than-most-oecd-countries/#:~:text=New%20Zealand%20teachers%E2%80%99%20reported%20AI,AI%20to%20generate%20lesson%20plans)). This suggests NZ teachers are eager to adopt AI for planning/support, even as official integration lags.

- **Stakeholder Perspectives**: Commentators note a mixed sentiment. Many NZ educators feel “the education system is falling behind” nations like Australia and Singapore in having a formal AI strategy ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=The%20New%20Zealand%20education%20system,safe%20and%20ethical%20use%20of)). EdTech advocates emphasize optimism: articles in SchoolNews and stakeholder blogs stress using AI to tailor learning, automate routine tasks, and act as a “personal tutor” for each student ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=Early%20news%20stories%20on%20AI,find%20its%20place%20in%20education)) ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,powered%20learning%20experiences)). However, voices also warn that rollout must ensure sufficient devices and connectivity; one analysis cautioned that poor infrastructure could create a two-tier system (AI-rich vs. AI-poor schools) ([theaicorner.co.nz](https://theaicorner.co.nz/p/major-nz-education-reforms-put-ai-at-the-centre#:~:text=,out%20AI%20assessment%20systems%20nationwide)). Media coverage is increasingly balanced between promise and caution: for example, RNZ reported NZQA’s initiative to use AI for generating exam question variations (to cope with uneven computer access) ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/515688/could-ai-be-used-to-help-write-school-exams-nzqa-thinks-so#:~:text=Klinkum%20said%20writing%20exams%20took,variations%20of%20exams%20more%20quickly)), and highlighted internal Ministry documents discussing AI assistance in curriculum writing (for research and consistency) ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/567601/school-curriculum-rewrite-had-serious-problems-managers-considered-using-ai-to-help#:~:text=A%20senior%20manager%27s%20briefing%20to,the%20material%20underpinning%20the%20curriculum)) ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/567601/school-curriculum-rewrite-had-serious-problems-managers-considered-using-ai-to-help#:~:text=Asked%20about%20the%20use%20of,with%20background%20research%20and%20insights)).

## Research Overview

- **Teacher & Student Use**: NZCER’s 2025 report on primary schools found that teachers are “experimenting widely” with generative AI for teaching tasks ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=,prone%20to%20error%20or%20bias)). Most use free LLMs (ChatGPT, Google Gemini) for lesson planning, creating worksheets, and drafting assessments ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=,prone%20to%20error%20or%20bias)). It also found that nearly 75% of these self-selecting teachers rely on only free AI tools (since 85% have no school-funded premium AI access) ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=supplemented%20by%20AI%E2%80%91enabled%20content%20platforms,environmental%20impact%20generative%20AI%20might)), making them vulnerable to outdated or error-prone models. Importantly, 85% of surveyed teachers wanted more AI training ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=supplemented%20by%20AI%E2%80%91enabled%20content%20platforms,environmental%20impact%20generative%20AI%20might)). Among students, about 90% had heard of AI and over half had tried generative tools, typically outside school (for drawing, homework help, etc.) ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=,or%20how%20to%20use%20AI)). Many children view AI as “helpful yet a bit like cheating” ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=%2A%20Self,or%20how%20to%20use%20AI)) and report little guidance from adults. The NZCER authors conclude that NZ needs centrally supported PD, better access to high-quality AI tools, and curriculum-based AI literacy that reflects NZ’s cultural values ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=This%20report%20suggests%20a%20need,tamariki%20in%20Aotearoa%20New%20Zealand)).

- **Educator Perspectives**: A 2024 EdTechNZ blog survey found that most NZ teachers and school leaders were unaware of the MoE’s AI guidelines (three-quarters had never heard of them) ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2024/10/23/teacher-voice-ai-in-education-in-aotearoa-reflections-and-research-insights/#:~:text=While%20AIED%20has%20rapidly%20evolved,a%20culturally%20responsive%20national%20framework)). The same author noted an emerging “AI divide” – some schools are enthusiastic early adopters, while others outright ban all AI, due to inconsistent policy and training gaps ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2024/10/23/teacher-voice-ai-in-education-in-aotearoa-reflections-and-research-insights/#:~:text=is%20still%20lacking,Generative%20AI%20in%20the%20school)). International research is also influencing NZ discourse. For example, a 2023 Harvard study (“Cheating in the Age of Generative AI”) reported no increase in student cheating after ChatGPT’s release, suggesting the focus should shift from policing to productive use. NZ commentators cite this finding while warning that without clear local policies and AI literacy education, misuse could rise ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2024/10/23/teacher-voice-ai-in-education-in-aotearoa-reflections-and-research-insights/#:~:text=Academic%20integrity%20is%20still%20stealing,the%20focus%20on%20AIED%20innovation)).

- **AI in Learning Theory**: Thought leaders outline multiple futures (“paradigms”) for AI education. In one vision, AI provides highly personalized learning journeys (AI-tutors and adaptive content) ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=Paradigm%201%3A%20Personalised%20Learning%20Journeys%3A,could%20be%20a%20valuable%20solution)). Another paradigm sees AI as a collaborative assistant: it handles administrative burdens (like grading and lesson prep) so teachers can mentor students and build relationships ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,powered%20learning%20experiences)). A third emphasizes student-led, AI-empowered learning paths. NZ experts echo these: for instance, University of Auckland education researchers highlight that AI can “democratize education” by custom-fitting instruction, but that human-led communities must remain at the center ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/08/04/language-learning-and-ai.html#:~:text=However%20Wang%20is%20clear%20that,a%20tool%2C%20not%20a%20teacher)) ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,powered%20learning%20experiences)). Global frameworks (from UNESCO, OECD) also recommend shifting teachers’ roles toward facilitation while ensuring co-design of curricula and broad teacher training. NZ research is beginning to adapt these ideas—for example, a University of Auckland AI lab is surveying language teachers nationwide to assess readiness for AI integration (led by Assoc. Prof. Danping Wang, who advocates a human-in-the-loop pedagogy) ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/08/04/language-learning-and-ai.html#:~:text=In%20her%20own%20courses%2C%20where,doing%20course%20assessments%20for%20example)) ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/08/04/language-learning-and-ai.html#:~:text=However%20Wang%20is%20clear%20that,a%20tool%2C%20not%20a%20teacher)).

## Case Studies

- **University of Auckland – “Sofia” AI Tutor**: The Business School developed “Sofia,” a friendly AI chatbot for marketing students ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Sofia%20is%20an%20anthropomorphic%20AI,been%20mentioned%20in%20The%20Conversation)). Sofia can answer course FAQs, administer quizzes and mini-lessons, and hold informal Q&A dialogues in text. Crucially, it logs student interactions, giving instructors analytics on usage and understanding. Early evaluations report that Sofia helps clarify concepts, and the project team won regional AI awards for this innovation ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Sofia%20is%20an%20anthropomorphic%20AI,been%20mentioned%20in%20The%20Conversation)).

- **Toi Ohomai Institute of Technology – Cogniti Agents**: Toi Ohomai (a polytechnic) used the Australian Cogniti platform to create several course-specific AI agents ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Toi%20Ohomai%20Institute%20of%20Technology,may%20not%20be%20limited%20to)). Examples include an AI “revision coach” for beauty therapy using flashcards, a nursing mentor that quizzes and guides students, and a legal practice advisor that simulates law scenarios. These agents automatically review student inputs and give feedback. Toi Ohomai has documented how integrating these bots into classes saved teacher time and helped identify learning gaps ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Toi%20Ohomai%20Institute%20of%20Technology,may%20not%20be%20limited%20to)) ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=,that%20provides%20feedback%20on%20work)).

- **AUT – Research Proposal Assistant**: Auckland University of Technology built a Cogniti-based AI assistant for Master’s students drafting thesis proposals ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Auckland%20University%20of%20Technology%20has,with%20feedback%20and%20possible%20edits)). The tool pulls from a database of past thesis abstracts and provides critique on draft proposals (suggesting edits, highlighting structure issues). While not used for formal evaluation, it demonstrates how AI can give writers immediate feedback and examples, speeding up the research-writing process.

- **Māori Language AI (Kahu.code and Te Hiku Media)**: NZ has invested in AI for te reo Māori. The Kahu.code collective has developed bilingual AI text/voice models (e.g. *Whakawhiti Reo* for translation, *Kaiawhina Reo* for teaching te reo, and *Koro* as a voice assistant) to bridge Māori and English technology use ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=kahu,%E2%80%9D%20Their%20products%20include)). Te Hiku Media, a Māori radio network, launched tools like **Kaituhi** (speech-to-text transcription in te reo), **Reo Ora** (pronunciation aid), and **Papareiō** (text synthesis). These products preserve and promote mātauranga Māori in AI, and could be integrated into education. They highlight a trend: NZ AI in education must respect linguistic diversity. As Danping Wang points out, minority language educators have been underrepresented in AI development, so such projects are vital to ensure Māori voices are included ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/08/04/language-learning-and-ai.html#:~:text=One%20concern%20in%20the%20language,says%2C%20is%20the%20digital%20divide)).

- **Vocational Oral Assessments – Scarlatti Project**: The Food & Fibre Centre of Vocational Excellence has funded Scarlatti Research to create an AI oral-exam agent ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=,Fibre%20Centre%20of%20Vocational%20Excellence)). This system (in development) generates oral exam questions in te reo and English, converses with the student via voice, and automatically grades the responses against competency rubrics. It aims to give rural and part-time vocational students more practice opportunities while reducing the resource burden of live oral exams. Early prototypes suggest learners engage well, and teachers can use the transcripts and scores for follow-up support.

- **NZQA AI Chatbot and Grading Tools**: NZQA, responsible for qualifications, is cautiously deploying AI to help students. It has an existing call-center chatbot and is upgrading it to a generative-AI conversational assistant ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai#:~:text=New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20Office%20of%20the,keep%20these%20guidelines%20in%20mind)) ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=,NZQA)). Separately, RNZ reported in 2024 that NZQA piloted an AI tool to **generate exam question banks** (listing variations of each exam item) ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/515688/could-ai-be-used-to-help-write-school-exams-nzqa-thinks-so#:~:text=Klinkum%20said%20writing%20exams%20took,variations%20of%20exams%20more%20quickly)). NZQA executives also piloted an AI grader: a generative model that initially graded Year-10 literacy and numeracy exams ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=The%20NZQA%20have%20just%20finished,10%20literacy%20and%20numeracy%20assessments)). These pilots show NZQA’s strategy: using AI to scale assessment development and grading, while keeping human oversight.

- **Rosehill College – Education Perfect AI**: At the secondary level, Rosehill College (Auckland) trialled Education Perfect’s AI tutor for senior science classes in 2024 ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=In%202024%2C%20Rosehill%20College%20trialled,approach%20needs%20to%20be%20adjusted)). The AI conducted diagnostic quizzes to place students on NZ curriculum levels and then gave ongoing formative quizzes as the course progressed. Teachers reported it helped them quickly see which concepts needed reteaching. This demonstrates AI’s role in formative assessment and tracking progress within secondary schools.

## Trends and Predictions

- **Personalized Learning Journeys**: Experts envision classrooms where AI gives each student a tailored learning path. For example, one analysis describes *“AI-driven digital textbooks”* that adapt in real-time to a student’s pace and understanding ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=Paradigm%201%3A%20Personalised%20Learning%20Journeys%3A,could%20be%20a%20valuable%20solution)). Rather than one-size-fits-all lessons, AI could routinely adjust tasks and content for individuals. In NZ’s context, this aligns with moves toward data-driven teaching. Synthesizing studies, commentary suggests we may see more individualized tutoring bots or apps, letting students follow personal projects under teacher guidance. However, commentators stress caution: AI must not create a sterile “robotic” factory-line of learning ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=However%2C%20we%20need%20to%20be,in%20an%20age%20of%20AI)). The human touch (creative, relational aspects) remains crucial.

- **Teachers as Facilitators**: A consistent theme is shifting teachers’ time from admin to mentorship. OECD/WEF analyses predict AI will take over grading, scheduling, and routine content creation ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,powered%20learning%20experiences)). In NZ, the new NCEA model (with 100-point A–E grading) already assumes AI will handle much of the marking burden ([www.1news.co.nz](https://www.1news.co.nz/2025/08/05/ai-will-aid-with-ncea-replacement-education-minister-says/#:~:text=Stanford%20said%20New%20Zealand%20will,literacy%20and%20numeracy%20corequisite%20exams)). As one tech analysis put it, AI could free teachers to do what computers **can’t**: coach students, design rich projects, and address social-emotional learning ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,powered%20learning%20experiences)). Many advocate a “human-in-the-loop” model – AI offers suggestions or drafts, but teachers shape the narrative and make final judgments ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/08/04/language-learning-and-ai.html#:~:text=However%20Wang%20is%20clear%20that,a%20tool%2C%20not%20a%20teacher)) ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,powered%20learning%20experiences)).

- **Professional Development Surge**: Given widespread teacher interest and the speed of change, systemic upskilling is expected. NZ education leaders are calling for embedded AI literacy training across teacher education and PLD. Already, the number of AI-related courses and workshops is growing (e.g. AI-focused seminars, online guides). Surveys show strong demand: as noted, 85% of primary teachers in one study wanted more AI training ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=supplemented%20by%20AI%E2%80%91enabled%20content%20platforms,environmental%20impact%20generative%20AI%20might)). Trend-watchers predict formal credentialing (micro-credentials in AI pedagogy) and ongoing teacher communities of practice (per UNESCO advice) will emerge ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2024/10/23/teacher-voice-ai-in-education-in-aotearoa-reflections-and-research-insights/#:~:text=UNESCO%2C%20on%20the%20other%20hand%2C,for%20AI%20integration%20in%20education)) ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/02/24/teachers-voice-kicking-off-2025-with-ai-in-education/#:~:text=In%20the%20meantime%2C%20I%E2%80%99ve%20developed,Please%20register%20your%20interest%20here)).

- **Policy and Curriculum Evolution**: NZ is likely to develop clearer policies and curriculum elements over the next few years. The recent education reform review (including the AI working group) is expected to produce AI-related guidelines (ethics, equity, data use) for schools. We may see AI/ICT competencies written into the refreshed curriculum, especially in technology and digital literacy subjects. (Already, a generative-AI course for Year 13 is under consideration ([www.rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/572844/professor-calls-for-government-to-begin-ai-education-earlier#:~:text=Speaking%20to%20media%20announcing%20the,AI)).) Without such leadership, experts warn NZ risks falling further behind international peers ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=The%20New%20Zealand%20education%20system,safe%20and%20ethical%20use%20of)). 

- **Equity & Inclusion Focus**: NZ’s commitment to inclusivity means AI initiatives must not widen gaps. Analysts emphasize ensuring disadvantaged schools have the hardware and connectivity to use AI tools. Māori and Pasifika education leaders insist AI content be culturally relevant – hence projects like Kahu.code and Te Hiku (above) are vital. NZCER explicitly recommends subsidizing premium AI access for low-decile schools and embedding AI literacy in te reo and Pasifika curricula ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=This%20report%20suggests%20a%20need,tamariki%20in%20Aotearoa%20New%20Zealand)) ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/08/04/language-learning-and-ai.html#:~:text=One%20concern%20in%20the%20language,says%2C%20is%20the%20digital%20divide)). The degree to which AI helps close (or accidentally widen) achievement gaps will be a key measure.

- **Evolving Assessments**: The examples above suggest that more AI-enabled assessment tools will appear. Expect growth in automated writing evaluation, oral exam simulators, and AI-driven adaptive testing in tertiary and vocational settings. However, alongside this, formal integrity measures will evolve: e.g. NZQA and exam boards will use detection algorithms or require evidence of student thought, and schools will refine authenticity policies. In sum, 21st-century assessment may blend AI practice with human oversight.

- **Global Alignment and Innovation**: NZ will continue to align with global trends. International collaboration (OECD, UNESCO, EdTech companies) and competition means NZ schools will test products like ChatGPT-for-Teachers (launched 2025 internationally) and learn from overseas pilots (such as Australia’s co-developed chatbots). UNESCO’s new AI competency frameworks for educators and global research (e.g. AI4K12 guidelines) provide references. NZ’s AI education strategy will likely weave in these international standards, adapting them to local contexts.

## Conclusion

AI in NZ education is at an inflection point. **Current state:** Adoption in practice is still very uneven – students already experiment with AI (in and out of school) and many teachers use it informally ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/11/new-zealand-teachers-use-ai-more-than-most-oecd-countries/#:~:text=Over%20two,TALIS%29%202024)), but official integration is nascent. The government and institutions have made first moves (guidelines, pilot projects, working groups), and universities/voc-tech schools are innovating with tutors and chatbots. 

**Key insights:** 
- *Promise:* AI can help personalize learning and reduce teacher workload (e.g. automated grading and content creation) ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,powered%20learning%20experiences)). It dovetails with NZ’s curriculum emphasis on differentiated, data-informed teaching. If used wisely, AI could boost achievement for students who need extra support and allow teachers to focus on high-value tasks ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,powered%20learning%20experiences)) ([www.1news.co.nz](https://www.1news.co.nz/2025/08/05/ai-will-aid-with-ncea-replacement-education-minister-says/#:~:text=Stanford%20said%20New%20Zealand%20will,literacy%20and%20numeracy%20corequisite%20exams)).  
- *Challenges:* Uptake has been slow without clear leadership ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=The%20New%20Zealand%20education%20system,safe%20and%20ethical%20use%20of)). Many schools still lack the devices, networks and training needed. Ethical concerns persist over bias (especially cultural bias) and academic integrity. New research warns the rollout must prioritize equity – ensuring Māori/Pasifika contexts are served ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/08/04/language-learning-and-ai.html#:~:text=One%20concern%20in%20the%20language,says%2C%20is%20the%20digital%20divide)) ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools#:~:text=This%20report%20suggests%20a%20need,tamariki%20in%20Aotearoa%20New%20Zealand)). Trust is also an issue: one tech analysis noted NZ has among the **lowest global trust in AI** ([theaicorner.co.nz](https://theaicorner.co.nz/p/major-nz-education-reforms-put-ai-at-the-centre#:~:text=The%20success%20of%20this%20transformation,AI%20is%20trained%20and%20used)), so authorities must communicate transparently and involve teachers closely.  
- *Next steps:* Stakeholders should develop a strategic roadmap. This means investing in infrastructure (devices, bandwidth), mandating teacher PD, supporting Māori-led AI projects, and forming collaborations between schools, researchers and tech providers. Ongoing case-study sharing and evaluation will guide scale-up. Global experts like Jacinda Ardern advise framing AI as an opportunity, not a threat, and proceeding in small, open steps ([www.1news.co.nz](https://www.1news.co.nz/2025/08/05/ai-will-aid-with-ncea-replacement-education-minister-says/#:~:text=AI%20marking%20was%20as%20good%2C,than%20human%20marking%2C%20she%20said)) ([theaicorner.co.nz](https://theaicorner.co.nz/p/major-nz-education-reforms-put-ai-at-the-centre#:~:text=The%20success%20of%20this%20transformation,AI%20is%20trained%20and%20used)). This living whitepaper will be updated as NZ’s education sector learns from pilots and global experience, ensuring leaders have up-to-date insights for policy and practice.
