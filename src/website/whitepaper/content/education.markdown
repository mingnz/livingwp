# AI in Education in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper  
*Updated for publication on 9 March 2026*

## Executive Snapshot

AI adoption in New Zealand education is now best understood as **broadly established but unevenly governed**. In schools, use is strongest in teacher workflow support, resource generation, feedback, and classroom experimentation. In tertiary education, the center of gravity has shifted away from AI-detection tools and toward assessment redesign, staff guidance, and institutional governance. At system level, NZQA has already moved beyond pilots into scaled operational deployment, using automated text scoring in national literacy assessments and expanding AI-enabled customer support. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

Three sector-wide realities stand out as of **9 March 2026**. First, school policy settings are clearer than they were a year ago: the Ministry of Education requires schools with consent to assess to include acceptable GenAI use in authenticity policy, says GenAI is not permitted in NCEA external assessment, and frames AI marking around teacher responsibility, human oversight, and appropriate use cases. Second, AI is now visible in live curriculum architecture, with consultation on the Years 0–10 draft curriculum open until **24 April 2026** and draft Years 9–10 technology content explicitly covering algorithms, data, logic, and AI. Third, evidence gathering is catching up with practice: the Ministry’s 3 March 2026 school leaders bulletin says ERO is actively surveying teachers and leaders about current AI use in schools. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

## What This Edition Adds

Compared with the supplied draft, this edition adds stronger evidence that **tertiary education is moving into a governance phase**. The University of Otago’s institution-wide AI Governance Policy is scheduled to take effect on **10 March 2026**, setting a formal university-wide framework for responsible AI oversight. The University of Auckland has also established an AI Education Advisory Group and an AI in Education Action Plan to guide AI use in teaching, learning, and curriculum design. At the same time, NZQA’s monitoring of tertiary moderation systems shows many providers still do **not** explicitly cover generative AI in assessment and moderation policy, underscoring how uneven maturity remains across the sector. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/administration/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy?utm_source=openai))

## Overall State of Adoption

### Schools: widespread use, cautious formalisation

The strongest national benchmark remains **TALIS 2024**. It found that nearly two-thirds of New Zealand Year 1–10 teachers had used AI in some form in the previous year, and **69%** of New Zealand Year 7–10 teachers had used AI compared with **36%** across the OECD lower-secondary average. Among New Zealand teachers who used AI, the most common uses were generating lesson plans (**78%**) and learning about or summarising a topic (**73%**), while only **12%** reported using AI to assess student work. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

That pattern is strategically important. Adoption is strongest where teacher oversight remains straightforward and stakes are lower: planning, drafting, adaptation, and support. Ministry guidance aligns with this pattern by insisting that teachers remain central, retain responsibility for learning, and use AI to support rather than replace professional judgement. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### Tertiary: less faith in detection, more emphasis on redesign and governance

In higher education, the earlier emphasis on “catching” AI misuse is weakening. RNZ reported on **30 September 2025** that several universities had stopped using AI-detection software, with Auckland having earlier decided it could not definitively prove AI use, and Massey judging detection unreliable. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/574517/universities-give-up-using-software-to-detect-ai-in-students-work/))

The replacement model is still forming, but its outlines are clear. Victoria University of Wellington’s law faculty required some exams to be handwritten in **May 2025** because of concerns about AI-enabled cheating through laptops, while Lincoln University required about **120** postgraduate finance students to explain code in person after AI-assisted misconduct concerns in **August 2025**. These responses show a shift toward in-person validation, oral defence, secured tasks, and assessment redesign. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/560059/victoria-law-students-not-allowed-laptops-in-exams-to-prevent-ai-cheating?utm_source=openai))

At the governance level, the picture is maturing but uneven. Otago’s AI Governance Policy, effective **10 March 2026**, formalises institution-wide oversight and requires AI use to respect Aotearoa New Zealand’s cultural context, Te Ao Māori principles, and Māori/Pacific data sovereignty. The University of Auckland’s AI in Education Action Plan similarly signals that at least some universities are moving from local experimentation to coordinated institutional strategy. But NZQA’s moderation monitoring shows many tertiary providers still do not explicitly cover generative AI in their assessment and moderation policies, indicating that sector capability is advancing faster than sector consistency. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/administration/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy))

### System agencies: the most operationally mature AI deployment

On current evidence, the most mature AI deployment in New Zealand education sits with **NZQA**. NZQA says it used an AI-powered Automated Text Scoring tool to mark more than **55,000** writing literacy assessments in **May 2025**, enabling results to be returned **3.5 weeks earlier** than the previous year. It also says a 2024 large-scale trial on around **35,000–36,000** writing responses found the tool as reliable as human markers, with boundary cases still checked by human markers. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

NZQA is also using AI in service delivery. Its Awhina chatbot has been upgraded with generative AI capability, with the first upgraded phase live in the international section of the website, and NZQA says its AI tool supports **over 250,000** student and customer interactions each year. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/our-website-chatbot-awhina-is-growing/?utm_source=openai))

## Policy, Governance, and Curriculum

### Ministry of Education position

The Ministry’s guidance is now substantially clearer than the sector’s first-wave 2023 response. It tells schools not to enter personal data into public AI tools, requires acceptable-use coverage in authenticity policy, warns about bias and inaccuracy, and says AI in education should support knowledgeable teachers rather than displace them. For AI marking, its framework centers on **teacher responsibility and professional judgement**, **human oversight**, and **appropriate use cases**. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

For assessment, the Ministry remains restrictive in high-stakes contexts. It states that GenAI is **not permitted in NCEA external assessment**, and that any school with consent to assess must include acceptable GenAI use in assessment policy. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### Privacy and Māori data sovereignty

Privacy and sovereignty are no longer side issues; they are now core adoption tests. The Office of the Privacy Commissioner expects organisations considering generative AI to secure senior approval, test necessity and proportionality, conduct a Privacy Impact Assessment, be transparent about use, and seek feedback from affected communities including Māori. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/ai/generative-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

Tāhūrangi’s AI resource reinforces that New Zealand’s approach is distinctively shaped by Te Tiriti. It says Māori data sovereignty is a significant consideration under Te Tiriti o Waitangi, that Māori data is a taonga, and that AI cannot replace engagement with local whānau, hapū, and iwi. ([newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/introduction-to-artificial-intelligence/5637235331.p?utm_source=openai))

### Curriculum direction

AI now has clearer curriculum visibility than in earlier iterations of the refresh. Consultation on the Years 0–10 draft curriculum is open until **Friday, 24 April 2026**, and the draft Years 9–10 technology learning area explicitly says computer science focuses on “algorithms, data, and logic in digital systems and AI,” including the ethical implications of intelligent technologies. ([newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/nzc---years-0-10-draft-curriculum-consultation/5637305076.p))

At senior secondary level, the implementation horizon remains gradual. Tāhūrangi says there is no formal requirement to use the new Years 11–13 curriculum content until **2028 for Year 11**, **2029 for Year 12**, and **2030 for Year 13**. ([tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://tahurangi.education.govt.nz/senior-secondary-subjects?utm_source=openai))

## Research Overview

### National teacher-use evidence

TALIS 2024 shows New Zealand teachers are ahead of many international peers in AI use, but also more alert to risk. New Zealand Year 7–10 teachers were more likely than OECD peers to see AI as useful for improving lesson plans and adapting materials, but they were also more likely to worry that AI enables students to misrepresent others’ work as their own and may produce incorrect recommendations. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

### Primary-school evidence

NZCER’s 2025 study of primary schools provides a sharper view of early adoption. It reports survey data from **266** primary teachers and **147** students across four case-study schools, concluding that AI is already in use by both teachers and students while gaps remain in policy, resourcing, and professional learning. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/Generative%20artificial%20intelligence%20in%20Aotearoa%20New%20Zealand%20primary%20schools%E2%80%94Teacher%20and%20student%20survey%20findings.pdf))

Among surveyed primary teachers using AI, the most common tasks were lesson planning (**82%**), assessment design (**66%**), personalising learning (**65%**), research for teaching (**59%**), and writing student feedback reports (**51%**). But **68%** had observed inaccuracies in AI-generated information, fewer than half felt confident teaching responsible AI use, and **85%** wanted more training. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/Generative%20artificial%20intelligence%20in%20Aotearoa%20New%20Zealand%20primary%20schools%E2%80%94Teacher%20and%20student%20survey%20findings.pdf))

The same report shows why “wait and see” is not a viable school strategy. Across the four schools, **90%** of students had heard of AI, more than half reported some use of generative AI tools, and self-reported use was higher **outside school than inside**. It also found that most teachers whose schools had or were developing AI policy were unsure whether Māori data sovereignty had been considered. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/Generative%20artificial%20intelligence%20in%20Aotearoa%20New%20Zealand%20primary%20schools%E2%80%94Teacher%20and%20student%20survey%20findings.pdf))

## Case Studies

### 1) Aotea College: school-wide process design

Aotea College created a staff AI group and moved from ad hoc concern to system design. Its response included updated authenticity statements, a staff handbook covering referencing and verbal questioning, staff professional learning, student information sessions, and guidelines for referencing GenAI in assessments. Teachers were encouraged to add checkpoints, supervise more assessment stages, know each student’s style, and use verbal questioning when authenticity concerns arose. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Aotea%20College_MAR2025.pdf))

### 2) Hobsonville Point Secondary School: checkpoints over detectors

Hobsonville Point built school-wide assessment templates with checkpoint due dates, required original working documents with full version history, used verbal checks of understanding, and paired these controls with restorative conversations involving families where needed. The Ministry case study says these interventions led to a **significant drop** in inappropriate AI use in assessments. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Hobsonville%20Point%20Secondary%20School_MAR2025.pdf?VersionId=.9dochWl4SsdLrmFkwawNSFNaw_SoYAW))

### 3) New Windsor School and the Ako Hiko cluster: primary capability-building

Education Gazette’s profile of New Windsor School shows AI use moving beyond isolated teacher experimentation into cluster-supported practice. The school’s pilot integrates AI into curriculum work around cyber-safety, creativity, and critical engagement, while leadership and kaiako also completed an NZQA AI micro-credentials course. ([gazette.education.govt.nz](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/articles/ai-approach-at-new-windsor-school-prepares-akonga-for-the-future/?utm_source=openai))

### 4) NZQA: AI in national assessment operations

NZQA’s automated writing scoring remains the clearest example of production-grade AI in New Zealand education. Unlike many school pilots, this is already embedded in a nationally significant assessment workflow, with human moderation retained for assurance and boundary decisions. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

### 5) University of Auckland: service AI and culturally grounded learning AI

The University of Auckland’s AI Assistant now provides **24/7** responses, facilitates more than **9,000 conversations** and **60,000 searches** each month, and helps users navigate information spread across **90+** university domains. Separately, the University is leading a **$1 million**, three-year project to build an AI-powered te reo Māori pronunciation coach with real-time personalised feedback, in partnership with Te Hiku Media and grounded in tikanga Māori and Indigenous data sovereignty. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/10/07/ai-assistant-enhances-digital-experience-at-uoa.html))

## Emerging Trends

### 1) Augmentation is winning over substitution

Across the sector, the most durable uses are still augmentative: lesson planning, summarisation, feedback support, language learning, helpdesks, and service navigation. Human judgement remains central in the most sensitive domains. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

### 2) Assessment redesign is replacing forensic detection

The most credible integrity responses now rely less on detectors and more on checkpoints, version history, oral explanation, supervised stages, and redesigned tasks. The direction is consistent across schools, universities, and NZQA guidance. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Aotea%20College_MAR2025.pdf))

### 3) Governance is becoming the real differentiator

The next dividing line is no longer “uses AI” versus “does not use AI.” It is whether an institution has workable governance around privacy, culture, assessment, oversight, and accountability. Otago’s new governance framework, Auckland’s action-planning approach, and NZQA’s findings on moderation-policy gaps all point to this next phase. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/administration/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy?utm_source=openai))

### 4) AI literacy is moving toward mainstream status

Day of AI Aotearoa aims to make free foundational AI literacy available to every school, teacher, and learner by the end of **Term 1 2026**, while Kōtui Ako’s nationwide AI Digital Literacy offering opened registrations for classes beginning **9 March 2026**. Combined with curriculum refresh work, these are signs that AI literacy is becoming part of mainstream capability-building rather than a fringe digital-tech topic. ([dayofai.org](https://dayofai.org/program-hubs/new-zealand-aotearoa))

### 5) New Zealand’s AI-in-education model is becoming more culturally specific

Aotearoa’s approach is increasingly distinguished by explicit treatment of Te Tiriti, Māori data sovereignty, and te reo Māori revitalisation. That does not mean these principles are fully embedded in practice; NZCER’s research suggests many educators still cannot say whether they have been. But it does mean responsible AI in New Zealand education is being defined more broadly than efficiency, integrity, or productivity alone. ([newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/introduction-to-artificial-intelligence/5637235331.p?utm_source=openai))

## Conclusion

As of **9 March 2026**, AI adoption in education in New Zealand is best described as **active, uneven, and maturing**. It is active because teacher use is already widespread, NZQA has production AI systems in place, and AI literacy initiatives are scaling nationally. It is uneven because governance, confidence, and policy maturity vary sharply between schools, universities, and tertiary providers. And it is maturing because the conversation has shifted from novelty and panic to workflow design, assessment credibility, privacy, and institutional oversight. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

The strategic direction is now clearer. New Zealand is not moving toward a model in which AI replaces educators. It is moving toward one in which AI supports planning, tutoring, language learning, service delivery, and selected assessment operations, while human judgement, authenticity, cultural legitimacy, and data governance become more explicit design requirements. On the available evidence, the institutions making the fastest progress are those treating AI as a whole-system change issue rather than a standalone tool choice. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

The core question for the next phase is no longer whether AI has entered New Zealand education. It has. The real question is whether the sector can scale use in ways that are educationally credible, culturally grounded, and operationally trustworthy. Current evidence suggests that transition is underway, but not yet complete. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/administration/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy?utm_source=openai))
