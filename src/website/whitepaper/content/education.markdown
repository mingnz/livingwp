# AI Adoption in Education in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper

## Introduction  
Artificial intelligence (AI) is rapidly transforming education worldwide, and New Zealand is actively exploring its implications. Notably, UNESCO dedicated International Education Day 2025 to AI, signaling the global importance of this shift ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=AI%20is%20making%20a%20significant,gain%20traction%20in%20early%202023)). In NZ, early media coverage emphasized *student misuse* of AI (“cheating”), but the conversation has quickly pivoted to **integration** – how AI can enhance learning rather than replace educators ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=Early%20news%20stories%20on%20AI,find%20its%20place%20in%20education)). New Zealand faces persistent challenges (e.g. low literacy rates in decile-1 schools), and AI tools promise **personalized support** to bridge such gaps. For instance, a local AI-driven literacy program reportedly *reversed* decades of declining literacy outcomes in trial classes ([m.scoop.co.nz](https://m.scoop.co.nz/stories/SC2506/S00007/ai-driven-structured-literacy-programme-delivers-impressive-results-in-nz-schools.htm#:~:text=While%20the%20past%20five%20years,confidence%2C%20and%20strong%20academic%20outcomes)) ([m.scoop.co.nz](https://m.scoop.co.nz/stories/SC2506/S00007/ai-driven-structured-literacy-programme-delivers-impressive-results-in-nz-schools.htm#:~:text=Yet%2C%20these%20statistics%20have%20been,efficacy%2C%20and%20engagement%20all%20lift)). Experts caution that these tools should *augment* human teaching: AI can be a “personal tutor” or assistant, but the human-led, relational aspects of education (mentorship, community learning) must be preserved ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2024/12/11/integrating-ai-and-academia.html#:~:text=Generative%20AI%20is%20a%20disruptive,is%20the%20purpose%20of%20the)). 

## Policy & Frameworks  
Government guidance and policies have been emerging to help schools navigate AI:  
- **Verification & Safety:** Ministry guidelines urge teachers to *verify AI outputs*, avoid submitting personal data to AI tools, and be alert to cultural bias ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai/#:~:text=Some%20educators%20are%20already%20using,about%20this%20linked%20to%20below)). Schools are advised to audit existing privacy and ICT policies and adapt them for AI (e.g. Netsafe’s AI policy template) ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai/#:~:text=Some%20educators%20are%20already%20using,about%20this%20linked%20to%20below)).  
- **Academic Integrity:** The MOE and NZQA require all NCEA schools to explicitly address AI in their authenticity/assessment policies. Official advice recommends schools clearly define the *purpose and scope* of AI use and instill principles of honest AI use in students ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=Schools%20who%20assess%20listed%20standards,are%20in%20ensuring%20academic%20integrity)). Teachers are encouraged to take a practical, educative approach (“adopt a practical, educative approach to AI”) when guiding learners ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=Schools%20who%20assess%20listed%20standards,are%20in%20ensuring%20academic%20integrity)).  
- **Professional Development:** To build capacity, PLD providers and tertiary institutions now offer AI training for educators. The Ministry notes that “PLD providers…are offering short courses and learning opportunities for teachers about genAI tools,” along with online guides and exemplars ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai/#:~:text=PLD%20providers%20and%20tertiary%20institutions,specific%20learning%20areas%20or%20approaches)). For example, local teacher-led resources (e.g. ChatGPT guides by Selwyn College’s Kit Willett) and workshops are helping build AI literacy.  
- **Privacy & Compliance:** NZ’s Privacy Commissioner has issued expectations for responsible use of generative AI. Schools are reminded to treat student data carefully – e.g. comply with the Privacy Act, avoid inputting personal information into AI services ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai/#:~:text=New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20Office%20of%20the,teachers%20should%20be%20mindful%20of)). The MOE’s security frameworks (e.g. Security & Privacy in Software reports) are also starting to flag risks of AI apps in schools. Leaders are advised to formally approve any AI systems, per the Office of the Privacy Commissioner’s recommendations ([www.education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai/#:~:text=New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20Office%20of%20the,teachers%20should%20be%20mindful%20of)).  

## Current News  
- **International Focus:** The 2025 UNESCO International Day of Education theme was AI, highlighting its transformational role ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=AI%20is%20making%20a%20significant,gain%20traction%20in%20early%202023)). Global thought leadership (e.g. OECD, UNESCO frameworks) emphasizes co-designing AI policy with teachers and focusing on human agency. Locally, educators stress leveraging this momentum.  
- **Government Initiatives:** In early 2025, Education Minister Erica Stanford announced a cross‐ministry working group to plan AI in teaching and learning ([nztech.org.nz](https://nztech.org.nz/2025/02/25/teachers-voice-kicking-off-2025-with-ai-in-education/#:~:text=AI%20Update%20from%20Our%20Minister,So%20watch%20this%20space)).  This follows the NZ AI Forum’s formation of a dedicated Education subgroup to accelerate safe, innovative AI use in schools ([nztech.org.nz](https://nztech.org.nz/2025/02/25/teachers-voice-kicking-off-2025-with-ai-in-education/#:~:text=AI%20Forum%E2%80%99s%20Commitment%20to%20Empowering,AI%E2%80%99s%20benefits%20reach%20all%20learners)). Such moves signal that AI will feature in upcoming education strategy reviews.  
- **Surveys & Research:** The NZ Council for Educational Research (NZCER) launched nationwide surveys in late 2024, inviting Year 5–8 teachers and students to share their experiences and attitudes about AI in the classroom ([www.nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/news-and-blogs/nz-primary-school-teachers-years-5-8-share-your-insights-ai-classroom#:~:text=NZCER%20is%20conducting%20online%20surveys,will%20contribute%20to%20this%20research)). These surveys aim to gauge current use, expectations, and concerns, informing future policy. Early feedback indicates teachers want more clear direction and training on AI.  
- **EdTech Activity:** The Kiwi EdTech sector is active. For example, EDConnect’s January 2025 “AI in Education” conferences engaged ~900 NZ educators (in Hamilton, Taupō, Nelson, Wellington), featuring international speaker Holly Clark. This indicates high teacher interest in practical AI applications ([nztech.org.nz](https://nztech.org.nz/2025/02/25/teachers-voice-kicking-off-2025-with-ai-in-education/#:~:text=What%20an%20incredible%20way%20to,enhanced%20learning%20experiences)). A homegrown edtech, *Teacher’s Buddy*, launched a curriculum-aligned AI platform for NZ classrooms – offering assistive tools for lesson planning, marking, and creating differentiated content tailored to NZ curriculum and NCEA standards ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/06/24/edtech-in-the-spotlight-teachers-buddy-a-homegrown-ai-assistant-built-for-kiwi-classrooms/#:~:text=This%20curriculum,supports%20learners%20at%20all%20levels)). The platform, developed with teacher collaboration (including PPTA and MoE support), exemplifies NZ innovation leveraging AI to reduce admin workload while upholding teacher judgment ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/06/24/edtech-in-the-spotlight-teachers-buddy-a-homegrown-ai-assistant-built-for-kiwi-classrooms/#:~:text=This%20curriculum,supports%20learners%20at%20all%20levels)).  
- **Classroom Use & Attitudes:** Observers note that many NZ schools have allowed tools like ChatGPT informally, leading to questions about fairness and integrity. Domestic news pieces reflect a shift: experts now discuss AI as a *“personal tutor”* that can help under-resourced schools catch up, rather than just a cheating risk ([www.schoolnews.co.nz](https://www.schoolnews.co.nz/2025/06/ai-and-the-future-of-education/#:~:text=Early%20news%20stories%20on%20AI,find%20its%20place%20in%20education)) ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Sofia%20is%20an%20anthropomorphic%20AI,been%20mentioned%20in%20The%20Conversation)). For example, early adopters of a NZ-developed AI literacy tutor (Writer’s Toolbox) report *higher student engagement and confidence* in writing ([m.scoop.co.nz](https://m.scoop.co.nz/stories/SC2506/S00007/ai-driven-structured-literacy-programme-delivers-impressive-results-in-nz-schools.htm#:~:text=While%20the%20past%20five%20years,confidence%2C%20and%20strong%20academic%20outcomes)) ([m.scoop.co.nz](https://m.scoop.co.nz/stories/SC2506/S00007/ai-driven-structured-literacy-programme-delivers-impressive-results-in-nz-schools.htm#:~:text=Yet%2C%20these%20statistics%20have%20been,efficacy%2C%20and%20engagement%20all%20lift)), illustrating AI’s potential to lift achievement if used thoughtfully. Commentators also point out that NZ currently trails countries like Australia and Singapore in formal AI-education policy ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=The%20New%20Zealand%20education%20system,safe%20and%20ethical%20use%20of)), spurring calls for a cohesive national strategy.

## Research & Analysis  
- **Education Leadership:** Academic and sector analyses describe several future scenarios. NZ tertiary experts describe generative AI as “the most significant democratization of education since the printing press,” but warn that maintaining human-led, community-based learning is vital ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2024/12/11/integrating-ai-and-academia.html#:~:text=Generative%20AI%20is%20a%20disruptive,is%20the%20purpose%20of%20the)). A University of Auckland report argues universities must evolve: credentialing remains valuable, but the emphasis should be on deep, relational learning that AI alone can’t replicate ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2024/12/11/integrating-ai-and-academia.html#:~:text=Generative%20AI%20is%20a%20disruptive,is%20the%20purpose%20of%20the)) ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2024/12/11/integrating-ai-and-academia.html#:~:text=One%20of%20the%20core%20functions,Many%20great)).  
- **International Insights:** Global frameworks (UNESCO, OECD) and reports stress rebalancing teacher roles. They envision AI handling administrative burdens (grading, schedule, routine tutoring) so that teachers can become coaches and mentors ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,powered%20learning%20experiences)). One NZ technology editor outlines three paradigms: from factory-style AI-led learning to a collaborative partner model to fully personalized AI mentors. The consensus is that NZ education needs to move beyond one-way content delivery (Paradigm 1) toward systems where teachers skillfully integrate AI (Paradigms 2–3) ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=Paradigm%201%3A%20Personalised%20Learning%20Journeys%3A,could%20be%20a%20valuable%20solution)) ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,powered%20learning%20experiences)).  
- **Teacher Preparedness:** Surveys (global and anecdotal) find many NZ teachers feel unprepared. For example, a recent (mid-2024) research effort found NZ secondary leaders reported *“very slow”* AI uptake in their schools, with many unaware of the Ministry’s genAI guidelines. Teachers overwhelmingly desired a clear national framework and more AI-specific training. This echoes UNESCO recommendations that emphasize ongoing teacher AI literacy and communities of practice. NZ universities (e.g. Auckland’s August 2024 AI Symposium) have begun faculty training on AI pedagogy and ethics.  
- **Equity & Inclusion:** Researchers caution that AI could widen existing gaps if not managed. NZ analysts urge efforts to ensure rural, low-decile and Māori/Pasifika schools have access to AI tools and relevant content. There is active work on culturally responsive AI: projects are emerging that develop te reo Māori AI models and bilingual interfaces to include indigenous perspectives (see case studies below). Future studies in NZ are expected to monitor AI access disparities and strive for a “head start” in narrowing the digital divide ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=Closing%20the%20digital%20divide%3A%20We,use%20it%20and%20those%20without)).  

## Case Studies & Pilot Projects  
- **University of Auckland – “Sofia” AI Tutor:** Waipapa Taumata Rau (University of Auckland) has piloted an anthropomorphic AI assistant for a marketing course ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Sofia%20is%20an%20anthropomorphic%20AI,been%20mentioned%20in%20The%20Conversation)). “Sofia” answers common student questions, quizzes students on content, and provides 24/7 help via chat. It also tracks student interactions, giving instructors analytics on where students struggle. The Sofia team won an Oceania AI award and published their findings, demonstrating how AI tutoring and analytics can be blended into tertiary teaching ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Sofia%20is%20an%20anthropomorphic%20AI,been%20mentioned%20in%20The%20Conversation)).  
- **Toi Ohomai Institute (Te Pūkenga) – Cogniti Course Agents:** Toi Ohomai has built multiple AI agents using the Cogniti platform, each tailored to a specific programme ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Toi%20Ohomai%20Institute%20of%20Technology,may%20not%20be%20limited%20to)). For example, beauty therapy students use an AI revision assistant; nursing students have a mentoring/chatbot for test prep; and law classes have a scenario simulator with a virtual legal advisor. Localizing content with NZ legal cases and technical terms makes these agents culturally relevant. Toi Ohomai researchers have documented pilot evaluations (both papers and videos) showing positive student feedback and integration strategies ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Relevance%20for%20us%3A)). These pilots illustrate AI’s use in supplementing face-to-face instruction across diverse vocational subjects.  
- **AUT (Auckland University of Technology) – Research Proposal Helper:** AUT’s faculty developed an AI agent to assist postgrad nursing and science students drafting thesis proposals ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Auckland%20University%20of%20Technology%20has,with%20feedback%20and%20possible%20edits)). The agent draws on a database of previous theses’ abstracts and gives feedback and suggested edits on drafts. While not a graded tool, it exemplifies how AI can facilitate writing skills: it compares student work to exemplars and helps refine structure. This prototype shows the potential of generative AI to scaffold research skills at tertiary level ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=Auckland%20University%20of%20Technology%20has,with%20feedback%20and%20possible%20edits)).  
- **Māori Language AI – kahu.code:** The indigenous tech venture *kahu.code* has launched bilingual AI tools to support te reo Māori learning and use ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=kahu,%E2%80%9D%20Their%20products%20include)). Their products include *Whakawhiti Reo* (a te reo translator), *Kaiawhina Reo* (a te reo tutor bot), and *Koro* (an AI voice assistant speaking Māori) ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=kahu,%E2%80%9D%20Their%20products%20include)). Developed with a focus on “reo hangarau” (technology vocabulary) and Māori pedagogy, these agents address the gap that mainstream AI often lacks Māori language competency. They exemplify NZ’s commitment to inclusivity, enabling Māori learners to interact with AI in their own language ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=kahu,%E2%80%9D%20Their%20products%20include)).  
- **Vocational Oral Assessment (Scarlatti/FFCoVE):** The Food & Fibre Centre of Vocational Excellence is funding a Scarlatti Research project to create an AI oral-assessment agent. This voice-driven AI will simulate an oral exam for, say, primary industry qualifications: it generates questions, engages in spoken dialogue with learners, and automatically grades responses. The goal is to give students (especially in remote or trade settings) realistic oral exam practice and reduce strain on teachers’ time. This pilot, an example of generative AI for assessment, is still in development ([www.scarlatti.co.nz](https://www.scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/assessment-possibilities-different-types-of-ai-for-assessment/#:~:text=With%20the%20support%20of%20the,haves%E2%80%99)).  
- **NZQA Chatbots and AI Grading:** The NZ Qualifications Authority (NZQA) is experimenting with generative AI. In late 2024-25 it piloted an AI tool to provide *preliminary grades* on NCEA literacy and numeracy exams (trialed on Year 10 papers) ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=The%20NZQA%20have%20just%20finished,10%20literacy%20and%20numeracy%20assessments)). Separately, NZQA’s student helpdesk (Awhina chatbot) is being upgraded to use generative language models, aiming for more natural, conversational support for inquiries ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=NZQA%20already%20have%20a%20chatbot,it%20can%20be%20more%20conversational)). These initiatives show the NZQA engaging AI to improve assessment efficiency and student services while carefully monitoring accuracy.  

## Trends and Predictions  
- **Personalized Learning Journeys:** Experts anticipate wider use of AI-driven *adaptive learning paths* in NZ classrooms. AI could dynamically tailor content to each student’s level and interests ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=Paradigm%201%3A%20Personalised%20Learning%20Journeys%3A,could%20be%20a%20valuable%20solution)) – for example, delivering customized digital textbooks or exercises in real-time. This promises to address one-size-fits-all challenges and help teachers differentiate. By the late 2020s, many predict common features like AI-curated lesson modules and on-demand “digital tutors” that guide students individually.  
- **Teachers as Facilitators:** Consistent with OECD/WEF views, NZ trend-watchers foresee AI taking over routine tasks (grading, scheduling, drill exercises), freeing teachers to focus on mentorship, creative lesson design, and student well-being ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,powered%20learning%20experiences)). The teacher’s role is expected to evolve into that of a coach who helps students critically engage with AI-provided material. NZ thought leaders emphasize that AI adoption should *augment* teacher professionalism (and not lead to a “robot-led” factory model). Systemic upskilling is predicted: many call for embedding AI literacy in teacher training and creating communities of practice to share effective AI integration methods.  
- **Professional Development Surge:** As tools like ChatGPT become ubiquitous, a big expansion in teacher training is underway. NZ initiatives (e.g. national AI training workshops, EdTechNZ webinars, UNESCO’s proposals) are scaling up. Experts stress that quality professional learning must cover both tech use and ethics. It’s expected that by 2026 most NZ teacher preparation programs will include AI topics. A concerted effort to build digital literacy – understanding AI’s strengths and pitfalls – is a top trend.  
- **Policy & Framework Evolution:** Observers predict major policy updates soon. The new Education Minister’s working group and AI Forum subgroup suggest forthcoming official strategy and guidelines on AI use in schools. Within a few years NZ may publish explicit AI competency standards or curriculum elements (mirroring moves in countries like Estonia or China). The focus will likely be on ethics, equity, and data use. Preliminary reports advocate developing safe, transparent policies rapidly (e.g. [54†L187-L194] calls for clear guidelines on privacy and ethics). The coming 2025/26 National Education Strategy reviews are expected to embed AI as a core consideration.  
- **Equity and Inclusion:** NZ analysts will be watching that AI does not widen the achievement gap. There is consensus that resources must be targeted to disadvantaged contexts (e.g. subsidized devices for low-decile schools, community training). NZ-specific AI content (like Māori language models) will be crucial. Thus, predictions stress efforts to ensure AI tools serve te reo Māori and Pasifika learners effectively ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=Closing%20the%20digital%20divide%3A%20We,use%20it%20and%20those%20without)) ([scarlatti.co.nz](https://scarlatti.co.nz/case-studies/shaping-the-future-ai-education-projects-in-oceania/#:~:text=kahu,%E2%80%9D%20Their%20products%20include)). If AI-backed tutoring is to *lift* attainment, schools in poorer areas will need infrastructure support and culturally relevant tools. Monitoring the “AI digital divide” – much like the internet equity campaigns – is expected.  
- **Assessment Transformation:** The landscape of testing may shift. Beyond the NZQA pilots, more AI-driven assessment tools are on the horizon. These might include automated essay scoring, AI-based oral exams (like the FFCoVE project), and interactive skill simulations. However, this will coexist with enhanced academic integrity systems: NZQA and schools will increasingly use AI detectors or evaluate assignments with AI-awareness rubrics. The overall trend is towards more *formative*, ongoing feedback (via AI) rather than solely summative exams.  
- **Global Alignment:** NZ will continue aligning with global trends and partners. Engagement with UNESCO’s and OECD’s AI-Ed initiatives is increasing. NZ EdTech companies and schools are networking internationally (e.g. Teacher’s Buddy piloting in Australia/US). More international conferences and research collaborations are expected. This global alignment means NZ educators will follow international best practices (AI ethics, data privacy) and potentially participate in multinational AI-product trials in education.  

## Conclusion  
AI adoption in NZ education is at a turning point. **Current state:** AI tools are readily available to students, yet formal uptake by schools is still early. The Ministry and agencies have published guidance, and pilot projects (both grassroots and institutional) are demonstrating possibilities – from AI tutors to ChatGPT-integrated workflows. However, a coordinated strategy is still forming. 

**Key insights:**  
- **Promise:** AI can personalize learning, support teachers, and potentially raise achievement levels. Early Kiwi examples (personalized literacy programs, AI tutors) show it can engage struggling learners and streamline onerous tasks ([m.scoop.co.nz](https://m.scoop.co.nz/stories/SC2506/S00007/ai-driven-structured-literacy-programme-delivers-impressive-results-in-nz-schools.htm#:~:text=While%20the%20past%20five%20years,confidence%2C%20and%20strong%20academic%20outcomes)) ([edtechnz.org.nz](https://edtechnz.org.nz/2025/06/24/edtech-in-the-spotlight-teachers-buddy-a-homegrown-ai-assistant-built-for-kiwi-classrooms/#:~:text=This%20curriculum,supports%20learners%20at%20all%20levels)). If well-implemented, AI aligns with NZ’s emphasis on differentiated, student-centered pedagogy, and could help NZ education reach global standards.  
- **Challenges:** Uptake is uneven. There is no “one-size-fits-all” solution – technology alone won’t fix educational issues. Experts warn that without clear leadership and training, AI could exacerbate inequities or lead to reliance on technology for its own sake ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=The%20New%20Zealand%20education%20system,safe%20and%20ethical%20use%20of)) ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=Closing%20the%20digital%20divide%3A%20We,use%20it%20and%20those%20without)). Ensuring teachers everywhere have access, that Māori voices shape AI content, and that students’ data is protected are major tasks ahead. Academic integrity and bias remain concerns, requiring ongoing vigilance.  
- **Next Steps:** Stakeholders should collaborate on a practical roadmap (the AI working group and AI Forum subgroup are good starts). This “living whitepaper” underscores the need for continuous dialogue: share results from case studies and pilots, iterate on policies, and involve educators in decision-making. Consistent with NZ leaders’ advice to frame AI optimistically yet cautiously, schools and policymakers must pair innovation with ethics training ([www.auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2024/12/11/integrating-ai-and-academia.html#:~:text=Generative%20AI%20is%20a%20disruptive,is%20the%20purpose%20of%20the)) ([futurelearning.nz](https://futurelearning.nz/p/charting-the-future-of-education#:~:text=,This%20includes)). Over the coming year, watch for guidelines formalizing AI competencies, further PD rollout, and expansion of local AI solutions (like Teacher’s Buddy).  

In summary, AI in New Zealand education offers both **opportunity and risk**. Early initiatives suggest cautious optimism – AI can enrich learning if aligned with NZ values and supports. The success of this ongoing journey will hinge on equity, ethics, and retaining the central role of teachers and communities. This report will be updated as new data and developments emerge, to guide NZ education leaders in this evolving landscape.
