# AI in Education in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper  
**Updated: March 9, 2026**

## Executive Snapshot

AI adoption in New Zealand education has moved from experimentation to managed implementation. In schools, the Ministry of Education now has a fuller generative AI guidance stack, including school policy guidance, NCEA-specific rules, case studies, and marking guidance. In tertiary education, multiple institutions have shifted from ad hoc advice to formal governance, secure tool access, and redesigned assessment models. At the national level, New Zealand’s AI strategy, released in July 2025, explicitly names education as a priority domain for AI adoption. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

The clearest signal of real-world adoption is no longer classroom experimentation alone; it is operational deployment. NZQA used automated text scoring on more than 55,000 writing assessments in May 2025, with human check-marking for quality assurance, and reported results were returned 3.5 weeks earlier than the prior year. That places assessment infrastructure, not just teaching support tools, at the centre of AI adoption in Aotearoa’s education system. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/publications/newsletters-circulars/assessment-matters/a2025-6/?utm_source=openai))

## Current State of AI Adoption

### The sector is using AI faster than it is standardising it

New Zealand teachers are already among the heavier AI users in the OECD. TALIS 2024 found 69% of New Zealand teachers had used AI in the previous 12 months, compared with an OECD average of 36%. Their most common uses were lesson planning and summarising topics, while use for assessing or marking student work remained relatively low. TALIS also found that non-users often cited capability gaps: 71% said they lacked the knowledge and skills to teach using AI. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

The NZCER’s 2025 primary-school study points in the same direction: AI is already present in teaching and learning, but policy, resourcing, and professional learning are lagging. That report drew on 266 Year 5–8 teachers and 147 students across four case-study schools, and concluded that centrally supported professional learning, better privacy-protected access to higher-quality tools, and culturally grounded policy are now system needs rather than optional extras. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools))

### The conversation has shifted from “cheating” to “design”

The sector’s posture is increasingly pragmatic. Ministry guidance now frames AI as something schools should govern, teach about, and design around rather than simply ban. The guidance tells schools to verify outputs, avoid entering personal data, account for cultural bias, and build explicit AI policy settings around risk, privacy, professional development, and review. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

In practice, New Zealand is moving toward a “human-led, AI-assisted” model: teachers remain responsible for judgments, while AI is increasingly acceptable for planning, feedback, drafting support, and administration. The strongest resistance remains around high-stakes marking and authenticity. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-10/1373%20GenAI%20Marking%20Tools%20flyer_V2.pdf?VersionId=Oz_o7IGHBcO7x7DAX6SXlIT2JF60QBmv))

## Policy, Governance, and Regulatory Setting

### School and NCEA settings

The Ministry of Education’s updated generative AI guidance now states that schools with consent to assess must include acceptable GenAI use in their authenticity policies. For NCEA, the Ministry is explicit that evidence submitted for assessment must be the student’s own work, and generative AI is not permitted in NCEA external assessment. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

In October 2025, the Ministry added more specific guidance on AI-assisted marking. That guidance is principles-based and keeps teacher responsibility central: teachers remain fully responsible for marking decisions, human oversight is essential, and AI is better suited to formative feedback and straightforward tasks than to complex summative judgment. It also states that use of AI to support marking of NCEA internal assessments is discouraged and should be limited to supporting the assessor’s own final judgments. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-10/1373%20GenAI%20Marking%20Tools%20flyer_V2.pdf?VersionId=Oz_o7IGHBcO7x7DAX6SXlIT2JF60QBmv))

Privacy and safety have also become more formalised. Ministry guidance points schools to the Privacy Commissioner’s AI expectations, while Digital Government’s February 2025 Responsible AI Guidance for the Public Service established a broader government framework of safe, transparent, and responsible adoption. That wider public-sector framing is increasingly visible in education agencies’ AI work. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### Tertiary governance is becoming formal

Tertiary institutions have accelerated from guidance to institutional architecture. The University of Auckland now provides students and staff with approved secure AI tools, including Copilot Chat, Gemini, and NotebookLM, explicitly positioning privacy-protected access as part of responsible adoption. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/students/my-tools/generative-ai.html?utm_source=openai))

AUT is implementing assessment principles across 2025–2026 that explicitly recognise the increasing role of generative AI, engage staff and students with GenAI in assessment, and reduce reliance on unseen invigilated exams in favour of other controlled assessments. ([aut.ac.nz](https://www.aut.ac.nz/about/teaching-learning-and-assessment/assessment-design))

Massey University has a formal Generative Artificial Intelligence in Assessment Policy requiring that AI use be explicitly permitted in assessment criteria and disclosed by students. The University of Otago has gone further at the governance level: its AI Governance Policy was approved in December 2025 and is scheduled to take effect on **March 10, 2026**, with requirements for risk assessment, Māori data sovereignty protection, and human oversight before new AI tools are deployed. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/2194/18_June_2025_Academic_Board_Agenda_Part_I.pdf?utm_source=openai))

## Recent Developments and News

### 1) AI is now part of curriculum reform conversations

On **September 11, 2025**, the government announced new senior secondary subjects and said it was investigating a new Year 13 subject in generative AI for later development. That matters less as a standalone subject announcement than as a signal: AI has moved from an edge issue to a curriculum design issue. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/top/572737/new-push-for-ai-as-education-minister-erica-stanford-announces-curriculum-changes?utm_source=openai))

Earlier, on **August 4–5, 2025**, the government’s proposed replacement of NCEA also brought AI into the mainstream policy conversation, with the Education Minister explicitly describing AI as part of future assessment administration and marking efficiency. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/568894/too-much-credit-counting-government-proposes-replacing-ncea-school-system?utm_source=openai))

### 2) NZQA has become one of the sector’s most important AI adopters

NZQA’s 2025 public statements show a maturing internal AI programme. Beyond automated text scoring, NZQA says it is using or testing AI-powered chat support, document recognition for international qualifications work, and tools intended to protect assessment integrity. Its framing is notable: AI as a “helpful team member,” but with ethics, sovereignty, explainability, and accountability built into deployment. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/nzqas-responsible-use-of-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

### 3) National AI policy now provides a stronger backdrop

New Zealand’s national AI strategy, released in **July 2025**, positioned education alongside agriculture and healthcare as one of the domains where AI should be applied, while emphasising adoption, confidence, and alignment with OECD AI principles. Separately, MBIE launched a major AI research-commercialisation investment through the New Zealand Institute for Advanced Technology, with decisions continuing into 2026. This broader policy environment is helping normalise AI capability-building across education. ([beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-07/New%20Zealand%27s%20AI%20Strategy%20-%20Investing%20with%20confidence.pdf))

## Research Overview

### Schools: strong use, uneven readiness

The strongest current evidence base for schools comes from TALIS 2024 and NZCER’s primary-school work. Together they show:

- **Adoption is already mainstream among many teachers**, especially for planning, summarising, and resource creation. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))
- **Assessment remains the caution zone**, with much lower use for grading or judgment-heavy work. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))
- **Professional learning demand is high**; Education Counts’ TALIS analysis notes that 85% of the AI-engaged primary cohort in the NZCER study wanted more training. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))
- **Policy and access are uneven**, especially around privacy-protected tools, school policy clarity, and culturally relevant AI performance. ([nzcer.org.nz](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/generative-artificial-intelligence-aotearoa-new-zealand-primary-schools))

### Tertiary: research is catching up with practice

Ako Aotearoa’s completed scoping project on AI in adult tertiary education, published on **December 16, 2025**, provides one of the best recent Aotearoa-specific syntheses for the tertiary sector. It frames the central question not as whether AI should be used, but how it can be integrated effectively and equitably for Māori, Pacific, neurodiverse, and other learners. The project produced three volumes covering educator insight, practical applications, and leadership/policy design. ([ako.ac.nz](https://ako.ac.nz/knowledge-centre/integration-of-ai-in-adult-tertiary-education))

A second Ako Aotearoa report, *Empowering Aotearoa*, sharpens the emerging consensus: responsible educational AI in New Zealand should be built around transparency, human oversight, accessibility, disclosure, and Māori data sovereignty rather than detection-based policing. It also notes that universities in Aotearoa have begun publishing staff guidance on inclusive digital pedagogy and permitted AI use, signalling a shift toward embedded institutional norms. ([ako.ac.nz](https://ako.ac.nz/assets/Knowledge-centre/45195-An-Inclusive-Approach-to-AI-Literacy/REPORT-Empowering-Aotearoa.pdf))

## Case Studies

### Aotea College: school-wide governance first

The Ministry’s March 2025 case study of Aotea College shows a governance-led model. The school ran a whole-school survey, formed an AI working group, updated its assessment policy, created staff resources, and added checkpoints and supervision mechanisms to reduce misuse. This is a strong example of AI adoption happening through school systems design rather than individual teacher experimentation alone. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Aotea%20College_MAR2025.pdf))

### Hobsonville Point Secondary School: restorative authenticity processes

The Ministry’s Hobsonville Point case study is important because it treats AI misuse as both an integrity and a support issue. The school combined checkpoints, document-history checks, verbal questioning, restorative conversations, and family involvement, reporting a significant drop in inappropriate AI use and improved staff confidence. ([web-assets.education.govt.nz](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/GenAI%20Case%20Study%20Hobsonville%20Point%20Secondary%20School_MAR2025.pdf?VersionId=.9dochWl4SsdLrmFkwawNSFNaw_SoYAW))

### New Windsor School: primary-school classroom integration

Education Gazette’s March 31, 2025 profile of New Windsor School shows AI being used in everyday teaching practice rather than as a specialist pilot. Students were using Brisk for real-time writing feedback, and the school was extending the programme through cluster-wide PLD and community engagement. The case suggests primary adoption is most durable when paired with local professional learning and clear pedagogical purpose. ([gazette.education.govt.nz](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/articles/ai-approach-at-new-windsor-school-prepares-akonga-for-the-future/))

### Marlborough Boys’ College: student-built AI for learning support

In late 2025, three students at Marlborough Boys’ College built **WordFrame**, an AI-assisted writing support tool designed to help students structure and improve writing rather than generate it for them. The significance here is cultural as much as technical: students are not just using AI; they are building education-specific AI aligned with local assessment realities and an explicit “support learning, not shortcuts” ethic. ([gazette.education.govt.nz](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/articles/akonga-develop-an-ai-tool-that-supports-learning-not-shortcuts/))

### University of Auckland: from chatbot support to assessment redesign

The University of Auckland has emerged as one of the clearest tertiary exemplars. Its UoA Assistant, launched in 2024, provides AI-supported navigation and support across university systems. The university has also rolled out secure GenAI tools for staff and students, endorsed an AI in Education Action Plan in June 2025, and adopted a two-lane assessment model that separates controlled assessments used to assure learning from more open assessments where responsible AI use is expected and designed for. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/students/student-support/uoa-assistant.html))

The same institution is also backing AI-enabled learning innovation more directly: in May 2025 it received a Meta grant to develop tools that help students learn foundational programming skills, including multilingual support, and in August 2025 it announced a $1 million-backed project to build an AI pronunciation coach for te reo Māori learners. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/05/02/university-wins-meta-grant-for-ai-project-.html?utm_source=openai))

### Vocational education: Ara and Otago Polytechnic

Ara Institute of Canterbury and Otago Polytechnic have become important contributors to the practice literature. Their 2025 book and related publication programme reposition AI from a plagiarism problem to a design opportunity, with case studies across construction, nursing, design, and business, including work supporting neurodivergent learners. This is one of the strongest signs that New Zealand’s vocational sector is trying to create distinctly local, practice-based AI models rather than importing generic ones. ([ara.ac.nz](https://www.ara.ac.nz/news-and-events/news-stories/ai-in-education/))

## Major Trends

### 1) Assessment is the pressure point

Most of the sector’s serious AI work is converging on assessment: authenticity, disclosure, redesign, feedback, and marking. New Zealand is not eliminating AI from assessment; it is differentiating where AI belongs, where it does not, and where human judgment must remain dominant. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### 2) Policy maturity is increasing, but unevenly

The system now has much more formal guidance than it did in 2023 or early 2024. But capability still varies by institution and school. Early adopters have secure tools, policies, and professional learning; others are still relying on individual staff initiative. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### 3) Equity and Māori data sovereignty are becoming core design criteria

This is one of the clearest New Zealand-specific themes. Ministry guidance warns that AI can be weak on mātauranga Māori and te reo Māori. Tertiary initiatives such as AIHOA and the University of Otago’s governance policy explicitly foreground Māori data sovereignty, tikanga, and culturally safe AI use. AI in education in Aotearoa is increasingly being framed not just as a productivity issue, but as a Treaty, culture, and equity issue. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

### 4) Professional learning is now strategic infrastructure

Teacher capability is emerging as the decisive bottleneck. The evidence no longer suggests that the main issue is whether educators are interested; many already are. The issue is whether the system can provide safe access, shared models, and training at sufficient scale. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))

## Outlook

As of **March 9, 2026**, AI in New Zealand education is best described as **selectively embedded**. It is embedded in some operational functions, especially NZQA assessment workflows and university support systems; it is increasingly embedded in tertiary governance and assessment design; and it is widely used by teachers for preparation and feedback. But it is not yet embedded evenly across the system, and it remains tightly constrained in high-stakes school assessment. ([www2.nzqa.govt.nz](https://www2.nzqa.govt.nz/about-us/news/embracing-ai-in-student-assessments/?utm_source=openai))

The next phase is likely to be less about initial experimentation and more about system discipline: clearer institutional rules, better protected tool access, more explicit assessment redesign, and stronger local capability in culturally grounded AI. If that happens, New Zealand’s education sector will move from fragmented adoption to a recognisably Aotearoa model of AI-enabled education. ([ako.ac.nz](https://ako.ac.nz/knowledge-centre/integration-of-ai-in-adult-tertiary-education))

## Conclusion

The biggest update since the prior edition is this: AI in New Zealand education is no longer mainly a debate about whether it should be allowed. It is now a practical question of **where it adds value, where it creates risk, and what human oversight, cultural safeguards, and assessment redesign are required to use it well**. Official guidance is stronger, research is more locally grounded, tertiary institutions are formalising policy, and NZQA has demonstrated that AI can already play a significant operational role in assessment when tightly governed. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/school/digital-technology/generative-ai))

Overall, the sector is advancing—but unevenly. The leading edge is increasingly sophisticated; the system as a whole is still building capability. That makes 2026 a pivotal year: not for hype, but for governance, professional learning, culturally aligned design, and evidence-led scaling. ([educationcounts.govt.nz](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0006/250791/TALIS-2024-Findings-for-New-Zealand-1.pdf))
