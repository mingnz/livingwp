# AI in Data Analytics in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper

**Introduction:** New Zealand’s data analytics sector is rapidly embracing AI technologies to unlock insights and efficiency.  From startups to large enterprises, Kiwi companies are using machine learning, natural language processing, and generative AI to enhance data analysis and decision-making.  This living report synthesizes the latest developments – news, studies, and case examples – illustrating how AI is reshaping data analytics in Aotearoa. It highlights key adoption rates, common use–cases, and challenges (such as data quality and governance) based on recent surveys and reports.

## Current News

- **AI Investment and Spending:** A June 2024 IDC report found that ANZ software spending related to AI (platforms) grew a remarkable **62% year-over-year**, driven especially by generative AI capabilities ([www.idc.com](https://www.idc.com/getdoc.jsp?containerId=prAP52348124#:~:text=In%20contrast%2C%20the%20artificial%20intelligence,value%2C%20costs%2C%20and)).  IDC analysts note that organisations remain cautiously focused on productivity and efficiency amid economic uncertainty ([www.idc.com](https://www.idc.com/getdoc.jsp?containerId=prAP52348124#:~:text=region%20faced%20very%20noticeable%20financial,Data%20%26%20Analytics%2C%20IDC%20A%2FNZ)).  Both Australian and NZ governments are backing AI research and ethics; the NZ Government and *AI Forum NZ* have developed a joint AI governance strategy ([www.idc.com](https://www.idc.com/getdoc.jsp?containerId=prAP52348124#:~:text=of%20artificial%20intelligence%2C%20as%20it,mitigation%20of%20any%20associated%20risks)).  

- **Business Adoption Rates:**  Recent surveys show AI use is now mainstream in NZ businesses.  Datacom’s 2024 State of AI Index (surveying 200 senior managers) reports **66–87% of companies using AI**, up sharply from prior years ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=Datacom%E2%80%99s%20latest%20%E2%80%9CState%20of%20AI,within%20the%20last%20two%20years)) ([datacom.com](https://datacom.com/nz/en/solutions/artificial-intelligence/ai-insights/state-of-ai-index-2025#:~:text=Datacom%E2%80%99s%202025%20State%20of%20AI,in%202023)).  For example, a NZBusiness report (Aug 2024) cites that “two-thirds of New Zealand companies are now utilising some form of AI, up from 48% in 2023” ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=Datacom%E2%80%99s%20latest%20%E2%80%9CState%20of%20AI,within%20the%20last%20two%20years)).  Among those using AI, **80–88% report positive impacts** on productivity and decision-making ([datacom.com](https://datacom.com/nz/en/solutions/artificial-intelligence/ai-insights/state-of-ai-index-2025#:~:text=Datacom%E2%80%99s%202025%20State%20of%20AI,in%202023)) ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=The%20study%20highlights%20a%20notable,advantages%20and%20new%20revenue%20opportunities)).  Businesses describe AI as “exciting,” and roughly 71% expect economic benefits or new revenue from it ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=The%20study%20highlights%20a%20notable,advantages%20and%20new%20revenue%20opportunities)).

- **Data and Trust Issues:**  Many industry leaders highlight data bottlenecks.  A September 2024 survey of NZ finance executives (ActiveOps/Censuswide) found **94% struggle to extract insights from their data** due to quality issues ([cfotech.co.nz](https://cfotech.co.nz/story/nz-finance-leaders-struggle-with-outdated-data-ai-challenges#:~:text=According%20to%20an%20international%20study,financial%20sector%20in%20New%20Zealand)).  The same study reported 84% of finance leaders **do not fully trust their data**, and 40% are working with data over two weeks old ([cfotech.co.nz](https://cfotech.co.nz/story/nz-finance-leaders-struggle-with-outdated-data-ai-challenges#:~:text=According%20to%20an%20international%20study,financial%20sector%20in%20New%20Zealand)).  Datacom’s survey similarly found only 9% of companies consider their data fully “clean,” while 30% admit a large portion of data is outdated or inconsistent ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=Effective%20AI%20implementation%20is%20heavily,%E2%80%9D)).  Poor data quality is widely seen as a barrier: subjects note that AI’s “garbage in, garbage out” problem must be addressed before scaling analytics ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=Effective%20AI%20implementation%20is%20heavily,%E2%80%9D)) ([itbrief.co.nz](https://itbrief.co.nz/story/ai-adoption-surges-among-new-zealand-businesses-report#:~:text=Additionally%2C%20the%20report%20points%20to,clean)).

- **Workforce and Training:**  Despite rising AI use, formal training is lagging.  A July 2025 workplace survey (Cultivate Recruitment/NewZealand.AI) of 829 NZ white-collar workers found **76% feel positive about AI’s role** in their jobs, and 44% already use AI daily (e.g. ChatGPT, copilot tools) ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/BU202507/S00163.htm#:~:text=%3E%2076,they%20were%2012%20months%20ago)) ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/BU202507/S00163.htm#:~:text=Advertisement%20,reading)).  However, only 13% have received any employer-led AI training, and over half had **none at all** ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/BU202507/S00163.htm#:~:text=%3E%2076,they%20were%2012%20months%20ago)).  This “bottom-up” adoption suggests companies may need to invest more in staff upskilling to fully leverage data analytics tools.

- **Industry Highlights:**  Other news include reports of AI tackling specific data challenges.  For example, NZ startup Aider offers a real-time analytics assistant that **consolidates small businesses’ sales, weather, and social media data** into actionable reports; one case noted it saved a cafe-owner over 10 hours per week on insights ([newzealand.ai](https://newzealand.ai/artificial-intelligence-case-studies#:~:text=1.%20Aider%20%E2%80%93%20Real,Analytics)).  Similarly, Lynker Analytics (a Wellington firm) used AI and Lidar data to map solar-panel potential for homes.co.nz – a first for NZ – generating rooftop energy estimates and heatmaps ([newzealand.ai](https://newzealand.ai/case-studies/lynker-analytics-rooftop-solar#:~:text=In%20a%20New%20Zealand%20first%2C,properties%20in%20Auckland%20and%20Wellington)) ([newzealand.ai](https://newzealand.ai/case-studies/lynker-analytics-rooftop-solar#:~:text=Finally%20based%20on%20the%20distribution,the%20average%20retail%20power%20price)).  These exemplify how AI-driven analytics are being applied in local contexts.

- **Datacom State of AI Reports:**  Datacom’s **State of AI Index** (2023–25) shows strong momentum.  By 2024/25, about **87–88% of NZ organisations are using AI**, with 88% reporting positive outcomes ([datacom.com](https://datacom.com/nz/en/solutions/artificial-intelligence/ai-insights/state-of-ai-index-2025#:~:text=Datacom%E2%80%99s%202025%20State%20of%20AI,in%202023)).  Notably, growth in AI adoption jump from 48% (2023) to 66% (2024) to 87% ([itbrief.co.nz](https://itbrief.co.nz/story/ai-adoption-surges-among-new-zealand-businesses-report#:~:text=The%20research%20indicates%20that%20two,in%20the%20previous%20year)) ([datacom.com](https://datacom.com/nz/en/solutions/artificial-intelligence/ai-insights/state-of-ai-index-2025#:~:text=Datacom%E2%80%99s%202025%20State%20of%20AI,in%202023)).  The biggest reported benefit is **productivity gain**; many firms cite faster insights and decision automation.  However, only ~12% have fully integrated AI across the organisation – most are still in pilot or partial rollout stage ([datacom.com](https://datacom.com/nz/en/solutions/artificial-intelligence/ai-insights/state-of-ai-index-2025#:~:text=Datacom%E2%80%99s%202025%20State%20of%20AI,in%202023)).  Key barriers identified are **skills shortages, data silos, and lack of governance** ([datacom.com](https://datacom.com/nz/en/solutions/artificial-intelligence/ai-insights/state-of-ai-index-2025#:~:text=Despite%20the%20momentum%2C%20scaling%20AI,AI%20continue%20to%20slow%20progress)) ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=Despite%20the%20increased%20adoption%2C%20there,audit%20assurance%20and%20governance%20frameworks)).

- **IDC and Industry Analyses:**  IDC research (Jun 2024) projects the ANZ AI platforms market to keep growing (CAGR ~44% to 2028) ([www.idc.com](https://www.idc.com/getdoc.jsp?containerId=prAP52348124#:~:text=Worldwide%20Semiannual%20Software%20Tracker%20,ends%20Antonova)).  IDC notes NZ organisations are focused on productivity (improved employee output is a primary AI driver) ([www.idc.com](https://www.idc.com/getdoc.jsp?containerId=prAP52348124#:~:text=course%20to%20receive%20the%20highest,in%20the%20era%20of%20AI)).  IDC also confirms **generative AI** (LLMs, co-pilots) as a major growth area ([www.idc.com](https://www.idc.com/getdoc.jsp?containerId=prAP52348124#:~:text=In%20contrast%2C%20the%20artificial%20intelligence,value%2C%20costs%2C%20and)).  Similar findings come from global firms: a Microsoft-backed report (Aug 2024) by Accenture predicts **Generative AI could add NZ$76–102 billion to NZ GDP by 2038** and save workers an average 275 hours per year ([news.microsoft.com](https://news.microsoft.com/en-nz/2024/08/21/generative-ai-expected-to-more-than-double-new-zealands-productivity-report/#:~:text=,New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20competitiveness%20at%20risk)). Organizations are also keen on enhancing customer/employee experiences with AI ([itbrief.co.nz](https://itbrief.co.nz/story/ai-adoption-surges-among-new-zealand-businesses-report#:~:text=The%20research%20identifies%20the%20most,to%20innovation%20and%20strategy%20development)). ## Case Studies

- **Aider (Real-Time Business Analytics):** A NZ startup, Aider provides an AI “digital assistant” for small businesses.  It integrates data from point-of-sale (POS) systems, weather forecasts, and even social media to deliver actionable analytics via a mobile app.  For example, the owner of a local café reported saving over 10 hours a week in manual analysis, thanks to Aider’s real-time operational insights ([newzealand.ai](https://newzealand.ai/artificial-intelligence-case-studies#:~:text=1.%20Aider%20%E2%80%93%20Real,Analytics)).  This shows AI is enabling **SMEs to harness data** more efficiently without large analytics teams.

- **Lynker Analytics – Solar Energy Mapping:**  Lynker Analytics (Wellington) partnered with homes.co.nz and Harrisons Energy to apply AI and LiDAR geospatial data for residential solar planning ([newzealand.ai](https://newzealand.ai/case-studies/lynker-analytics-rooftop-solar#:~:text=In%20a%20New%20Zealand%20first%2C,properties%20in%20Auckland%20and%20Wellington)).  They built detailed 3D models of Auckland/Wellington rooftops, accounting for orientation and shadows, then generated “solar heatmaps” for each property ([newzealand.ai](https://newzealand.ai/case-studies/lynker-analytics-rooftop-solar#:~:text=Using%20Lidar%2C%20we%20generated%20detailed,detail%20contained%20within%20the%20models)).  Using machine learning calibrated against real production data, the tool estimates annual solar output (kWh) and financial return for rooftop panels ([newzealand.ai](https://newzealand.ai/case-studies/lynker-analytics-rooftop-solar#:~:text=Finally%20based%20on%20the%20distribution,the%20average%20retail%20power%20price)).  This first-of-its-kind NZ project illustrates how **advanced analytical models** can turn large environmental datasets into consumer-facing insights about renewable energy.

- **Castlepoint Systems – Compliance Analytics:**  Castlepoint, a NZ software firm, deployed AI to analyze policy documents and reports for misconduct or abuse keywords ([newzealand.ai](https://newzealand.ai/artificial-intelligence-case-studies#:~:text=1,Detection)).  The system scans entire document repositories, flagging any misconduct-related terms.  In practice this achieved **100% accuracy** in detecting relevant keywords ([newzealand.ai](https://newzealand.ai/artificial-intelligence-case-studies#:~:text=1,Detection)).  For organizations with vast archives (e.g. in education or corporate HR), such AI-driven text analytics greatly speeds compliance reviews and risk management.

*(Other AI case studies in NZ span sectors like healthcare, finance, and retail.  These examples show that firms are embedding AI-driven data analysis into core business functions – from chatbots to risk assessment – often reporting double-digit efficiency gains.)*

## Trends and Predictions

- **Generative AI and Productivity:**  The rise of LLMs (e.g. ChatGPT, CoPilot, Bard) is becoming a major trend in NZ analytics.  Surveys indicate NZ workers are early adopters: by mid-2025, 82% of office workers had used general AI assistants daily at work ([www.scoop.co.nz](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/BU202507/S00163.htm#:~:text=Advertisement%20,reading)).  Generative AI is expected to handle routine queries and summarizations, freeing analysts for higher-level tasks.  If NZ firms invest in skills and trustworthy AI use, studies forecast huge productivity gains (e.g. saving 275 hours per worker annually) ([news.microsoft.com](https://news.microsoft.com/en-nz/2024/08/21/generative-ai-expected-to-more-than-double-new-zealands-productivity-report/#:~:text=,New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20competitiveness%20at%20risk)).  However, this will require addressing policy and trust issues, as noted in recent reports ([news.microsoft.com](https://news.microsoft.com/en-nz/2024/08/21/generative-ai-expected-to-more-than-double-new-zealands-productivity-report/#:~:text=,New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20competitiveness%20at%20risk)).

- **Data Governance and Ethics:**  A clear trend is that NZ organizations are paying more attention to data governance as analytics scale up.  Many experts emphasize that *AI’s promise hinges on data quality*.  Business leaders in recent panels stressed the need for better data storage, classification, and privacy practices as AI use grows ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=Effective%20AI%20implementation%20is%20heavily,%E2%80%9D)).  The government is also acting: in July 2025 NZ released a national AI Strategy (“Investing with Confidence”) that highlights AI as essential to productivity and calls for responsible use ([www.mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/digital-policy/new-zealands-ai-strategy-investing-with-confidence#:~:text=Artificial%20intelligence%20,our%20economy%20needs%20to%20thrive)).  This suggests increasing support (and potentially future regulation) around ethical data and AI frameworks.

- **Industry-Specific Analytics:**  AI-driven analytics are penetrating traditional NZ sectors.  For example, agritech companies use AI to optimize farm yields, retailers use AI for personalized marketing, and insurers use AI to tailor policies.  Recent projects show generative AI being applied to customer data, and machine learning to financial forecasting.  The trend is toward **augmented analytics** – using AI as a co-pilot for human analysts.  Many NZ businesses are exploring ways to embed analytics into SaaS tools (in CRM, accounting, etc.).  This aligns with global forecasts that specialized AI solutions will proliferate in vertical markets.

- **Skill Shortages and Adoption Barriers:**  Forecasts agree that a shortage of trained AI/data professionals will constrain uptake.  A significant fraction of NZ firms (around 40–50%) cite lack of expertise as a top challenge to adopting AI ([itbrief.co.nz](https://itbrief.co.nz/story/ai-adoption-surges-among-new-zealand-businesses-report#:~:text=Additionally%2C%20the%20report%20points%20to,clean)) ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=Despite%20the%20increased%20adoption%2C%20there,audit%20assurance%20and%20governance%20frameworks)).  In practice, companies are hiring data engineers and data-scientists, and industry groups are launching AI training programs.  But until skills and infrastructure catch up, some NZ firms may struggle to build advanced analytics in-house.  This suggests continued opportunities for consulting firms, startup platforms, and collaborative data projects to fill gaps.

- **Predictions:**  Industry analysts predict that AI adoption in NZ’s data analytics will continue strong growth.  For 2025–2028, models suggest double-digit annual growth in AI software and platforms.  In the near term, executive surveys imply NZ will leverage AI for **predictive analytics** (e.g. demand forecasting, risk modeling) and **natural language insights** (automated reporting).  Longer-term, AI is expected to become a standard part of analytics toolkits; e.g., embedded in cloud analytics services (like Microsoft Fabric or Google Cloud Vertex).  Stakeholders anticipate NZ becoming a regional leader in niche AI analytics – particularly in areas such as agritech, environmental data, and specialized finance – if barriers are resolved.

## Conclusion

Overall, New Zealand’s data analytics industry is at an inflection point with AI adoption accelerating rapidly.  Recent surveys and news reports document growing AI usage (now around two-thirds of businesses) and clear productivity improvements ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=Datacom%E2%80%99s%20latest%20%E2%80%9CState%20of%20AI,within%20the%20last%20two%20years)) ([datacom.com](https://datacom.com/nz/en/solutions/artificial-intelligence/ai-insights/state-of-ai-index-2025#:~:text=Datacom%E2%80%99s%202025%20State%20of%20AI,in%202023)).  Case studies from KIwi companies show practical gains: AI analytics are speeding time-intensive tasks, enabling real-time insight, and even opening new service offerings.  At the same time, common challenges – insufficient data quality, lack of formal governance, and skills gaps – are evident across the sector ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=Despite%20the%20increased%20adoption%2C%20there,audit%20assurance%20and%20governance%20frameworks)) ([cfotech.co.nz](https://cfotech.co.nz/story/nz-finance-leaders-struggle-with-outdated-data-ai-challenges#:~:text=According%20to%20an%20international%20study,financial%20sector%20in%20New%20Zealand)).  

Looking ahead, NZ’s data analytics professionals are increasingly working in a **“data + AI”** paradigm.  Analysts are adopting generative AI tools to summarize data, and ML models to detect patterns at scale.  Experts advise that NZ firms invest now in training and data infrastructure, as success will depend on having clean, accessible data sets and a “human-in-the-loop” governance model ([www.idc.com](https://www.idc.com/getdoc.jsp?containerId=prAP52348124#:~:text=Worldwide%20Semiannual%20Software%20Tracker%20,ends%20Antonova)) ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=Effective%20AI%20implementation%20is%20heavily,%E2%80%9D)).  If these investments occur, AI is projected to deliver substantial economic and productivity gains for New Zealand by 2030 and beyond ([news.microsoft.com](https://news.microsoft.com/en-nz/2024/08/21/generative-ai-expected-to-more-than-double-new-zealands-productivity-report/#:~:text=,New%20Zealand%E2%80%99s%20competitiveness%20at%20risk)) ([www.mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/digital-policy/new-zealands-ai-strategy-investing-with-confidence#:~:text=Artificial%20intelligence%20,our%20economy%20needs%20to%20thrive)). 

**Key Takeaway:**  AI-driven analytics are becoming integral to New Zealand business. This living report will be updated as new studies, news, and examples emerge, but current evidence suggests the trend is clear: organisations that integrate AI into their analytics processes tend to outperform peers, while neglecting data readiness risks losing out in a data-centric economy ([datacom.com](https://datacom.com/nz/en/solutions/artificial-intelligence/ai-insights/state-of-ai-index-2025#:~:text=Datacom%E2%80%99s%202025%20State%20of%20AI,in%202023)) ([nzbusiness.co.nz](https://nzbusiness.co.nz/news/new-zealand-companies-embrace-ai-amid-governance-gaps-and-data-challenges#:~:text=Effective%20AI%20implementation%20is%20heavily,%E2%80%9D)).
