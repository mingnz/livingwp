# AI in Academic Research in Aotearoa New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper

**Last updated: 31 March 2026**

## Introduction

AI adoption in New Zealand’s academic research sector is continuing to institutionalise. Since the previous update on **9 March 2026**, the biggest changes are not a sudden wave of new projects, but firmer **selection, governance, and infrastructure decisions**: the national **AI Research Platform** process has moved into its final proposal stage, the **University of Otago’s AI Governance Policy** took effect on **10 March 2026**, and funder guidance for **Marsden 2026** now sets clearer rules on both funding availability and the use of generative AI in applications and assessment. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/refocusing-the-science-innovation-and-technology-system/public-research-organisations/new-zealand-institute-for-advanced-technology/call-for-proposals-for-the-artificial-intelligence-research-platform))

## Executive Summary

- **The national platform contest is now the main strategic hinge-point.** MBIE’s AI Research Platform has progressed from concept selection to final proposal development. Five concepts received **NZ$250,000** each in phase 1, phase 2 proposals are due **31 March 2026**, assessment is scheduled for **mid-April 2026**, and funding decisions are expected in **late April/May 2026**. The platform remains backed by **up to NZ$70 million over seven years**. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/refocusing-the-science-innovation-and-technology-system/public-research-organisations/new-zealand-institute-for-advanced-technology/call-for-proposals-for-the-artificial-intelligence-research-platform))
- **Health research remains the clearest leader in actual adoption.** The HRC’s 2025 **AI in Healthcare** initiative created a **NZ$5 million** funding pool, and the HRC’s 2025 annual report says **10 studies** were funded for a total of **NZ$4.6 million**. The funded portfolio spans screening, stroke care, radiology, dermatology, dementia, hospital monitoring and clinical workflow redesign. ([gateway.hrc.govt.nz](https://gateway.hrc.govt.nz/funding/priorities-funding/2025-artificial-intelligence-in-healthcare))
- **Governance is getting more operational.** Otago now has a university-wide AI governance policy in force; AUT’s January 2026 research guidance explicitly addresses Māori and Pacific data sovereignty; UC has issued researcher-facing guidance warning that many AI transcription tools are not compliant with New Zealand privacy requirements for sensitive research data; and the University of Auckland provides approved access to Copilot Chat, Gemini and NotebookLM as secure institutional tools. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/humanresources/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy))
- **Research funders are tightening both expectations and process.** For **Marsden 2026**, approximately **NZ$55.8 million** is available for new grants, fewer grants are expected, no Marsden Fund Council Awards will be offered, applicants must take full responsibility for any AI-assisted proposal content, and panellists/external reviewers must not use LLMs to assess proposals because of confidentiality concerns. ([royalsociety.org.nz](https://www.royalsociety.org.nz/what-we-do/funds-and-opportunities/marsden/marsden-announcements/information-for-the-marsden-fund-2026-selection-round/))
- **Infrastructure planning is shifting from generic eResearch to explicit AI capacity.** REANNZ’s integration with NeSI remains the backbone change, but the stronger new signal is REANNZ’s **AI Infrastructure Platform** procurement process for national GPU capability and its 2025/26 strategy update, which frames AI, trusted research environments, data sovereignty and equitable access as central sector needs. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/new-era-for-digital-research-infrastructure?utm_source=openai))

## What Changed Since the 9 March 2026 Version

- **10 March 2026:** Otago’s AI Governance Policy took effect, moving one of the country’s major universities from guidance into a formal governance framework covering procurement, deployment, research, teaching and third-party use. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/humanresources/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy?utm_source=openai))
- **Late March 2026:** The AI Research Platform is now at the end of phase 2 proposal development rather than seed-stage concept selection, making the next public funding announcement the most important near-term event for the sector. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/refocusing-the-science-innovation-and-technology-system/public-research-organisations/new-zealand-institute-for-advanced-technology/call-for-proposals-for-the-artificial-intelligence-research-platform))
- **Marsden 2026 settings are clearer:** funding is tighter, governance of decisions is shifting to the new Research Funding New Zealand board, and AI use is allowed cautiously for applicants but not for proposal assessment. ([royalsociety.org.nz](https://www.royalsociety.org.nz/what-we-do/funds-and-opportunities/marsden/marsden-announcements/information-for-the-marsden-fund-2026-selection-round/))

## Current State of AI Adoption

### High-level snapshot

As of **31 March 2026**, AI adoption in New Zealand academic research is best described as **mission-led, governance-heavy, and still unevenly measured**. The strongest evidence is not from one national adoption survey, but from the convergence of **government strategy, platform funding, ethics requirements, university governance, and applied research portfolios**. Health is still the most mature domain, but serious capability is also visible in climate science, digital twins, aquaculture, geospatial modelling, and real-world robotics/edge AI. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/artificial-intelligence-strategy-and-business-guidance-now-available?utm_source=openai))

A useful shift since mid-2025 is that AI is increasingly being treated as **research infrastructure and workflow redesign**, not just a writing or coding aid. The platform proposals alone cover agentic systems, creative AI, autonomous systems, bioeconomy AI, and physical/real-world AI; university operating rules are increasingly about **tool approval, data handling, ethics, and sovereignty**, not simply awareness. That is an inference, but it is strongly supported by the current mix of funding, governance, and infrastructure actions. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/refocusing-the-science-innovation-and-technology-system/public-research-organisations/new-zealand-institute-for-advanced-technology/call-for-proposals-for-the-artificial-intelligence-research-platform))

## Latest News and Strategic Developments

### 1) The national AI research platform is now in its decisive stage

MBIE’s AI Research Platform process has become more concrete since the last update. The Government’s objective is to create a platform that advances internationally leading AI research, strengthens commercialisation, and underpins a world-class AI institute. Publicly named phase-1 concepts span **agentic AI**, **creative AI**, **autonomous intelligence**, **bioeconomy AI**, and **physical/real-world AI**. MBIE says only one proposal is intended to be funded, although it reserves the right to support more than one or none. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/refocusing-the-science-innovation-and-technology-system/public-research-organisations/new-zealand-institute-for-advanced-technology/call-for-proposals-for-the-artificial-intelligence-research-platform))

For academic research, this matters because the shortlist is not narrow or discipline-bound. Universities, public research organisations and industry are all represented, and the concepts explicitly link AI to **healthcare, science discovery, primary industries, aerospace, marine systems, energy networks, manufacturing and environmental monitoring**. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/concepts-selected-for-ai-research-platform))

### 2) Governance has moved from principle statements to operational controls

Otago’s policy now makes AI governance a formal Council responsibility and embeds **Te Tiriti obligations, data sovereignty, transparency, accountability, cultural appropriateness, and environmental impact** into the governance framework. AUT’s January 2026 research guidance goes further into practice, explicitly tying GenAI use to **Te Mana Raraunga**, the **CARE Principles**, and Pacific data sovereignty expectations. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/humanresources/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy?utm_source=openai))

The University of Canterbury’s February 2026 transcription guidance is a particularly strong sign of operational maturity: it says many AI transcription tools used by researchers are **not compliant with New Zealand privacy laws** and advises against their use for sensitive or confidential research data. By contrast, the University of Auckland has standardised access to approved tools and presents Gemini, NotebookLM and Copilot Chat as institutionally supported options with protected access and source-aware research assistance. ([canterbury.ac.nz](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/research/eresearch-at-canterbury/research-ai/ai-transcription))

### 3) Funders are now defining acceptable AI use more explicitly

Royal Society Te Apārangi’s national GenAI research guidance remains the main cross-sector reference point: humans remain accountable, disclosure is expected, and the Aotearoa context is explicitly framed through **Te Tiriti**, Māori data sovereignty, Pacific concerns, fairness and community benefit. ([royalsociety.org.nz](https://www.royalsociety.org.nz/assets/Guidelines-for-the-best-practice-use-of-GenAI-in-research_Royal-Society-Te-Aparangi_June-2025_English-.pdf))

Marsden 2026 sharpens this further. Applicants may use generative AI cautiously, but must take full responsibility for proposal content and citations. Panellists and external reviewers **must not** use LLMs or other generative AI technologies to assess proposals, although limited use of AI search tools is permitted for **referee finding** if confidential proposal content is not entered and outputs are independently checked. ([royalsociety.org.nz](https://www.royalsociety.org.nz/assets/MF-2026-EOI-Guidelines_v1-v2.2.pdf))

## Research and Case Studies

### Case Study 1: Health research is still the strongest adoption cluster

The HRC’s AI in Healthcare programme is still the clearest evidence that AI adoption has moved beyond pilot talk into funded translational research. The HRC says it funded **10 studies worth NZ$4.6 million**, with examples including:
- **AI-enabled diabetic retinopathy screening** at Health NZ Waitematā, focused on faster and more equitable screening delivery. ([hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/HRC_Annual-Report-2025-WEB_0.pdf))
- **AI-assisted acute stroke care** at Health NZ Capital, Coast and Hutt Valley, with projected gains of an additional **850 New Zealanders** accessing critical treatment each year if successful. ([hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/resources/research-repository/optimising-acute-stroke-care-artificial-intelligence?utm_source=openai))
- **Explainable AI for dementia risk factors** at the University of Auckland, using the Stats NZ Integrated Data Infrastructure with a stated emphasis on Māori, Pacific and disadvantaged populations. ([hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/resources/research-repository/leveraging-explainable-ai-identify-novel-risk-factors-dementia?utm_source=openai))
- **Radiology and dermatology implementation studies**, showing that the portfolio is not only about model building but also evaluation, workflow integration and equitable deployment. ([hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/resources/research-repository/transforming-radiology-new-zealand-accuracy-implementation-ai?utm_source=openai))

### Case Study 2: Healthy ageing has become a nationally significant AI collaboration

MBIE’s New Zealand–Singapore **Leveraging AI for Healthy Ageing 2025** programme remains one of the densest AI research clusters in the country. It funds three projects, each with **NZ$4 million** to the New Zealand partner, covering an **AI-driven dementia risk score**, **AI-assisted interRAI assessment**, and **AIMCura**, a mobile-first cognitive assessment and support platform. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/funding-information-and-opportunities/investment-funds/catalyst-fund/funded-projects/catalyst-strategic-new-zealand-singapore-leveraging-ai-for-healthy-ageing-2025?utm_source=openai))

This cluster matters because it combines **clinical need, large datasets, international collaboration, and pathway-to-implementation**, which is still the most mature formula for AI adoption in New Zealand academic research. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/funding-information-and-opportunities/investment-funds/catalyst-fund/funded-projects/catalyst-strategic-new-zealand-singapore-leveraging-ai-for-healthy-ageing-2025?utm_source=openai))

### Case Study 3: Science discovery and digital twins are rising fast

The most important non-clinical frontier is now AI-enabled **science discovery and digital twin infrastructure**. MBIE’s NZ–US biotech digital twin programme is investing **up to NZ$4.5 million over four years** in a partnership led by the Auckland Bioengineering Institute with the University of Texas at Austin’s Oden Institute. MBIE says the programme will build a **physics-informed AI platform** for “digital human” models and also extend methods to non-human digital twins through the Bioeconomy Science Institute. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/funding-information-and-opportunities/investment-funds/catalyst-fund/funded-projects/catalyst-strategic-new-zealand-united-states-biotech-digital-twin-research-programme?utm_source=openai))

This is significant because it connects AI not just to analytics, but to **simulation, intervention design, translational medicine, and commercialisable research infrastructure**. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/government-backs-nzus-digital-twin-collaboration?utm_source=openai))

### Case Study 4: Environmental and primary-sector research is building real-world AI capability

Earth Sciences New Zealand’s NIWA climate programme released the first **REMS-MR** AI downscaled climate dataset in 2025, with **more than 15,000 years of simulations** across **20+ global climate models** and multiple emissions scenarios. The stated goal is to make high-resolution climate projections far more scalable and useful for rare-event analysis and uncertainty quantification. ([niwa.co.nz](https://niwa.co.nz/climate/research-projects/physics-and-ai-based-high-resolution-climate-projections?utm_source=openai))

At the University of Canterbury, AI is being applied to field-scale and infrastructure problems: the aquaculture programme says UC-designed AI and 3D technology could yield **up to NZ$80 million per year** in immediate benefits to the mussel industry, while the Geospatial Research Institute has used AI super-resolution methods to lower the cost of global flood-risk mapping. ([canterbury.ac.nz](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/news-and-events/news/2025/ai-technology-research-to-revolutionise-nz-s-aquaculture-sector?utm_source=openai))

## Emerging Trends

### 1) New Zealand is moving toward a coordinated AI research stack

The sector now has the outlines of a national stack: **strategy**, **platform funding**, **ethics forms**, **funder rules**, **approved institutional tools**, and **shared infrastructure planning**. That does not mean uniform adoption, but it does mean AI is no longer peripheral to the research system. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/artificial-intelligence-strategy-and-business-guidance-now-available?utm_source=openai))

### 2) Māori and Pacific data sovereignty are not side issues; they are shaping adoption

Across AUT, Otago, Royal Society guidance, REANNZ infrastructure planning and multiple platform proposals, **data sovereignty, tikanga, Te Tiriti and Pacific values** appear as design constraints and governance requirements, not as afterthoughts. That remains one of the most distinctive features of New Zealand’s AI research environment. ([aut.ac.nz](https://www.aut.ac.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0004/1095682/AUT-Guidelines-for-the-Ethical-Use-of-GenAI-in-Research-January-2026.pdf))

### 3) The next bottleneck is likely to be shared compute, secure environments, and capability

REANNZ’s GPU procurement process and 2025/26 strategy update suggest that the sector sees **compute access, trusted environments, and coordinated infrastructure** as immediate practical bottlenecks. This implies the next phase of adoption will depend as much on national infrastructure and operating models as on new research ideas. That is an inference, but it is well supported by REANNZ’s explicit emphasis on AI, trusted research environments, data sovereignty and equitable access. ([reannz.co.nz](https://www.reannz.co.nz/news-and-events/request-for-information-ai-infrastructure-platform))

## Overall Assessment

As of **31 March 2026**, AI adoption in New Zealand academic research is best understood as **institutionalising, specialising, and preparing to scale**. Health remains the leading edge, but the national platform contest, digital twin investment, climate modelling work, and real-world AI proposals show the sector is broadening beyond healthcare into **science infrastructure, environmental intelligence, primary-sector optimisation and autonomous systems**. ([hrc.govt.nz](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/HRC_Annual-Report-2025-WEB_0.pdf))

The biggest update since **9 March 2026** is not that the sector suddenly became AI-saturated. It is that the system now looks more **decision-ready**: governance is firmer, platform choices are nearing resolution, funders have clarified the rules, and infrastructure planning is becoming explicitly AI-oriented. If the AI Research Platform is funded on schedule in **May 2026**, that will likely mark the clearest transition yet from project-by-project momentum to a more coordinated national research capability. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/humanresources/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy))
