# State of AI in New Zealand

**Updated: 31 March 2026**

## Executive Summary

March 2026 did not bring a blockbuster AI law or a surge of headline startup funding in Aotearoa. Instead, it made the national AI story more concrete and more institutional. The strongest new signals were around **operational assurance, governance, and infrastructure**: emergency-department AI scribes moved from ministerial announcement to clinician-reported time savings in practice; the Privacy Commissioner and National Cyber Security Centre published more detailed AI guidance; Inland Revenue disclosed a deeper, more mature internal AI programme; the University of Otago adopted a formal AI governance policy; and Southland’s proposed “AI factory” data centre cleared a major consenting hurdle. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/589774/emergency-doctors-estimate-ai-scribe-heidi-saving-up-to-10-minutes-per-patient?utm_source=openai))

The month’s centre of gravity, in other words, was **not hype**. It was the less glamorous but more meaningful work of deciding how AI will be used, governed, secured, and embedded in New Zealand institutions. That is a notable shift from earlier strategy-setting: the country now looks increasingly like a cautious adopter building operating rules around real deployments, rather than merely debating AI in the abstract. ([beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-ai-strategy-boost-productivity?utm_source=openai))

## What Happened in the Past Month

### Health remained the clearest proof point for scaled deployment

The previous snapshot identified health as the strongest real-world deployment story, and March reinforced that view. Health Minister Simeon Brown’s 28 February announcement said AI scribe technology was live across all emergency departments, covering 1,250 ED doctors and frontline staff. In mid-March, RNZ reported clinicians estimating the tool was saving **up to 10 minutes per patient**, while Health New Zealand’s digital innovation and AI lead said the system had been assessed against privacy, security, and contractual requirements. That combination matters: New Zealand is not just piloting AI in health, it is now defending and validating a nationwide frontline use case. ([beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/ai-scribe-now-every-emergency-department?utm_source=openai))

### Privacy, cyber, and regulatory agencies published more practical AI guardrails

A major March development was the Office of the Privacy Commissioner’s new guidance, published in late March, setting out how the **Privacy Act 2020 applies to AI tools**. The guidance is notable for how operational it is: it expects senior leadership approval, privacy impact assessments, transparency to affected people, engagement with Māori on risks to information taonga, human review of outputs, and controls to ensure personal information is not improperly retained or disclosed by AI tools. This is a step beyond high-level principle-setting and towards concrete organisational obligations. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/assets/New-order/Resources-/Publications/Guidance-resources/AI-Guidance-Resources-/AI-and-the-Information-Privacy-Principles.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Cyber agencies also sharpened their message. On 6 March, the National Cyber Security Centre published joint guidance on **AI and machine-learning supply chain risks**, explicitly warning that AI systems introduce vulnerabilities across data, models, software, hardware, and third-party services. That landed alongside New Zealand’s new Cyber Security Strategy 2026–2030, published on 27 February, which says AI is now a common tool for both defenders and malicious actors and highlights more realistic phishing as one of the immediate effects of generative AI. ([ncsc.govt.nz](https://www.ncsc.govt.nz/protect-your-organisation/artificial-intelligence-and-machine-learning-supply-chain-risks-and-mitigations/))

Regulatory thinking also broadened beyond privacy and deepfakes. The Commerce Commission published a paper on AI from the perspective of a **competition and consumer regulator**, arguing AI can lower costs and improve services but also create competition risks and deepen imbalances between businesses and consumers. The important signal is not that the Commission has launched AI-specific enforcement, but that AI is now being actively worked through inside mainstream economic regulation. ([comcom.govt.nz](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/pdf_file/0029/367481/Paper-Navigating-the-rise-of-AI-Perspectives-from-a-Competition-and-Consumer-Regulator.pdf))

### Inland Revenue offered one of the clearest views yet into agency-level AI adoption

One of the month’s most useful public documents was Inland Revenue’s 6 March information release on its AI use. The release shows a department well past the “should we try AI?” stage. Inland Revenue said a proof of concept using Genesys Cloud AI for call summaries produced an average **17% reduction in after-call work time**, with rollout planned to part of its customer and compliance operation. It also disclosed staged deployment of Microsoft Copilot with mandatory training, a proof of concept for Microsoft 365 Copilot, work on a GenAI web-assistant use case, and a private LLM-based test-scenario generator built with open-source tools. ([taxpolicy.ird.govt.nz](https://www.taxpolicy.ird.govt.nz/publications/2026/ir-use-of-ai))

Just as important, the release shows institutional scaffolding forming around that activity: executive AI fluency training, staff e-learning modules on ethics, risk and governance, engagement with the Government Chief Digital Officer, and participation in OECD work on trustworthy AI in tax administration. That makes Inland Revenue one of the clearest examples of a New Zealand agency moving from experimentation toward governed operational use. ([taxpolicy.ird.govt.nz](https://www.taxpolicy.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/tp/publications/2026/ir-use-of-ai.pdf?modified=20260312221429))

### Education’s clearest March move was governance, not mass classroom rollout

The most substantive higher-education development was the University of Otago’s **AI Governance Policy**, approved by Council and taking effect on 10 March. It applies across AI development, deployment, procurement, and use, and explicitly ties AI oversight to Te Tiriti obligations, Māori data sovereignty, privacy, cyber security, bias, cultural appropriateness, and environmental impact. It also requires approval before AI is used on confidential or restricted information and embeds oversight across Council committees and Senate. That is one of the strongest examples yet of a New Zealand institution formalising AI governance at board level. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/humanresources/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy))

By contrast, the school sector still looks earlier-stage. In a 3 March Ministry of Education bulletin, the Education Review Office said it was surveying school leaders and teachers on **how AI is currently being used, or not used, in schools**, with the results intended to inform policy, guidance, and practice. That is a meaningful signal that AI is now on the official education evidence-gathering agenda, but it also shows the sector is still mapping the landscape rather than rolling out a settled national approach. ([education.govt.nz](https://www.education.govt.nz/bulletins/school-leaders/03-03-26))

### Infrastructure and sovereign-capability debates became more tangible

The month’s clearest infrastructure headline was in Southland. RNZ reported in mid-March that Datagrid New Zealand had received resource consent for a **78,000-square-metre, 280MW data centre near Invercargill**, described as the country’s first “AI factory”. RNZ also reported related approvals for the landing of the company’s Tasman Ring cable at Oreti Beach. If built as proposed, the facility would become one of the country’s largest electricity users. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/589629/ai-illiterate-nz-at-risk-of-being-left-behind-as-data-centre-plans-move-forward?utm_source=openai))

This does not mean New Zealand suddenly has a domestic frontier-model industry. But it does mean the sovereignty and compute conversation is moving beyond vendor presentations and into land use, power, cables, regional development, and environmental trade-offs. That is a meaningful escalation in the seriousness of the infrastructure debate. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/589629/ai-illiterate-nz-at-risk-of-being-left-behind-as-data-centre-plans-move-forward?utm_source=openai))

## Trend Line Across Recent Snapshots

The clearest multi-month pattern is that **health still leads**, but governance is catching up fast. February’s story was national ED scribe rollout and breast-screening exploration; March added evidence that the scribe deployment is producing practical time savings, while privacy and cyber agencies published more detailed expectations around how AI should be used safely. ([beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/ai-scribe-now-every-emergency-department?utm_source=openai))

A second trend is that AI governance is **diffusing outward**. Earlier snapshots focused mainly on central-government strategy and harms debates. This month, the activity was more distributed: Inland Revenue disclosed operational use and internal capability-building; the Commerce Commission framed AI through competition and consumer law; and the University of Otago installed formal governance machinery. That suggests AI governance in New Zealand is broadening from ministerial strategy into agency, regulator, and institutional practice. ([taxpolicy.ird.govt.nz](https://www.taxpolicy.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/tp/publications/2026/ir-use-of-ai.pdf?modified=20260312221429))

A third pattern is that **sovereignty is becoming more concrete**. In January and February, that theme showed up mainly in onshore private-cloud and data-residency announcements. By March, it had broadened into a much larger physical-capacity conversation through Southland’s proposed AI-focused data centre, while cyber and privacy guidance continued to stress control over data, systems, and third-party dependencies. ([hpe.com](https://www.hpe.com/us/en/newsroom/press-release/2026/01/hpe-and-2degrees-collaborate-to-accelerate-ai-innovation-and-strengthen-data-sovereignty-in-new-zealand.html?utm_source=openai))

What has **not** happened yet is also important. The national AI research platform remains in process rather than launch mode: MBIE’s phase-two proposals were due on **31 March 2026**, with the final platform expected to be confirmed in late April and investment decisions in May, ahead of funding from July if confirmed. So the research and commercialisation pipeline is still moving, but March itself was more about positioning than breakthrough funding decisions. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/refocusing-the-science-innovation-and-technology-system/public-research-organisations/new-zealand-institute-for-advanced-technology/call-for-proposals-for-the-artificial-intelligence-research-platform))

## What Looks Quiet, Unchanged, or Early

New Zealand still does **not** have a new AI-specific law or dedicated AI regulator as the centrepiece of this month’s activity. The government’s overall posture remains the one set last year: pro-adoption, light-touch, and reliant on existing frameworks, while agencies and regulators add guidance and internal controls around privacy, cyber security, and responsible use. ([beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-ai-strategy-boost-productivity?utm_source=openai))

Public-service enablement is still more visible as a **work programme** than as a set of newly launched shared tools. The Digital Government work programme continues to point toward a central AI hub, accelerator capability, sandboxing, skills uplift, supplier agreements, and shared events, but March did not bring a comparable new nationwide public-service AI platform launch. ([digital.govt.nz](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-ai-work-programme))

The education story also remains mixed. Otago’s governance move was substantial, but at the school level the state of play still looks exploratory, with ERO surveying usage and the Ministry’s digital bulletin mainly signalling platform and privacy implications rather than a system-wide AI teaching model. ([otago.ac.nz](https://www.otago.ac.nz/humanresources/policies/policy-collection/ai-governance-policy))

And while infrastructure discussion intensified, the month was relatively quiet on **big local startup or venture-capital moments**. The most visible enterprise signal was heavy infrastructure and institutional adoption, not a wave of new Kiwi AI firms announcing major raises or exits. That does not mean startup activity is absent; it means it was not the defining public signal of March. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/589629/ai-illiterate-nz-at-risk-of-being-left-behind-as-data-centre-plans-move-forward?utm_source=openai))

## Overall Assessment

March 2026 makes New Zealand’s AI trajectory look **broader, more serious, and more administratively real**. AI is now showing up not just in strategy documents and ministerial speeches, but in emergency departments, tax administration workflows, cyber guidance, privacy expectations, university governance, and physical compute infrastructure planning. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/589774/emergency-doctors-estimate-ai-scribe-heidi-saving-up-to-10-minutes-per-patient?utm_source=openai))

The deeper reading of the month is that Aotearoa is entering an **AI operations** phase. Health remains the clearest deployment story, but the bigger change is that multiple institutions are now building the controls, governance, and infrastructure needed to make AI use durable. That is healthier than hype, but it is also slower and less spectacular. ([beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/ai-scribe-now-every-emergency-department?utm_source=openai))

So the direction of travel is positive, but still measured. Momentum is broadening across sectors, yet it remains concentrated in adoption, assurance, and enabling infrastructure rather than frontier-model creation or large-scale domestic capital formation. If February showed New Zealand moving from AI strategy to frontline use, March showed the next step: **building the institutions that make AI use governable**. ([beehive.govt.nz](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-ai-strategy-boost-productivity?utm_source=openai))
