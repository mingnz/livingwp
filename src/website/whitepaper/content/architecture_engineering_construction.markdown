# **Title**

AI Adoption in the AEC Sector in New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper

Updated: 31 March 2026

# **Introduction**

- Publicly documented AI adoption in New Zealand’s AEC system has continued to move from general awareness into targeted operational use over the past 12 months. The strongest evidence is in computer-vision safety monitoring, property and consent administration, planning and risk analytics, and builder due-diligence rather than in end-to-end autonomous project delivery. ([downergroup.co.nz](https://www.downergroup.co.nz/Content/cms/media/2025/2025_FY_Results/Downer_2025_Sustainability_Report__spreads_.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- The operating environment has become clearer. New Zealand now has a national AI strategy and business guidance, a public-service AI framework and GenAI guidance, an AI toolkit for agencies, an AI Expert Advisory Panel, and new biometric privacy rules that matter directly for AEC uses such as site monitoring and public-sector workflows. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/artificial-intelligence-strategy-and-business-guidance-now-available?utm_source=openai))
- Organised sector capability is also more visible. The AI Forum NZ AEC Working Group is now the clearest public coordination point for the sector, framing AI adoption around assessment, upskilling, information sharing, innovation, and governance, and linking this work to the AI Blueprint for Aotearoa and infrastructure policy. ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/our-work/working-groups/architecture-engineering-and-construction-aec-working-group/?utm_source=openai))
- Evidence remains uneven, and that unevenness is itself an important finding. Public, non-promotional evidence is still much thinner for architecture-led design generation, structural-engineering production workflows, offsite manufacturing, and AI-enabled robotics on live New Zealand projects than it is for safety, admin, planning, and research-led applications. ([downergroup.co.nz](https://www.downergroup.co.nz/Content/cms/media/2025/2025_FY_Results/Downer_2025_Sustainability_Report__spreads_.pdf?utm_source=openai))

# **Policy and Frameworks**

- On 8 July 2025, MBIE released New Zealand’s first national AI strategy alongside responsible AI guidance for businesses. For AEC organisations, this provides the main national reference point for lawful, OECD-aligned adoption, with emphasis on fairness, privacy, robustness, security, and safety rather than AEC-specific regulation. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/artificial-intelligence-strategy-and-business-guidance-now-available?utm_source=openai))
- For public-sector AEC agencies, infrastructure owners, and regulators, the most relevant operating documents are the Public Service AI Framework, the Responsible AI Guidance for the Public Service: GenAI, and the Public Service AI Toolkit. Together they set expectations around governance, human oversight, privacy, assurance, procurement, records management, and agency policy settings. ([digital.govt.nz](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-artificial-intelligence-framework?utm_source=openai))
- The Government Chief Digital Officer strengthened stewardship on 30 June 2025 by appointing an AI Expert Advisory Panel. This matters to AEC because planning, consenting, infrastructure operations, and asset governance increasingly sit within agencies being asked to adopt AI consistently and responsibly. ([digital.govt.nz](https://www.digital.govt.nz/news/government-chief-digital-officer-appoints-ai-expert-advisory-panel-to-guide-responsible-ai-use-in-public-service?utm_source=openai))
- Privacy settings also materially shifted. The Privacy Commissioner announced the Biometric Processing Privacy Code in August 2025, creating more specific rules for biometric processing. This has direct relevance to AEC uses of computer vision, worker monitoring, access control, and other image-based systems that could capture sensitive personal information. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/tuhono-connect/statements-media-releases/privacy-commissioner-announces-new-rules-for-biometrics/?utm_source=openai))
- Sector alignment with the AI Blueprint for Aotearoa is clearest through the AI Forum NZ AEC Working Group. The group states that it emerged from the 2024 AI Blueprint workshops, is guided by the 2024 Blueprint and 2025 update, and has already made a public submission calling for stronger AI integration, digital enablement, workforce capability, and inclusive infrastructure outcomes. ([aiforum.org.nz](https://aiforum.org.nz/our-work/working-groups/architecture-engineering-and-construction-aec-working-group/?utm_source=openai))
- At agency level, Hutt City Council provides one of the clearest public examples of operational AI governance intersecting with AEC. Its public material describes an AI strategy, AI policy, AI risk management framework, governance group, and public AI register covering property, consent, and traffic-management workflows. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/our-projects/ai-at-council))

- 8 July 2025: MBIE released New Zealand’s AI strategy and responsible AI business guidance, giving AEC firms a clearer national policy baseline. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/artificial-intelligence-strategy-and-business-guidance-now-available?utm_source=openai))
- Early August 2025: the AI Forum NZ AEC Working Group submitted on the Draft National Infrastructure Plan, explicitly calling for stronger AI integration and digital enablement across the infrastructure system. While not all of this is AEC-specific, part of the programme directly touches LIMs, resource consents, BWOFs, and building-consent workflows. - 23 October 2025: the University of Auckland publicised research on AI-driven city planning, highlighting stakeholder concerns about bias, transparency, accountability, and the risk of overlooking vulnerable communities in planning and infrastructure decisions. ([futurefive.co.nz](https://futurefive.co.nz/story/new-zealand-ai-tool-launched-to-expose-risks-in-building-sector?utm_source=openai))

# **Research Overview**

## **Industry-led research and reports**

- Within the past 12 months, publicly accessible industry evidence has been stronger in reporting and case documentation than in formal independent sector studies. The clearest materials are Downer’s sustainability reporting on AI safety, Hutt City Council’s public AI register and property-services workflow disclosures, and the AI Forum NZ AEC Working Group’s infrastructure-policy submission. ([downergroup.co.nz](https://www.downergroup.co.nz/Content/cms/media/2025/2025_FY_Results/Downer_2025_Sustainability_Report__spreads_.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- Hutt City Council’s public AI register is unusually detailed by New Zealand standards. It names specific AEC-relevant assistants for traffic-management plan review, BWOF renewal vetting, building-consent vetting, building-consent enquiries, LIM processing, and resource-consent workflow tasks. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/our-projects/ai-at-council))
- Engineering New Zealand’s 2025–2026 events are also a useful capability signal. Public event material shows demand for discussion of AI in the built environment, including workshops on AI in building design, construction technology, infrastructure systems, and design-and-construction research using machine learning and generative AI. ([engineeringnz.org](https://www.engineeringnz.org/courses-events/event/ai-for-the-built-environment/?utm_source=openai))
- A notable gap remains: there is still limited publicly available, non-promotional New Zealand evidence that measures AI outcomes across multiple AEC firms or projects in a consistent way. The current public record is still dominated by isolated case examples and organisation-specific disclosures. This is an inference from the reviewed source set. ([downergroup.co.nz](https://www.downergroup.co.nz/Content/cms/media/2025/2025_FY_Results/Downer_2025_Sustainability_Report__spreads_.pdf?utm_source=openai))

## **Summary of AI in AEC research capabilities at NZ universities and recent research studies**

- University of Canterbury currently provides the clearest publicly visible built-environment AI research example in this review period. Its Geospatial Research Institute publicised a deep-learning model, Joint Spatial Propagation Super-Resolution, to improve open elevation datasets for flood modelling, stormwater design, transport planning, and broader infrastructure and urban planning. ([canterbury.ac.nz](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/news-and-events/news/2025/uc-innovation-makes-global-flood-risk-mapping-affordable---?utm_source=openai))
- The University of Auckland has publicly visible capability in AI-related planning research. In October 2025 it described a study, led from the School of Architecture and Planning, on the ethical and social risks of AI-driven planning, drawing on stakeholder input across Auckland, Wellington, Hamilton, and Christchurch. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/10/23/can-ai-driven-city-planning-be-fair-and-inclusive.html))
- Additional University of Auckland capability is visible through Engineering New Zealand’s December 2025 event material, which described research applying machine learning to seismic-performance data from the Canterbury Earthquake Sequence and an AI-powered framework to bridge design geometry and construction logic. Public visibility here is stronger through professional dissemination than through a readily sourceable open paper in the reviewed material. ([engineeringnz.org](https://www.engineeringnz.org/courses-events/design-and-construction-with-ai-learning-from-data/?utm_source=openai))
- Massey University contributed a 2025 paper, “Generative AI, Large Language Models, and ChatGPT in Construction Education, Training, and Practice,” which examined how GenAI and LLMs are affecting construction education, workforce development, and professional practice, while also highlighting regulatory and interoperability barriers. ([mro.massey.ac.nz](https://mro.massey.ac.nz/items/d39cc912-90ae-4975-9e1a-b65ee660c1bc))
- Auckland University of Technology contributed a 2025 paper, “Wasting Time: AI, Construction Waste and the Problem of Estimation,” which reported promising pilot results from using AI to categorise and quantify large volumes of construction waste without manual intervention. ([openrepository.aut.ac.nz](https://openrepository.aut.ac.nz/items/a765685c-effe-4171-a6e8-3b923ca5b51d))
- Taken together, the public research record is strongest in planning, resilience, education, and waste-related applications. It is weaker in openly published evidence of scaled commercial deployment in architecture practice, structural engineering production, or robotics-led construction delivery. ([canterbury.ac.nz](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/news-and-events/news/2025/uc-innovation-makes-global-flood-risk-mapping-affordable---?utm_source=openai))

# **Case Studies**

## **Downer, RUSH Digital, and Te Ara Tupua Alliance: AI computer vision for worksite safety**

- Context: road and transport worksites are dynamic, high-risk environments where traditional monitoring can miss fast-developing hazards. Hutt City Council described a live AI traffic-management trial at Tupua Horo Nuku, while Downer later documented broader operational use through its sustainability reporting. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/people-and-communities/news/2025/ai-driving-safety-changes-at-tupua-horo-nuku?utm_source=openai))
- AI method: computer vision and machine-learning models applied to site-camera feeds to detect exclusion-zone breaches, speed issues, PPE non-compliance, worker-down events, and people/plant interaction risks. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/people-and-communities/news/2025/ai-driving-safety-changes-at-tupua-horo-nuku?utm_source=openai))
- Application: road construction, traffic management, and transport-infrastructure environments. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/people-and-communities/news/2025/ai-driving-safety-changes-at-tupua-horo-nuku?utm_source=openai))
- Observed outcomes: public reporting described improved safety monitoring, better visibility of unsafe behaviours and high-risk locations, improved PPE compliance, and reduced pedestrian/mobile-plant interface risk. Hutt City Council also noted privacy protection was built into the pilot. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/people-and-communities/news/2025/ai-driving-safety-changes-at-tupua-horo-nuku?utm_source=openai))

## **Hutt City Council: AI in building, property, and consent workflows**

- Context: councils face time pressure, fragmented systems, and repetitive administrative work across property and regulatory services. Hutt City Council’s AI programme is one of the clearest public examples of AI being applied to AEC-adjacent public administration in New Zealand. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/our-projects/ai-at-council))
- AI method: generative AI assistants, workflow automation, and AI-supported search and summarisation across internal systems and regulatory processes. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/our-projects/ai-at-council))
- Application: BWOF renewal vetting, building-consent vetting, building-consent enquiry responses, traffic-management plan review, LIM automation, and parts of resource-consent processing and invoicing. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/our-projects/ai-at-council))
- Observed outcomes: Hutt City Council reported that resource-consent invoicing automation saves 3–5 minutes per invoice, with up to 100 invoices a week during busy periods. At programme level, the council separately reported 44,000 annual staff hours reclaimed and NZ$900,000 in annual savings across the broader AI-Volution initiative, though those totals are not stated as property-services-only outcomes. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/our-projects/ai-at-council))

## **University of Canterbury: AI-enhanced elevation data for infrastructure and resilience**

- Context: high-quality elevation data is expensive but critical for flood-risk assessment, stormwater design, infrastructure planning, and climate adaptation. ([canterbury.ac.nz](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/news-and-events/news/2025/uc-innovation-makes-global-flood-risk-mapping-affordable---?utm_source=openai))
- AI method: a deep-learning model, Joint Spatial Propagation Super-Resolution, that enhances open satellite elevation data. ([canterbury.ac.nz](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/news-and-events/news/2025/uc-innovation-makes-global-flood-risk-mapping-affordable---?utm_source=openai))
- Application: flood-risk assessment, stormwater design, transport planning, infrastructure and urban planning, and wider resilience work. ([canterbury.ac.nz](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/news-and-events/news/2025/uc-innovation-makes-global-flood-risk-mapping-affordable---?utm_source=openai))
- Observed outcomes: UC reported a ten-fold improvement in spatial resolution and about a 72% improvement in elevation accuracy over basic datasets, while noting the method is not a full substitute for LiDAR. Public evidence remains research-led rather than tied to a named commercial project deployment. ([canterbury.ac.nz](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/news-and-events/news/2025/uc-innovation-makes-global-flood-risk-mapping-affordable---?utm_source=openai))

## **CheckMyBuilder: AI-assisted builder due diligence**

- Context: builder-risk information in New Zealand is often fragmented across registers, court filings, notices, and media sources, making due diligence slow and uneven. ([futurefive.co.nz](https://futurefive.co.nz/story/new-zealand-ai-tool-launched-to-expose-risks-in-building-sector?utm_source=openai))
- AI method: AI-assisted search, extraction, and pattern detection across public records to identify insolvency history, liquidation notices, repeated-director patterns, and other risk indicators. ([futurefive.co.nz](https://futurefive.co.nz/story/new-zealand-ai-tool-launched-to-expose-risks-in-building-sector?utm_source=openai))
- Application: homeowner, lender, supplier, and advisor due diligence before signing building contracts or releasing money. ([checkmybuilder.co.nz](https://checkmybuilder.co.nz/faq?utm_source=openai))
- Observed outcomes: FutureFive reported more than 1,000 reports generated early in the tool’s life. The public evidence suggests demand is real, but independent validation of accuracy, false positives, and downstream decision quality is not yet publicly visible. ([futurefive.co.nz](https://futurefive.co.nz/story/new-zealand-ai-tool-launched-to-expose-risks-in-building-sector?utm_source=openai))

# **Trends and Outlook**

- New Zealand adoption is clustering around narrow, high-friction tasks rather than around fully autonomous design or construction delivery. Current evidence points to safety monitoring, regulatory administration, planning analytics, and due diligence as the leading use cases. ([downergroup.co.nz](https://www.downergroup.co.nz/Content/cms/media/2025/2025_FY_Results/Downer_2025_Sustainability_Report__spreads_.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- Public-sector AEC workflows are emerging as a particularly important adoption frontier. Hutt City Council’s disclosures suggest that LIMs, resource consents, BWOFs, building-consent checks, and traffic-management review are becoming practical early targets for AI because they are document-heavy, repetitive, and time-sensitive. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/our-projects/ai-at-council))
- Safety remains the strongest live-project deployment signal. The Downer/RUSH material is still the clearest publicly documented example of AI being used directly on New Zealand worksites to change operational safety practice. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/people-and-communities/news/2025/ai-driving-safety-changes-at-tupua-horo-nuku?utm_source=openai))
- The New Zealand startup pattern is still early and appears workflow-specific rather than platform-wide. The most visible categories in this review period are AI safety analytics and AI-driven due diligence/compliance intelligence. Public evidence is not yet strong enough to describe a mature national AEC AI startup segment. ([rush.co.nz](https://www.rush.co.nz/about/r-vision?utm_source=openai))
- Governance is moving closer to operations. Privacy rules for biometrics, public-service AI guidance, agency AI registers, and city-planning research on fairness all indicate that trust, accountability, and explainability are now operational constraints, not background issues. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/tuhono-connect/statements-media-releases/privacy-commissioner-announces-new-rules-for-biometrics/?utm_source=openai))
- The main risks emerging from the reviewed material are privacy and surveillance concerns, bias and inequity in planning and public decisions, hallucination and accountability problems in document-heavy workflows, fragmented data environments, and uneven workforce capability. ([privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/tuhono-connect/statements-media-releases/privacy-commissioner-announces-new-rules-for-biometrics/?utm_source=openai))
- A reasonable inference from the current public evidence is that independent outcome evaluation remains scarce. Most NZ sources describe pilots, organisation-reported results, or research outputs rather than third-party assessments of long-term value, error rates, or failure modes. ([downergroup.co.nz](https://www.downergroup.co.nz/Content/cms/media/2025/2025_FY_Results/Downer_2025_Sustainability_Report__spreads_.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- Early business-model signals point more to augmentation than replacement. The most visible pattern is AI being layered onto existing services: safety-as-a-service, due-diligence-as-a-service, and AI-assisted regulatory or advisory workflows built around domain knowledge and local data. ([downergroup.co.nz](https://www.downergroup.co.nz/Content/cms/media/2025/2025_FY_Results/Downer_2025_Sustainability_Report__spreads_.pdf?utm_source=openai))

# **A Global Perspective**

- In economies comparable to New Zealand, the strongest recent signal is structured enablement rather than ungoverned experimentation. Singapore announced a S$30 million Built Environment AI Centre of Excellence in February 2026 to develop AI-driven solutions for built-environment challenges such as labour shortages and climate impacts, while also aiming to grow “AI bilingual” talent with both technical and sector expertise. ([www1.bca.gov.sg](https://www1.bca.gov.sg/about-us/news-and-publications/media-releases/2026/02/05/stronger-support-and-partnerships-to-bring-urban-innovations-to-the-market?utm_source=openai))
- The reviewed global material shows AI use concentrating in a familiar set of AEC tasks: proposal and bid drafting, program and project management, approval acceleration, workflow automation, and building-energy optimisation. This suggests that global adoption, like New Zealand’s, is currently strongest where AI can remove friction from information-heavy processes. ([aecom.com](https://aecom.com/wp-content/uploads/documents/reports/2025/AECOM_2025_Annual_Report.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- Public disclosure from major firms indicates that internal AI capability is becoming strategic infrastructure. AECOM’s 2025 annual report describes an “AI for Engineering” platform embedded across proposal, bidding, program-management, and delivery workflows; an AI governance policy introduced in 2025; and a team of more than 200 specialists in AI, machine learning, mathematics, physics, and data science. ([aecom.com](https://aecom.com/wp-content/uploads/documents/reports/2025/AECOM_2025_Annual_Report.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- The visible impacts in these global sources are acceleration and standardisation rather than full automation of professional judgement. AECOM describes reduced rework, streamlined approvals, and faster delivery, while ACEC frames AI as accelerating human talent in engineering firms rather than replacing it outright. ([aecom.com](https://aecom.com/wp-content/uploads/documents/reports/2025/AECOM_2025_Annual_Report.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- The main global risks and learnings are consistent with New Zealand’s trajectory: firms need stronger data handling, governance, liability management, and workforce capability; and moving from pilot enthusiasm to reliable operational use remains difficult. ([acec.org](https://www.acec.org/resource/why-and-how-a-primer-on-ai-integration-for-engineering-firms-from-the-acec-technology-committee/?utm_source=openai))
- On startup patterns, the reviewed global sources are clearer on tool categories than on a stable named-startup set. The main commercially visible categories are inspection and computer vision, document and knowledge automation, workflow copilots, and optimisation tools such as AI-controlled energy systems. ([www1.bca.gov.sg](https://www1.bca.gov.sg/about-us/news-and-publications/media-releases/2026/02/05/stronger-support-and-partnerships-to-bring-urban-innovations-to-the-market?utm_source=openai))
- The clearest business-model signal globally is the rise of AI-enabled professional services: firms combining domain expertise, proprietary data, workflow integration, and governance controls around AI capabilities. That model is highly relevant to New Zealand, where local standards, public accountability, and fragmented project information remain defining constraints. ([aecom.com](https://aecom.com/wp-content/uploads/documents/reports/2025/AECOM_2025_Annual_Report.pdf?utm_source=openai))

# **Conclusion**

- The overall assessment is that AI adoption in New Zealand AEC is now real and publicly visible, but still uneven and early-stage. The strongest documented activity is in safety monitoring, regulatory and property-service workflows, planning and resilience analytics, and due-diligence intelligence. ([downergroup.co.nz](https://www.downergroup.co.nz/Content/cms/media/2025/2025_FY_Results/Downer_2025_Sustainability_Report__spreads_.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- The strategic shift over the past 12 months is not simply that more organisations are talking about AI; it is that New Zealand now has a clearer institutional setting for AEC use. National strategy, public-service guidance, privacy guardrails, and sector coordination through the AI Forum NZ AEC Working Group have made the environment more legible for firms and agencies. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/artificial-intelligence-strategy-and-business-guidance-now-available?utm_source=openai))
- The most important limitation remains the evidence base itself. Public documentation is still much stronger for pilots, workflow tools, and research demonstrations than for independently evaluated, scaled deployment across core design, engineering production, offsite manufacturing, or robotics-led construction. ([canterbury.ac.nz](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/news-and-events/news/2025/uc-innovation-makes-global-flood-risk-mapping-affordable---?utm_source=openai))
- The next system-level steps implied by the evidence are clear: more publicly documented New Zealand case studies with measured outcomes; more sector-specific governance patterns for consent, surveillance, and public-sector use; stronger data and records foundations; and continued capability building across firms, councils, and professional bodies. ([huttcity.govt.nz](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/our-projects/ai-at-council))
- If those steps occur, the most plausible near-term trajectory is not wholesale disruption but practical embedding: AI becoming a trusted layer inside existing AEC services to improve safety, speed, transparency, and decision quality in places where operational and regulatory friction is currently highest. ([downergroup.co.nz](https://www.downergroup.co.nz/Content/cms/media/2025/2025_FY_Results/Downer_2025_Sustainability_Report__spreads_.pdf?utm_source=openai))
