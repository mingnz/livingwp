# AI in Academia in Aotearoa New Zealand: A Living Whitepaper
**Updated: 9 March 2026**

## Introduction

AI adoption in New Zealand academia has moved beyond experimentation. Across universities, polytechnics, and research organisations, the focus has shifted from emergency responses to generative AI toward more structured work on governance, assessment redesign, research integrity, student guidance, and operational deployment. National policy is also becoming more enabling: the Government’s 2025 AI strategy explicitly prioritises adoption and application, while the 2025–2030 Tertiary Education Strategy places innovation, research impact, and industry alignment at the centre of future investment planning. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/digital-policy/new-zealands-ai-strategy-investing-with-confidence))

## Executive Snapshot

- **The sector is operationalising AI, not just trialling it.** An Australasian tertiary benchmark published in December 2025 found a **29% increase in operational maturity** from 2024 to 2025, with institutions moving from pilots toward structured deployment; the main barriers were **resourcing constraints (35%)** and **systemic governance issues (28%)**. ([open-publishing.org](https://open-publishing.org/publications/index.php/APUB/article/download/2786/2572/10796))
- **Assessment is the main pressure point.** Universities are redesigning assessment and tightening disclosure rules, while confidence in AI-detection tools has weakened. RNZ reported in September 2025 that several New Zealand universities had stepped back from AI-detection software because of reliability concerns. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/574517/universities-give-up-using-software-to-detect-ai-in-students-work/))
- **Student use is already mainstream.** At the University of Auckland, **80%** of survey respondents said they had used GenAI for study, while **5%** said they had used it in assessment when it was prohibited; **66.9%** wanted clearer instructions on permissible use. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/assets/staff/learning-teaching/Student_Feedback/Gen-AI_Survey_Highlights_Oct-2024.pdf))
- **Research governance is catching up quickly.** Royal Society Te Apārangi issued national best-practice guidance for GenAI in research in June 2025, centring te Tiriti o Waitangi, Māori data sovereignty, and Pacific data sovereignty. AUT followed with detailed institutional guidance in January 2026. ([royalsociety.org.nz](https://www.royalsociety.org.nz/assets/Guidelines-for-the-best-practice-use-of-GenAI-in-research_Royal-Society-Te-Aparangi_June-2025_English-.pdf))
- **New capability-building is visible across the system.** Examples include Massey’s new AI undergraduate major, Waikato’s AI Institute and sovereign-AI language work, Victoria University of Wellington’s AI policy and research leadership push, and Open Polytechnic’s NZQA-accredited GenAI micro-credential. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/about/news/new-artificial-intelligence-major-set-to-launch-in-2026/))

## Current News and Market Developments

### 1) National policy is becoming more supportive

- MBIE’s **July 2025 AI strategy** deliberately emphasises **AI adoption and application**, rather than trying to compete on frontier-model development. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/digital-policy/new-zealands-ai-strategy-investing-with-confidence))
- In research, MBIE’s proposed **Artificial Intelligence Research Platform** is intended to strengthen national AI capability and commercialisation, with **up to $70 million over seven years** available in phase 2; successful phase-1 concepts included the **University of Auckland** and a joint **University of Waikato/University of Canterbury** concept. ([mbie.govt.nz](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/refocusing-the-science-innovation-and-technology-system/public-research-organisations/new-zealand-institute-for-advanced-technology/call-for-proposals-for-the-artificial-intelligence-research-platform))
- The **Tertiary Education Strategy 2025–2030**, released in December 2025, requires providers seeking 2027 funding to show how they will support innovation, economic impact, collaboration, and stronger research-industry links. ([tec.govt.nz](https://www.tec.govt.nz/about-us/news-and-consultations/news-and-consultations/government-releases-new-tertiary-education-strategy-for-2025-2030))

### 2) Assessment policy is rapidly evolving

- Massey’s **May 2025** assessment policy says GenAI may support, but not substitute for, students’ intellectual work; use must align to an assessment-specific AI framework, be disclosed, and may require prompt records or version histories. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))
- The University of Waikato’s public student guidance still stated that **from 2025** it would use Turnitin functionality to identify work resulting from GenAI use. ([waikato.ac.nz](https://www.waikato.ac.nz/students/student-assessment-handbook/gen-ai/))
- At the same time, RNZ reported on **30 September 2025** that universities including Massey had moved away from AI-detection software because of concerns it “doesn't work overly well,” with more emphasis instead going onto assessment design and validation. ([rnz.co.nz](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/574517/universities-give-up-using-software-to-detect-ai-in-students-work/))

**What this means:** New Zealand academia is not converging on a single model. It is converging on a principle: **assessment must be redesigned for an AI-pervasive environment**, but institutions still differ on how much detection, disclosure, and controlled use they permit. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))

## Research Overview

### National and sector research

- **Ako Aotearoa’s AI in Education project** describes itself as a snapshot of how AI was being integrated into adult tertiary education in Aotearoa New Zealand as of **year-end 2024**, spanning universities, ITPs, and PTEs, and producing multiple volumes intended to build educator and organisational capability. ([ako.ac.nz](https://ako.ac.nz/knowledge-centre/integration-of-ai-in-adult-tertiary-education))
- A second Ako Aotearoa output, **Empowering Aotearoa: An inclusive approach to AI literacy in tertiary education** was published in **December 2025**, signalling that AI literacy is now being treated as a system capability, not just an institutional choice. ([ako.ac.nz](https://ako.ac.nz/assets/Knowledge-centre/45195-An-Inclusive-Approach-to-AI-Literacy/REPORT-Empowering-Aotearoa.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- The **ACODE 2025 whitepaper** shows that across Australasian tertiary institutions, governance is improving but still uneven: **58%** had formal AI ethics policies or guidelines, **38%** were still developing them, and only **10%** had comprehensive frameworks addressing learner wellbeing. ([open-publishing.org](https://open-publishing.org/publications/index.php/APUB/article/download/2786/2572/10796))

### Research integrity and data governance

- Royal Society Te Apārangi’s **June 2025** guidelines state that GenAI use in research in Aotearoa must account for **te Tiriti obligations**, **Māori data sovereignty**, and **Pacific data sovereignty**, and explicitly frame Māori knowledge, language, and data as taonga. ([royalsociety.org.nz](https://www.royalsociety.org.nz/assets/Guidelines-for-the-best-practice-use-of-GenAI-in-research_Royal-Society-Te-Aparangi_June-2025_English-.pdf))
- AUT’s **January 2026** research guidance operationalises that at institutional level: it warns against uploading confidential participant information, cultural data, unpublished findings, or export-controlled information to AI tools; it also says AUT-provided tools such as **AUTGPT** or local/offline tools are generally preferable from a privacy and security perspective. ([aut.ac.nz](https://www.aut.ac.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0004/1095682/AUT-Guidelines-for-the-Ethical-Use-of-GenAI-in-Research-January-2026.pdf))

## Case Studies

### University of Auckland: mainstream student use and enterprise deployment

- The University of Auckland’s 2024 student survey, published by the University in 2025, found **80%** of respondents had used GenAI for study, most commonly for explaining difficult concepts, summarising readings, and generating ideas; a majority said AI improved learning, knowledge, and productivity, but only **27.8%** rated it as reliable. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/assets/staff/learning-teaching/Student_Feedback/Gen-AI_Survey_Highlights_Oct-2024.pdf))
- On the operational side, Auckland’s AI Assistant was handling **over 9,000 conversations** and **60,000 searches per month** by October 2025, with the University reporting more interactions through the assistant than incoming phone calls. The project also received CAUDIT recognition and a Tertiary ICT Excellence Award. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/10/07/ai-assistant-enhances-digital-experience-at-uoa.html))

### Massey University: from governance to credential innovation

- Massey’s assessment policy formalised an **AI Use Framework** in 2025, signalling a move away from blanket prohibition toward controlled, disclosed use. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))
- In August 2025, Massey announced a new **AI major** in the Bachelor of Information Sciences for **2026**, describing it as the **first NZ university AI undergraduate major that can be completed entirely by distance**. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/about/news/new-artificial-intelligence-major-set-to-launch-in-2026/))

### University of Waikato: research infrastructure and sovereign AI

- Waikato’s **Artificial Intelligence Institute** positions itself as an interdisciplinary flagship for AI research and engagement; the University says its long-running machine learning software ecosystem includes tools such as **WEKA**, with more than **10 million downloads**. ([waikato.ac.nz](https://www.waikato.ac.nz/int/research/institutes-centres-entities/institutes/artificial-intelligence-institute/?utm_source=openai))
- In September 2025, Waikato highlighted the **first AI-generated Waikato-Maniapoto voice**, developed to support dialect preservation and described as part of the Institute’s commitment to **sovereign AI solutions**. ([waikato.ac.nz](https://www.waikato.ac.nz/int/news-events/news/a-voice-for-the-future-the-first-ai-generated-waikato-maniapoto-voice/?utm_source=openai))

### Victoria University of Wellington: policy, research leadership, and public-sector influence

- Victoria University of Wellington announced in December 2024 a **Bob Dykes Chair in Generative AI**, intended to build national capability, mentor future leaders, and connect with policy and industry stakeholders. ([wgtn.ac.nz](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/news/2024/12/victoria-university-of-wellington-leading-the-way-with-first-of-its-kind-chair-in-generative-ai?utm_source=openai))
- By 2025, the University had also established an **AI in Teaching and Learning Hub**, and its Policy Hub was acting as secretariat for the Government Chief Digital Officer’s **AI Expert Advisor Panel**. ([wgtn.ac.nz](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/teaching-support/victeach/hubs/ai-in-teaching-and-learning-hub?utm_source=openai))
- Its Centre for Data Science and AI reported in late 2025 that it had grown to **over 40 staff and 60 PhD students**, reinforcing Wellington’s role as a national AI research node. ([wgtn.ac.nz](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/cdsai/news/inaugural-cdsai-research-showcase-highlights-innovation-and-impact?utm_source=openai))

### Open Polytechnic: workforce-oriented AI literacy

- Open Polytechnic launched an **NZQA-accredited Introduction to Generative AI micro-credential** in June 2025 with Spark. The course includes **ethical use**, **Māori data sovereignty**, and practical workflow application; it is **40 learning hours**, **Level 3**, and priced at **$99**. ([openpolytechnic.ac.nz](https://www.openpolytechnic.ac.nz/about-us/news-publications-and-research/news/open-polytechnic-launches-new-introduction-to-generative-ai-micro-credential/))

## Key Trends

### 1) AI literacy is becoming core academic infrastructure
AI is increasingly being treated like digital literacy or information literacy: a cross-cutting capability for students, academics, and professional staff rather than a niche technical specialism. This is visible in Auckland’s student survey, Open Polytechnic’s micro-credential, Waikato’s short-course ecosystem, and Ako Aotearoa’s AI literacy work. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/assets/staff/learning-teaching/Student_Feedback/Gen-AI_Survey_Highlights_Oct-2024.pdf))

### 2) Governance is shifting from “can students use AI?” to “under what conditions?”
The dominant model now is conditional use, disclosure, and redesign of assessment rather than simple prohibition. Where institutions differ is in enforcement design, confidence in detectors, and how explicit the permitted-use framework is. ([massey.ac.nz](https://www.massey.ac.nz/documents/1445/Generative_Artificial_Intelligence_in_Assessment_Policy.pdf))

### 3) Māori and Pacific data sovereignty are central, not peripheral
In New Zealand academia, AI policy is not being framed only around privacy, bias, and copyright. It is also being framed around **taonga**, **te Tiriti**, **indigenous data governance**, and the risk of culturally homogenising AI systems. ([royalsociety.org.nz](https://www.royalsociety.org.nz/assets/Guidelines-for-the-best-practice-use-of-GenAI-in-research_Royal-Society-Te-Aparangi_June-2025_English-.pdf))

### 4) Research and operations are converging
Universities are adopting AI in teaching, research support, and administration simultaneously. Auckland’s enterprise assistant, Victoria’s public-policy role, Waikato’s institute model, and AUT’s research-tool governance all point to AI becoming part of institutional operating models rather than a standalone edtech issue. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/10/07/ai-assistant-enhances-digital-experience-at-uoa.html))

## Strategic Assessment

New Zealand academia is now in a **managed adoption phase**. The sector has accepted that AI is permanent, but it has not settled on a final operating model. The strongest areas of progress are **policy formation, student guidance, AI literacy, and flagship research capability**. The weakest areas remain **consistent implementation, wellbeing and equity frameworks, staff resourcing, and sector-wide assessment norms**. ([open-publishing.org](https://open-publishing.org/publications/index.php/APUB/article/download/2786/2572/10796))

The most distinctive feature of the New Zealand response is that AI adoption is being shaped not only by productivity and integrity concerns, but also by **te Tiriti obligations, Māori and Pacific data sovereignty, and public-interest questions about work, equity, and trust**. That gives Aotearoa a more values-led academic AI landscape than many peer systems, even if operational maturity remains uneven. ([royalsociety.org.nz](https://www.royalsociety.org.nz/assets/Guidelines-for-the-best-practice-use-of-GenAI-in-research_Royal-Society-Te-Aparangi_June-2025_English-.pdf))

## Conclusion

As of **9 March 2026**, AI in New Zealand academia is best understood as **broadly adopted, unevenly governed, and rapidly institutionalising**. Student use is mainstream; assessment redesign is accelerating; research governance is maturing; and leading institutions are building enduring capability through centres, policies, micro-credentials, enterprise tools, and new academic programmes. The next phase will be defined less by whether academia adopts AI, and more by whether it can do so in ways that are **credible, equitable, culturally grounded, and educationally defensible**. ([auckland.ac.nz](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/assets/staff/learning-teaching/Student_Feedback/Gen-AI_Survey_Highlights_Oct-2024.pdf))
